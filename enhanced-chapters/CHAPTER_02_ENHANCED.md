# CHAPTER TWO
## Echoes of Steel and Sand

There's a kind of lie a city tells with its architecture.

Utilta told it better than any city on Eloquence.

It rose white and luminous from desert sand—not a city so much as a country folded upright. Mile upon mile of pale towers and tiered districts, threaded through with iron rail-lines and broad stone highways, all of it catching sun and throwing it back as though light were a thing it owned.

Utilta didn't whisper its power. It announced it. Kept announcing past the edge of sight.

At the city's crown, set apart from the churn below, rose Solspire. Not merely a palace but a seat of rule unto itself—separate authority within the greater body, the way a heart is separate from the chest that keeps it. A single guarded road climbed the last distance to its gates. Every petitioner, every lord, every returning soldier walked it alone, leaving the city behind to come before the throne.

The country was called Quandrum.

But few said Quandrum anymore, and the word a man chose told you something true before he finished speaking.

Old houses—the ones who remembered when Utilta had simply *been* the country, before lesser cities pulled away and named themselves "of Quandrum" rather than live in Utilta's shadow—those houses still said Utilta and meant all of it.

Newer provinces said Quandrum. Meant: *we are not yours.*

Lord Mandu Dakshad said Utilta. Never once examined why.

---

## CHAPTER TWO · I
### Solspire, the Seat of Rule

*Past rails and roadways of the vast city, a single approach climbs to Solspire—the palace-seat crowning Utilta, separate authority unto itself.*

---

He walked marble halls with the unhurried stride of a man who'd earned the right. His cloak still carried faint grey ghost of southern ash.

The corridors breathed incense. Sweet, costly smoke meant to signify serenity—meaning, if you knew how to read it, money. Resins shipped from across the sea, burned in quantities that would've fed a southern village for a season.

Serenity, in Solspire, was a thing you could afford or couldn't.

He passed servants. Didn't look at them. Not-looking was the custom, and custom was the point.

Most wore plain grey. A few wore, in addition, a thin band of dull metal fitted close at the throat.

Nullification collar. Eltech keyed to still whatever gift its wearer had been born carrying.

Five hundred years since the Eliant Wars. The law still asked it of any Eliant who'd serve inside these walls.

The empire called this mercy. The bitter arithmetic of the age was that the empire wasn't wholly wrong. In Damascus they spent Eliants to death in mines. In Utilta, an Eliant might serve in the emperor's own house—collared, permitted, breathing.

Kinder cage.

Also why the conquered called Utilta *the benevolent oppressor*—phrase that was no compliment, no lie, and held the whole weather of the empire in four words.

Mandu had married an Eliant.

He set that thought where he set all such thoughts. Went in to see the most powerful man in the world.

---

**✦✦✦**

Attendants withdrew. Great doors sighed shut.

Emperor Fenice Rolt III let his shoulders drop a hand's width. Throne dissolved out of posture. Man settled back into it.

"Sit, Mandu, before you fall. You sway like a tent in east wind, and I've only just had this floor cleaned."

"I stand out of respect for the throne, Majesty."

"You stand out of respect for your *knees*, which I heard announce themselves from across the chamber."

Rolt gestured to the seat beside him. Not courtesy—quiet order of a man who'd once hauled this same general out of a flooded trench by collar and curses.

"Sit. The throne and I have an understanding. It pretends to be comfortable. I pretend to believe it."

Mandu sat. Something in his back spoke. He ignored it, as he ignored most things that couldn't be met with blade or plan.

Rolt poured for them both with his own hand—which no protocol allowed, exactly why he did it—and passed the cup across.

They'd been young together once. In places neither of them named anymore.

The throne had come between them the way a great inheritance comes between brothers. Not a wall, precisely. Just a thing always in the room, sitting in space that used to be theirs.

"You look tired, old friend," Rolt said. "Not the road. Something rode home with you."

"Something did." Mandu turned the cup in his hands. Didn't drink. "We met Valkhorra in the southern wastes. Not raiders, Fenice. A host. The Beastmen—their alchemists made of them a thing that doesn't break the way men break. I've soldiered thirty years. Haven't fought its like."

"I've had the reports." Warmth banked. Strategist came up beneath. "But Valkhorra doesn't cross the northern sea for plunder it could take on its own coast. They wanted something in our soil. Did you find what?"

"I found where they were digging."

Mandu chose his words with care. Told the wonder first—wonder buys a hearing.

He spoke of the ruin risen out of hills. Structure no map held, no record named, swallowed so deep in earth and years the world had forgotten it had ever been built. Impossible age. Frantic carvings. Procession of robed figures lifted in worship and terror, horned beasts driven beneath.

He spoke, at last, of the old northern tale carved into all of it.

"The Sleeping Gods," he said.

Rolt's expression didn't change. Which was itself a change.

"My grandmother frightened me to sleep with that one. Gods who didn't die but lay down to rest. World built quiet ever after so as not to wake them." He set down his cup. "Tale for children and drunk men, Mandu. The north has a hundred like it. I'd not have thought to hear it from a man who counts his own teeth before he believes he has a full set."

"I'd not have thought to repeat it." Mandu leaned forward. "Then I found what was sleeping."

---

He told it plainly.

The chamber. Dead machinery waking. Two children in pods. Light that was neither Orma nor Eltech nor anything he had a name for. Beastmen pouring in behind, hungry for the same prize.

"They crossed a sea and marched a thousand miles, Fenice, to take what now sleeps two days' ride from this room. That was the errand. Not our coast. Not our grain. *Those two children.*"

Long silence.

What moved behind Rolt's stillness, he couldn't have fully named. A man's blindness about his own heart is the truest thing you'll ever learn of him.

Wonder—the clean kind the throne hadn't yet eaten.

Fear. Reasonable fear. His whole reign stood on one buried stone: the last people to hold power past common measure had nearly cracked the world, had to be cracked first. An emperor found sheltering two unknown vessels of such power wouldn't stay emperor long.

And a third thing, nearest the bone, he'd have denied with hand on old fire:

*Appetite.*

Power knows its own. No man climbs to the roof of the world without learning to love the climbing.

"You don't believe it," Mandu said, watching him. "The prophecy."

"I believe two children fell into your hands and great many monsters wanted them. That's enough to act on without dragging my grandmother into the war room."

Rolt's eyes went to high windows. To the white city falling away beneath. To desert past it that had buried older empires than his.

"The Sleeping Gods are a story, Mandu. I'm near certain of it." He was quiet a beat too long. "It's the *near* that's kept me from sleep since you walked in. I don't enjoy it. I'd be obliged if you took it back south with you."

"I'd be obliged to be rid of it."

"Then we're both to be disappointed. Natural condition of men who serve a state."

Rolt almost smiled. Let it pass. Came to the matter waiting under all of it.

"Where are they now? This moment."

"My house. Resting. Safe."

"Your house." The emperor turned from the window. "Not the sanctioned halls. Not the Imperial physicians, who exist, last I was told, precisely so strange and dangerous things that wash up in my empire are examined by men *I've* vetted—rather than slipped quietly into the private home of a lord, however dear that lord is to me."

"My own physicians have seen them. Men I trust with my life. Have trusted."

"Men loyal to *you*, Mandu. That's rather the splinter in it."

"It's the whole of it." Mandu didn't raise his voice. Never needed to. "Fenice. You know what the sanctioned halls would've made of them. The moment those boys passed under that door they stop being children. Become a *finding*. Catalogued. Measured. Opened, if the measuring asked for it."

He paused.

"There are men in your sanctioned halls who'd slice a sleeping child to see what light he was made of, write it up before supper, call it service to the crown. I've buried enough sons not my own. I won't hand two living ones to a table."

He set the untouched cup aside.

"So no. I took them home. I'll answer for it. But I won't pretend I'd choose otherwise."

---

Different silence. Different grain.

Rolt looked at him long.

Somewhere under the emperor's regard moved a thing he wouldn't examine either.

The children were now in the house of Aelayla.

*Aelayla.*

Who'd been his once, before his house taught him what an emperor's heart was permitted to want and what it must surrender. Whom he'd let go for this throne, called it duty, married instead a woman he honored and didn't warm to.

The woman he'd given up was now raising, under another man's roof, the two most consequential children on the planet.

He felt the shape of it pass through him like draft from a door he kept shut.

Shut it again.

Said none of it.

"You disobeyed me before I'd given the order you were disobeying," he said at last. "Rare talent. Most men require the order first."

"Saves time."

"Saves *your* time. Costs me the pleasure of being asked."

But the steel had gone out of it.

He rose. Crossed to the window. Stood with back to his oldest friend, looking down on the city that was his to keep and his to be crushed beneath.

"You're right about the halls. That's what galls me—not that you went around me, but that you were right to. A man should be able to trust his own house, Mandu. I command an empire. Can't send two children into half the rooms of my own palace."

He paused.

"Sit with that a moment. I have to sit with it always."

"I know it."

"You know it. You don't carry it."

Rolt turned back.

"Keep them. They're yours to guard—on your head and your honor. You'll bring me the truth of them as it comes. To *me*. Not the council, not a clerk, not that nest of mouths that calls itself my chamber of advisors and would sell the contents of my chamber pot to Damascus if the price were right."

Weary humor crossed him. Gone.

"Now. How many men stood in that chamber when you pulled those children out?"

"My platoon. Two dozen, near enough. And the ones who carried them home."

"Two dozen." Rolt let the number sit like smoke. "Two dozen men, Mandu. Your good wife. Whatever physicians you let lay hands. That's not a secret. That's a rumor that hasn't yet remembered it is one."

"They're my men." Mandu said it like stating the sun had risen. "They followed me into that tomb when the dark was screaming. They'll hold their tongues because I tell them to. Because they were there—felt what was in that room. Not one of them eager to speak of it aloud."

"Men are loyal until they're drunk. Or frightened. Or in love. Or merely bored on a long night with a stranger who buys the next cup."

The emperor wasn't unkind in it. Simply ruled long enough to know what people were.

"Their loyalty I'll grant you. Their silence you will go command—plainly, tonight—so no man mistakes your trust for permission. Loyalty is what a man feels. Silence is an order. See they know the difference."

"It will be done. They won't breathe a word."

"Then guard the boys close. Guard the knowing of them closer."

Rolt's hand found Mandu's shoulder. Emperor to subject by the look of it. But it was the grip of the younger man in the trench.

They both felt the years in it.

"Go carefully, old friend. Carefully is how the few who walk out of this part of the tale manage to walk."

## CHAPTER TWO · II
### Behind Closed Doors

*The throne dissolved out of posture. The man settled back—two old soldiers, shared cup, thirty years between them.*

---

Mandu bowed. The bow was real. Given to the man, not the throne.

Lord Mandu Dakshad trusted Fenice Rolt with his life. Had. Would again.

It was the *throne* he didn't trust.

He'd seen what the throne asked of men who sat it. How it spent their love like coin when keeping required spending. He couldn't be wholly easy knowing friend and seat were now the same person.

He carried that out unspoken, the way he carried most things.

Doors closed.

The emperor sat alone with lantern and city and the very long memory of the grave he ruled.

Told himself the Sleeping Gods were a story.

Didn't let himself wonder whether he'd just become a figure in one.

---

**✦✦✦**

Bandar Sulph waited in the lower courtyard—broad shape against hard noon light.

Sight of him eased something in Mandu's chest. Bandar was a man with no locked rooms in him. What he felt, he wore.

"Well? Am I still employed, or did you finally insult him to his face?"

"He gave us the boys. To guard. Here, quiet. Not even the council."

"Praise the unwise gods for that much." Bandar said *council* the way a man names a rotten tooth he's been meaning to pull. "Half in Damascus's purse, other half in their own. Fewer mouths a secret passes, longer it lives to be a secret."

His grey eyes settled. Jest left them.

"But that's the emperor's mind. What's yours? You wear it like dust."

Mandu watched the city move below. Market rivers. Water-sellers. Long slow current of people who wouldn't in their lives climb so high as the step the two soldiers stood on.

"My mind," he said slowly, "is that we've agreed to put two children in a box and name the box protection. And protection is the true reason. And it's not the only reason. I'm too old not to know that the reasons a man won't say aloud are the ones that come back to collect."

He shook his head.

"They didn't fall into that ruin by accident, Bandar. Someone meant them to wake—to a chosen world, at a chosen hour, in chosen hands. The hands weren't ours. I'd like to know whose they were before those hands come asking."

"Then we hold the boys until we learn it. Close every mouth that was in that tomb—yours, mine, the men, the household, all of it."

Bandar uncrossed his arms.

"My Naska's been asking after them already. Children find each other quicker than we'd like."

"Rolt wants the men ordered to silence tonight. Plainly. I'll see to it before I sleep." Mandu's gaze stayed on the city below. "They'll hold. Not a man in that platoon I haven't bled beside. But the emperor's right to want it said—a thing a man assumes is a thing he eventually forgets to keep."

"They'll hold." Bandar said it with flat certainty, vouching for his own. "I'd stake my house on every one. We dragged those children out of screaming dark together. That kind of night seals a mouth better than any order."

Pause.

"But we'll give the order regardless. Twice-sealed is best sealed."

"Agreed. And we watch even the small ways it could slip—your Naska, my house, careless word in a barracks." Mandu heard the cold in it. Let it stand. Cold was the work. "Until we know what they are, this stays ours to carry. No clerks. No council. No strangers buying the next cup."

"Agreed."

Bandar studied him a moment. Then, quieter: "You're not worried about the boys. You're worried about what loving them will cost."

Mandu looked at him. Said nothing.

Bandar grunted. "Thought so. Welcome to fatherhood, old man. The fear doesn't get smaller. You just get better at carrying it."

Something in Mandu's chest loosened. "When did you become wise?"

"The day Naska was born and I realized I could kill a hundred men but couldn't stop the world from touching her." Bandar's smile was brief, sad. "You'll do fine. You're already scared. That's the first requirement."

---

*The second wall went up—raised by good men, for good reasons, out of love and caution and duty.*

*The thing about such walls: hands that build them to keep a wound out are so often the same hands that seal one in.*

Above them, Solspire's towers burned white and certain in noon. Beautiful. Sure of themselves. Built upon a grave.

The great city went about its enormous life, knowing nothing of the small room and small courtyard where its course had just been bent.

---

**✦✦✦**

The estate was dark and hushed when Mandu came home. Boys long asleep.

He looked in on them out of a habit he'd have denied having. Two shapes breathing slow in blue dark.

Felt the day's weight shift toward something he had no rank for.

He found Aelayla where he often found her when the house had gone quiet: smaller study, single lamp burning, book open under her hand that she didn't appear to be reading.

"They went down without a fight, for once," she said, not looking up. "Aesir argued. Then he didn't. I think he's learning some battles cost more than they pay."

"Lesson his father never mastered."

Mandu lowered himself into the chair across from her. Old wood took his weight with a complaint that matched his own.

"I saw Rolt."

Her hand went still on the page.

"He'll let us keep them. Guarded, quiet—not even the council." He rubbed his eyes. "Pressed me on the physicians. I told him the truth. He didn't like it. Trusted it anyway. Went as well as such things go."

"And you told him what they are."

"I told him what I know. Which is almost nothing."

He caught the thing in her voice. Slowed.

"Aelayla. What is it."

She closed the book.

When she looked at him, lamp put hard gold edge along her face.

"I think it was a mistake. Speaking of them to him at all. I've been turning it over since you left. It doesn't get smaller."

She paused. Choosing, as she always chose.

"There's something in those boys, Mandu, that reads—to the kind of eye that's trained to read it—as ours. As Eliant, or near enough the difference won't save them. I can't tell you how I know. I know the way I know weather."

She leaned forward.

"And the moment power begins to wonder about a child who looks like mine, the child stops being a child. You've handed Fenice Rolt a question. Questions, in that house, have a way of becoming policies."

"He's not like that with me. You know what he is to me—"

"I know what he is to *you*." Something flickered under it. Governed. "That's not the same as knowing what he *is*."

Mandu spread his hands. Gesture of a reasonable man being reasonable—which is so often the most maddening thing a man can be.

"Aelayla, I think you worry past the trouble. Rolt's an ally. The boys are extraordinary—let them be seen for it. And you—"

He smiled. Walked, smiling, off the edge he couldn't see.

"—you've done well enough by this world, haven't you? Look how you live. The chair, the name, this house. It hasn't been cruel to you."

The room changed temperature.

"Look how I live," she repeated.

Very softly.

The softness was the warning he was too tired to hear.

"I only mean—"

"I know what you mean. You mean the proof is in the parlor. You mean a woman in a fine study with a fine name has clearly been spared the worst of it, so her fear must be a luxury."

She set the book aside with care that was its own kind of violence.

"Let me tell you how I live, Mandu, since you've invited it."

---

"I live knowing the lower city snickers when my carriage passes. *The professor. The emperor's old pet. The Eliant who got above herself.*"

"I live being introduced at every gathering as a marvel—which is the word this empire uses for a person it's decided is an exception to her own kind, so it need not revise its opinion of the kind."

"I came from House Vasari. Old house. Learned house. A house that gave this world scholars when half of Utilta was still learning to read. My mother was turned from doors that would've opened for a Utiltan fishwife."

Her voice was precision itself. A blade laid flat.

"Wealth didn't buy us out of it. Wealth is not a door out. There *is* no door out. There's only the size of the room they let you stand in. I've spent my life making myself the precise dimensions required to be permitted."

She paused.

"And there is not one day—not *one*, Mandu—that I forget who I was before this house decided I was tolerable."

Mandu opened his mouth. "I didn't—"

"Didn't what." Not a question. "Didn't mean it? I'm sure you didn't. That's rather the whole of it. Your privilege isn't the cruel thing you say. It's the kind thing you say without ever once feeling the floor it stands on."

She grew more precise. Aelayla never shouted. She did something worse.

"You move through this world and it holds you up. Because it's always held you up, you mistake the holding for the ground. You think everyone's standing on ground."

She leaned forward.

"Some of us are standing on each other's backs, Mandu. Going back generations. Just to keep our chins above the water your sort calls *the way things are*."

---

He said nothing.

He'd learned, over eleven years, there were rooms in his wife he couldn't enter with wit. Wit was a tool of the comfortable. This wasn't a comfortable room.

So he did the harder thing. The thing the soldier in him had spent decades unlearning.

He sat in it.

Let himself be wrong.

---

Aelayla, seeing him stay—seeing him not defend, not retreat, not reach for soft excuse—felt the worst of the heat go out of her.

What was left underneath wasn't anger.

It was the thing the anger had been protecting.

"I want something different for them," she said, quieter now. "That's all this is, in the end. You want to make them strong. Good. Be the one who does that. You're better at it than any man alive."

She paused.

"But I know what they're walking toward in a way you cannot. Because I've walked it. My mother walked it. Hers. If I'm going to love these boys—and Mandu, I already do, past all sense, past all caution, more than is wise for a woman who's already lost—"

Her voice caught on the edge of something old. Didn't fall in.

"—then I won't love them blind. I will know the realities you're spared. I owe them that. It's the only inheritance I have that's worth more than this house."

---

She turned back to her cold lamp.

Mandu watched her.

Understood he'd married a woman with a country inside her he'd never bothered to learn the geography of.

Had called that country fortunate to its face.

What she didn't say—what she'd never said to him, turned over only in the privacy lamplight gave her—was the shape of how she knew what she knew about Rolt.

She didn't distrust the emperor's position from theory. She distrusted it from memory.

There'd been a time, before Mandu, before any of this, when Fenice Rolt had looked at her the way no Utiltan of his rank was supposed to look at an Eliant girl, however highborn.

He'd meant it. She'd never doubted that. Not then. Not now.

He'd loved her.

And he'd let her go. Because his family set their weight against it, and a Rolt doesn't, in the end, stand against the weight when the weight is the whole machinery of the thing he's to inherit.

He'd chosen the legal. The prudent. The good-of-the-house. Dressed the choosing in duty. Believed the dress.

That was the man. Not cruel. Never cruel.

Simply a man who would always, when pressure came and the cause could be called just, fold toward comfort of the many and keeping of his own.

She'd learned that about Fenice Rolt with her whole heart. Years ago. The lesson had cost her enough she'd never once had to relearn it.

She felt no bitterness anymore. Or told herself so—which is nearly the same, never quite.

She bore the man no grudge.

There was even, still, in some small disused room she kept locked and visited never, a quiet warmth toward him she wouldn't call by any name. Wouldn't feed.

It was nothing. Would stay nothing.

She had a husband she'd chosen with eyes open—more than the emperor could say of his own marriage—and she wouldn't insult that choice by tending a ghost.

But it was why she was the only soul in Solspire who spoke to Fenice Rolt without fear.

Not boldness. *Knowledge.*

She'd seen the man inside the emperor. Loved him. Survived losing him.

You can't be frightened of a throne once you've wept over the man who sits it.

So she spoke to him plainly. Always. Never cruelly and never crouched.

And he—starved as the powerful always are for a single voice that wants nothing from them—had come to prize it more than she knew.

They crossed paths rarely. He spent the grace of those rare meetings like a man rationing water in drought.

---

None of which she said.

She only sat in her study with her husband and her cold lamp, in a house built on a grave, raising two boys the world didn't yet know to be afraid of.

Let the silence be the apology Mandu didn't have words for and the forgiveness she didn't quite have heart to refuse.

"Teach me, then," Mandu said at last. Into the quiet. "The geography. I'll be a poor and aging student. But I'd rather learn it late than keep insulting it for free."

Aelayla looked at him long.

Corner of her mouth moved. Not quite a smile. Its near relation.

"That," she said, "is the first thing you've said all night I didn't have to forgive you for."

## CHAPTER TWO · V
### The Tomb Reopened

*A thing lost to maps for an age, opened again—two casings, one shattered, one empty, and chained things hauling stone in the dark.*

---

The tomb Mandu had sealed weeks before stood open again.

It had cost labor. Great deal of it.

That labor stood about him now in clinking chains: Beastmen, kin to the things that had died on his road south. Except these wore collars. Hauled stone. Ground their resentment into rain.

They hadn't chosen this any more than their southern brothers had chosen the alchemy that twisted them. Somewhere, long before any drew breath, a man had decided their bodies belonged to other men's purposes.

*Even the monsters of this tale are some earlier cruelty's harvest.*

*That's no excuse for what they became. It's only the truth—which is a different thing than an excuse, and a more useful one.*

---

The cloaked figure paid them no mind.

He moved through reopened dark with surety of a man who'd read of a place so often that reaching it felt like remembering. Hood low. Lower face lost beneath it. Cloak so deep-black it seemed to drink torchlight rather than answer it.

He came to the tomb's heart. Stopped before two stone casings.

One broken to gravel. The other simply empty—lid cast back, keeping already carried off.

He stood over the empty one long.

Lesser man might've read the emptiness as loss.

He read it as proof.

Something had been worth the building of this place and the sealing of it. Worth a tomb lost to maps for an age.

And a thing that valuable didn't cease to be valuable because another hand had reached it first.

He'd learn where it had gone. He'd learned the whereabouts of harder things.

He was turning to leave when the floor caught light wrong.

---

## CHAPTER TWO · VI
### Red Orma

*Not the gutter-drug that hollows men into wraiths—the rarer thing, named in the same breath as relics of the vanished ones.*

---

He knelt. Brushed rubble back.

There in broken stone, a crystal pulsed.

Not the sickly blue green of common Orma—gutter-drug that hollowed men into wraiths, poison the empire hanged men for touching while its own quiet corruption fattened on the trade.

This was deeper. Red so dark it neared black.

And it didn't merely catch torchlight. It *answered* it. Brightening as his hand drew near—as though it knew him, or hungered to.

"Red Orma," he breathed.

Beneath surprise sat something older and far worse: *recognition.*

He'd spent years and a fortune and other men's lives chasing the rumor of precisely this.

Not the cheap fire that fed fighting pits and smugglers' little kingdoms.

The rarer thing. The thing the oldest texts named in the same breath as relics of the vanished ones.

He turned it in his fingers. Watched it drink flame.

Didn't smile. Men who smile at such moments haven't yet understood their weight.

He'd understood for a long time.

---

He believed—and here was the thing that made him dangerous and the thing that made him comprehensible, which were one thing—that the powerful of the world sat hoarding what was never theirs to hoard. Fat and frightened atop secrets that belonged to all.

And a man with spine to take such things back was no thief but the corrector of an ancient theft.

He had reasons. Always had reasons.

*The most dangerous men of any age are the ones who've made the most thorough peace with their own.*

"I'll carry this back to the capital," he said—half to himself, half to the rising storm beyond the tomb's mouth.

And in those few words, a thread drew taut between this southern grave and the white towers far to the north.

The rot didn't only lay siege to Utilta from without.

It had a road in.

Had always had one.

---

He slid the red crystal into the pouch at his belt. Waved chained things back to their digging.

Walked out into rain. Into dark. Into the long first hour of a hunger the whole world would one day learn the name of.

Behind him, thunder turned over. Beastmen toiled. Rain came down on the grave of an age.

Washing nothing clean.

*Some things don't wash clean.*

*And this story, from its very first hour, had never once been about the clean.*
