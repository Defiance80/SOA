# CHAPTER TEN
## Winds of the South

The way out of Utilta was a hole in the floor of a flooded cellar. And it was for sale.

Aesir went down to it on a night in the wet end of winter. Through a coal chute and a dead bakery and four doors that each cost a coin.

The last stair ended in black water. He stood in it to the knees, in a room whose ceiling was somebody's floor. That floor somebody else's. And so on up through a thousand years of building until you came out at last on a street with a name on it.

The cold went through his boots and set its teeth in the bones of his feet.

Above him, at the top of all that stacked and inherited masonry, eleven million people slept. Certain of their walls.

---

The old man's lamp made one yellow coin of light on the water.

"There," Sarn said, and nudged the toe of his boot against something Aesir could not see and had to feel for.

An iron grating. Sunk flush in the floor of the world. Its bars worn thin as finger bones by water that had been passing through them since before the Rolts were a name anybody said aloud.

"That's your door, young sir. Since you've been three months and a great deal of other men's money asking after one."

Aesir crouched. Put his hand on it. Could feel the pull of the water going down.

"Where does it come out?"

"Conduit runs two miles and a half. You'll swim some of it. Then a culvert under the footing of the wall itself, which is the bad part. I'll not lie to you about the bad part, because men who lie about the bad part get people killed and then have to drink with themselves after."

Sarn spat, delicately, into the dark.

"And then you come up in a drainage field on the far side, in a stand of poplars. And there's the world."

---

*There's the world.*

The old man said it the way another man might say *and there's the coal shed.*

Aesir found his own heart going hard against his ribs. Which annoyed him.

"The price."

Sarn named it. It was a great deal of money. Aesir had it, sewn along the inside of his coat where the seams took the weight. Had had it for a month. Had come down here tonight fully intending to spend it.

He reached for the coat.

"Payable to me," Sarn said, "who keeps a tally and hands it up. Same as everything down here."

"Up to whom?"

The old man looked at him. The lamp guttered and the coin of light broke and reformed on the water between them.

"*Up,*" he said.

---

Aesir stood there in the cold black water with his hand inside his coat. Did the arithmetic he'd spent a year teaching himself to do.

Got the answer.

"You're telling me," he said slowly, "that the only road out of this city belongs to the same man the only road in belongs to."

"I'm telling you I keep a grating."

Sarn's voice had gone flat and careful. His eyes had gone, very briefly, to the collar of his own coat. And away again.

Aesir had seen that gesture before. Had seen it on the loveliest and coldest woman in the underworld, in a dead warehouse, checking for a stone that swallows sound.

"I'm telling you that in thirty years I've never once known a soul to go down that hole who wasn't expected, polite and smiling, in the poplars."

"Then why keep it open?"

"Because a door that nobody can open isn't a door." The old man lifted his lamp and turned to go. The light swung and the drowned room turned with it. "It's a wall. And there's no money in a wall, young sir. The money's in the door."

---

He came up into the alley an hour later with the coat still heavy and the seams still sewn. Stood shivering. Looked at the actual wall two streets away, pale and enormous and utterly beside the point.

His whole life he'd been told the wall kept him in.

He'd spent a winter proving the wall could barely be bothered to.

What kept eleven million people in was the registry that fed you and the work that placed you and the neighbor who noticed. Under all of it the rumor of ash on the other side of the world.

And now, at the bottom of it, this: that every gap in the old stone had a keeper, and every keeper had a price, and every price ran up and up until it arrived at a silence with no face which would be delighted to sell him a road.

And would be waiting at the end of it.

Utilta was not a locked room.

It was a baited one.

## CHAPTER TEN · I
### The Door and the Wall

*The only way out of the sealed city, sunk flush in the floor of a drowned room, kept by an old man with a lamp who has never once known a soul to go down it who was not expected, polite and smiling, on the far side.*

---

He tried eight more seams before the spring. The seams gave him eight versions of the same answer in eight different accents.

Somewhere in the middle of them he turned seventeen.

Nobody marked it. Aelayla put a second candle on the supper table and said nothing at all about it. Let him decide whether he'd noticed.

Aesir looked at the small flame for a while. Decided he had.

That was the whole of it.

---

The rest of the year lived in his body rather than his memory.

Wet boots.

Lysse's green door in the Ninth, which had never once opened to him again.

A cache burning near the eastern gate with the smoke lying flat under a low sky.

The particular ache in the ribs where the knife had gone in, which had closed faster than it had any right to and now hurt only when the weather turned.

---

And the story.

He heard it in a Ninth tavern in the spring. Told to a table by a man three drinks past caution who did not know whose face was in the corner.

The burning boy. Who was eight feet tall. Who had killed eleven men in a warehouse. Who wept light and could not be cut.

The table laughed.

One of them did not. Touched a charm at his throat. Changed the subject.

Aesir finished his drink and paid and walked home in the small hours through streets that had nodded him past for two years and now went quiet as he came.

He had never in his life been so alone in a city of eleven million people.

Fear he could have carried.

It was the reverence he could not stand.

The low city had done the one thing it knew how to do with a thing it could not explain, which was to make it a story and then keep well clear of the story.

---

Askan held. It cost him. He paid it where nobody could see.

Once a month he sat at his father's table and answered General Bandar's easy questions about the cadets with a face Aesir was never permitted to look at.

Never once mentioned it.

But he grew quieter through that winter. More exact. Coming up out of a bad cellar one night with blood drying on the shaft of his glaive he said, to nobody, in the flat voice of a man checking a figure:

"You had better be worth it."

Did not say to whom.

---

Naska stood at the fire's edge all year and let Aesir circle it.

Neither of them stepped in.

There was already too much weight on the rope and they both knew it without saying so. In the way they knew everything without saying so.

But the not saying had lost its lightness.

On the watches they shared she sat closer than the cold required.

One night on the broken tower, with the roofs falling away black beneath them and the charcoal smudge of the forest lying along the bottom of the southern sky, she said:

"When you finally go, I'm going."

Not as an offer. Not as a question. As a fact she was tired of carrying by herself.

He did not argue. Had been carrying the same fact for months. Had not once found the courage to set it down where she could see it.

---

And Raijeen read.

Not as he had read when he was small, for the plain pleasure of knowing a thing.

He read now the way a man checks a rope.

*Ask me when I've read more,* he said in the autumn.

*Ask me when I'm certain,* in the winter.

By spring he'd stopped saying anything at all. Had taken to sitting with their mother in the high library behind a door that was, more and more often, shut.

Whatever the two of them kept behind it changed the temperature of the supper table. Nobody named that either.

Aesir did not push. He'd learned, at some cost, what it does to a person to be pushed at for a truth before they can carry it.

He owed his brother a patience nobody had ever owed him.

---

So the winter went. And the spring.

The papers went soft at the folds from handling. The answer did not change.

Every road out of Utilta ran down through the underworld. Every road in the underworld ran up to Veskar.

Every road but the one that wore a uniform.

---

Soldiers left. Soldiers had always left.

It was the single exception the War of the Black Queen had written into the sealing of the gates. Because even a city that has decided the world outside is finished still needs men to go and stand at the edge of the finishing.

The columns went out under the empire's seal and came back and told nobody anything.

That was the whole of Utilta's window on creation.

Which meant the thing Aesir had spent a year refusing to do had become, by the plain arithmetic he hated most in the world, the only move left on the board.

He was going to have to carry the truest thing he'd ever found up out of the dark and hand it to the power he trusted least. Ask it, politely, to open the door.

His mother had told him once that the world charges you for doing the right thing as though you'd broken something.

She'd never told him what it charges you for asking permission.

---

**✦✦✦**

He chose the hour with a strategist's care.

Then, being Aesir, abandoned the plan the moment the door opened.

He'd meant to catch Mandu in the study after supper. Papers laid out in good light. The case built as Askan would have built it, foundations first.

What happened instead was that his father came home two days early from the eastern garrisons. Dust still on him. Crossed the yard toward the house with the particular heaviness he brought back from the palace and nowhere else.

Aesir looked at him and heard himself say, across twenty feet of evening air:

"I need to show you something. You're going to want to sit down for it. You're going to want to shout at me first. I'd take it as a kindness if we could do those in the other order."

---

Mandu stopped. Looked at his son a long moment in the failing light. The way he inspected a wall he expected to find a crack in.

Something in him seemed to settle and brace at once.

"The study," he said. "Bring whatever it is."

He was already walking.

"And Aesir. I never shout. I have never once in my life needed to."

Which was true. And was worse.

---

Aesir laid it all out on the great desk. Did it the way he'd rehearsed with Raijeen. In order. Without heat.

The year in the low city, trimmed of the parts that would stop his father's heart.

The trade.

The children, from the first year, whom Aelayla had quietly taken in and documented and rehomed. About whom Mandu already knew.

Then the new things.

The three kings by name. The fourth name, and the shape of the silence around it.

And last, the papers. Unfolded flat under the lamp. The shafts, the shoring, the spoil. The surveyor at four times a soldier's wage. And the one word, *Orma,* entered in a small hand as a yield.

---

He'd expected questions. Had braced for fury.

What he had not braced for was what actually happened.

His father went perfectly still over the papers. Then, without a word, reached out and touched one line at the bottom of the second page with a single finger. Left the finger there.

Aesir looked.

It was not a line he'd thought anything of at all.

It was the mark pressed into the corner of the requisition sheet. A founder's stamp.

"Where," Mandu said, and his voice had gone somewhere Aesir had never heard it go, "did you get this paper."

"Off a dead man. A year ago. Father, what is it?"

---

Mandu did not answer at once. Crossed to the window and stood with his back to the room.

Aesir watched the shape of him in the dark glass. A soldier of thirty years reading a thing over and over as though repetition would change it.

"Three years ago," Mandu said finally, "there was a fire in the dead dockyards. Cages. People in them. Crates left behind in the ash. The watch report crossed my desk because nothing like it should have been possible."

He paused.

"Near the bottom of that report was one line the scribe who wrote it did not understand. The crates bore founder's marks the quartermaster's office could not place. Not forged. Not smuggler's scratch. Clean maker's marks from no foundry inside these walls."

He turned around.

"I have carried that line for three years, Aesir. Taken it to the palace twice. Was told, twice, by men above my patience but not above my suspicion, that the matter was examined and closed."

He came back to the desk. Set his scarred hand flat beside the requisition sheet. Beside the stamp.

"This is that mark. Your paper and my report are the same paper. The thing I have been forbidden to chase inside the walls, you have just handed me an address for, outside them."

---

He looked up at his son.

For one unguarded moment the general was gone from his face entirely. What was left was older and simpler.

A man who'd lost a brother down the throat of this exact darkness, thirty years ago. Had been standing at the top of it ever since with a lamp.

"You should have come to me a year ago," he said. "And a year ago I would have locked you in this house. You knew it. Which is why you didn't. We will both have to live with which of us that shames more."

He gathered the papers with a decision in his hands.

"No one touches this but me. Not the garrison offices, not the quartermasters, none of the channels that closed my report. There is exactly one room in this empire where a thing like this can be said out loud safely. I have bled beside the man who sits in it."

"And if the palace is where your report went to die?"

Mandu paused at the door. Did not turn around.

"Then we are all a great deal further down the well than I have let myself believe," he said. "And I would rather learn that looking my Emperor in the eye than learn it from a wall falling on my sons."

---

**✦✦✦**

The highest room in Solspire had not changed in the seven years since Mandu had last stood in it with a secret this size.

The man in it had.

Fenice Rolt the Third had grown into his weight the way stone grows into a riverbed. Worn to fit by the thing that ran over him daily.

There was more grey in him than the years alone explained.

The letters between the two old soldiers had thinned in recent seasons. Warm and getting less so. Each of them privately blaming the office and neither of them blaming the man.

When the attendants withdrew and the great doors sighed shut, there was a moment where the two of them simply looked at one another across the distance the years had opened.

Measured it. Grieved it. Neither of them said so.

---

"You come unannounced," Rolt said, "with Bandar at your shoulder and a satchel under your arm, at the end of the day's audiences. In thirty years you have done that twice. Both times I ended the week older."

He poured with his own hand. Three cups. No protocol allowing it.

"Say the worst of it first. I've lost my taste for stairs."

---

Mandu laid the papers on the emperor's table.

Said the worst of it first.

"There is a foreign work camp on Quandrum soil. Three years established. Two days' hard ride past the southern wall, in the great forest. Sunk deep and supplied quietly. The supply runs through our own underworld, paid through our own criminals, under a name our own reports refuse to write down."

He paused.

"They are mining, Fenice. On our ground, under our law, inside a country the world believes is sealed. Whatever they are lifting out of that ground, the Orma they ship north through my city is only the spoil of it."

---

What followed was the longest silence Mandu had ever kept in that room. And he'd kept some long ones.

Rolt read every page. Read the way he'd read field orders as a younger man. Twice through. The second time for what was missing.

When he came to the founder's stamp his face did the thing that Mandu had crossed the city hoping against hope it would not do.

It did not look surprised.

---

"You knew," Mandu said.

It came out of him quiet and even. Landed in the room like a dropped blade.

"I knew there was a hole."

Rolt set the pages down with great care. Rose. Went to the tall windows where the whole bright bowl of his city lay smoking gently in the dusk.

"I have known there was a hole in the world for three years, Mandu. Since a report about impossible crates crossed a desk two floors below me. The men whose office it is to know such things came to me and told me it was nothing. I looked in their faces and saw that it was not nothing."

He paused.

"I made the emperor's choice. Do you know what the emperor's choice is? It is the one they do not put on the coins. A sealed city is not sealed by stone. It is sealed by belief. Eleven million people consent to these walls every morning because they believe the walls are absolute. The day that belief cracks, it will not crack politely."

He turned from the window.

"If I had stood up three years ago and said to my council, *something crosses our wall at will and I cannot find it,* I would have started a fire in this city that no truth could put out. So I closed the report. Set quiet men to watch, who found nothing, or were paid to find nothing. I have carried the hole around inside me since, the way you carry yours."

He looked at Mandu steadily.

"And now you put a map of the far side of it on my table."

---

"Then act on the map."

Bandar had held his peace to this point. A mountain by the door. When he spoke it was with the bluntness that had always been his office between these two.

"Quietly, if quiet is the price. A survey column under seal. Soldiers' business, soldiers' silence. The city has watched columns march south its whole life and never once asked why. Give me forty picked men and your writ. I will bring you back the truth of that forest, whatever it costs."

---

"And there it is."

Rolt's tired mouth found a quarter of a smile with no light in it.

"The entire art of ruling, gentlemen, in one sentence: finding out what a thing costs after you have already agreed to pay it."

He stood a moment with his hands on the back of his chair. An old soldier and an old throne wrestling for one body.

Mandu watched the man he'd bled beside win, this once, by a hand's width.

---

"A survey column," Rolt said. "Under my private seal, answerable to me alone, provisioned off the palace books. It observes, it maps, it counts. It does not engage unless engaged. I need the shape of this thing before I need its blood."

He paused.

"Bandar commands. Mandu."

Here the emperor paused. The pause had all the weight of the seven cooled years in it.

"Mandu does not go."

"Fenice."

"*No.*"

It was not the throne that said it. That was the strange thing. The thing Mandu would turn over on all the long rides afterward.

It was the man.

---

"You are the single most watched lord in my city. Clean coats I do not command have sat at your table twice this season, counting the hours of your household. Do not think I have failed to notice. Do not ask me yet whose coats they are, because I am still pulling that thread and it goes higher than I like."

He paused.

"If Mandu Dakshad rides south under arms, every eye in Solspire follows you out the gate. Whatever is in that forest hears of it a week before you arrive. You will serve this best the way I have had to serve everything for twenty years."

The bitterness in it was old and banked and entirely without self pity.

"By standing in a bright room, being seen, and lying with your posture."

---

It was, Mandu knew, correct.

Correct in the exact way that the worst orders of his life had all been correct.

"There is one more thing in the satchel," he said at last. Because he'd sworn to himself on the ride over that he would not leave the room with it unsaid. "The source of these papers. My son found them. My son, and Bandar's two, pulled this thread out of the dark of this city over two years while every office of the empire looked away. They are the only living souls on our side of the wall who know the trade, the players, and the road."

He paused.

"I am not asking you to enlist children, Fenice. I am telling you, as the man who trained them, that if that column marches without what is in their heads, you are sending forty men into a forest blind."

---

Rolt looked at him for a long moment. Something moved far back behind the tired eyes that might, in a younger man, have been the beginning of a smile.

"The boy from the tomb," he said quietly. "Seven years I have been asking you in letters how the boys are coming along. Seven years you have answered me in weather. And now here you stand telling me the elder one outworked my whole intelligence service before his beard came in."

He gathered the papers. Squared them. Set his palm on them. His seal ring clicked once against the table like a lock going over.

"Briefing officers, then. Attached, not enlisted. They ride at the rear. They map and they advise. At the first drawn blade they are Bandar's own charge, sworn on his House."

He glanced at Bandar.

"I am told your daughter shoots."

"Better than anyone in my command," said Bandar, with a father's flat treason against his own modesty.

"Of course she does. The gods forbid anything about this generation arrive in moderation."

---

Rolt turned back to the window. To the smoking dusk gold bowl of the last certain city in the world.

For a moment his voice came out of him older than the throne itself.

"Seven years ago you sat in that chair and told me the age we were born into might be ending. I called it soldier's weather. Do you remember what I actually feared, old friend? I feared that you were right. That it would end on my watch, quietly, in some report I chose to close."

The seal came down on the orders. Once, twice, three times. Wax and iron and the whole weight of the last empire behind it.

"The column musters in six days. Go home, Mandu. Lie with your posture."

## CHAPTER TEN · II
### The Emperor's Choice

*Three old soldiers and one throne in the highest room of Solspire: the map of the hole in the world laid flat on the table, and the seal coming down like a lock going over.*

---

**✦✦✦**

The column went out through the Soldiers' Gate at the grey end of night, six days later.

The city did exactly what Rolt had said it would do. Which was nothing at all.

Aesir had imagined the leaving his whole life, he realized, without ever once imagining it correctly.

He'd pictured drama. A chase, a bribe, a squeeze through some weeping seam in the old stone with the watch's lanterns swinging behind him.

What the empire gave him instead was paperwork and a grey cloak and a place in the fourth rank of a supply train. A gate that opened for a stamped writ exactly the way a door opens for a key.

Without wonder. Without ceremony. Without the world so much as clearing its throat.

---

Forty men. Six wagons. Four attached briefing officers with their hoods up.

The oldest threshold on Eloquence went by under his boots like a line of ordinary shadow.

That was the thing he had not braced for.

The sheer ordinariness of the impossible, once power decides to permit it.

And then the wall was behind them.

---

He'd promised himself he would not look back. Kept the promise for the better part of a mile.

Then he broke it.

Because everyone breaks it. Because there has never in the history of walls been a soul who left one without turning.

Utilta stood against the paling sky the way it had stood his whole life at the bottom of every street. Vast, pale, certain. Its high quarter catching the first sun and burning gold above the shadowed sprawl.

From outside, it looked exactly as it looked from the broken tower.

That was the devastation of it.

He'd expected the world to change when he crossed. Some seam in the air. Some toll taken.

Nothing had changed at all.

The road ran on south perfectly indifferent. The sky came down to a horizon that no lesson had ever drawn for him. The city of eleven million people shrank behind them into something a thumb could cover.

The world did not care. Had never cared. Had been out here the whole time not caring.

Enormous and unfinished and real.

Every ceiling he'd ever been given had been called the sky.

Here was the sky. It fit no room.

---

Beside him, Naska rode with her hood back. Face turned south. Her eyes were too bright.

He knew better than to look at her directly.

Behind them Askan rode with his eyes on the wagon intervals. As though geometry could hold a world together. But even he kept glancing up, at the size of everything, with an expression Aesir had never seen on him.

A strategist meeting a board with no edges.

---

"All my mother's people came from out here," Naska said at last. To the horizon. To no one. "Somewhere. Out past all of it. And this is the first time anyone of my blood has stood outside those walls in three generations. I'm doing it in an empire's cloak, on an empire's writ."

She laughed. One breath. At nothing that was funny.

"She'd have something to say about that. She always had something to say."

---

He did not answer. Some things are not for answering.

But he moved his horse half a pace closer, so that their stirrups nearly touched.

She did not move hers away.

They rode south like that a long while, into the wind coming up off the far green dark.

Two people from a sealed room learning the size of the actual sky.

They smelled the forest a full day before they reached it.

---

## CHAPTER TEN · III
### The Underside of the Sky, Corrected

*The gate opening for a stamped writ the way a door opens for a key: the sealed city shrinking to a thing a thumb could cover, and the sky, met at last, fitting no room.*

---

**✦✦✦**

The forest of the south did not begin.

That was the first wrongness. Every man in the column felt it before any of them said it.

Forests are supposed to begin. Thinning outward into scrub and outriders of lone trees.

This one stood up off the plain like a verdict.

A wall of green black older than any argument.

The column's noise fell away as they came under its eaves the way voices fall in a great hall. Unbidden. By instinct. Out of a respect nobody had ordered.

---

They left the wagons and the road on the second morning and went in on foot. By compass and by the captured papers.

The deep forest closed over them and began, quietly, to disassemble everything they knew.

Light came down green and swimming, from nowhere.

Distance stopped keeping its promises. A ridge that stood an hour off arrived in ten minutes. A stream they could hear plainly took half a day to find.

The trees were not trees as Utilta's parks had taught the word.

They were towers. Buttressed like architecture. Their crowns lost in their own weather.

Between them the silence was not empty.

It was tenanted.

Every man felt watched. Said nothing. Watched.

---

Askan hated it with a whole and honest hatred that he wore openly.

Because a strategist with no sightlines is a man being robbed.

Naska went the other way. The forest fit her like a key going home. Within a day she was reading it, moving through it, teaching the vanguard which silences were weather and which were wrong.

Bandar, watching his daughter flow through country no Sulph had walked in three generations, wore his flat command face over something that was not flat at all.

---

On the third evening, she and Aesir stood a listening watch together half a mile off the camp. In a green dusk going blue.

Somewhere far above them a wind was moving through the crowns that never once came down to where they stood.

It happened, nearly.

He knew exactly where she was without looking. Which had been true for three years and had never once been convenient.

She'd turned. The argument they'd been having wordlessly since they were children came nearer to being lost, or won, than it had ever come.

And then the wind found its way down at last, out of the south.

It carried a taste of cold iron and opened earth that belonged to no living forest.

They both knew it in the same instant. The moment folded itself away unspent.

*Not now* went between them, in the old fluent silence.

But the silence had changed its word. It no longer said *not ever.*

It said *soon.*

---

"Smell it?" she breathed.

"Iron. And spoil."

He turned his face into the wind. South. Toward the taste of the wound in the ground.

"Winds of the south. We're close."

---

They found it on the fifth day. It was worse than the papers.

The papers had let them picture a camp. Rough men, rough works. A smugglers' operation grown fat.

What lay below the ridge where the scouts finally bellied down was an industry.

A wound had been opened in the forest floor half a mile across. Terraced, shored, drained by pumps whose slow iron heartbeat they'd been hearing for a day without knowing it.

Out of its center descended the shafts. Timber collared. Lamplit. Exhaling their own faint weather into the evening.

Spoil stood in grey mountains.

An overhead line of buckets ran on cables. Endlessly. With the patience of a thing that has run for years.

And around it all, in towers and pickets and walking pairs, were the guards.

---

They were not Draven's kind.

Aesir knew it in the first ten seconds. Felt the knowledge go through the scouts around him like cold water.

Underworld muscle stands like property.

These men stood like doctrine.

They walked their rounds off rhythm so no watcher could time them. Carried long arms of a pattern no Utiltan armory had ever issued. Wore, every one of them, the same grey green half masked kit with no device on it anywhere. No banner over the works. No colors. No name.

A whole army in uniform declining to say whose.

---

Beside him, Bandar was very still for a very long time.

"Soldiers," the general said at last, softly, and the word had a weight Aesir had never heard him give it. "Not hirelings. Not renegades. Somebody's soldiers, sworn and drilled and paid, dug in on Quandrum ground in disciplined strength."

He let out a slow breath through his nose.

"Son, I have spent forty years learning exactly one thing about the world. It is that men do not stand like that for money. They stand like that for a country. And I do not know whose country I am looking at."

---

He began, quietly, to give the orders that would map it. Ranges, counts, rotations. The geometry of a thing too big to fight.

It was in the middle of that murmured arithmetic, with the light dying and the pumps beating and the survey doing at last exactly what its emperor had sealed it to do, that the night went wrong.

---

It went wrong in the smallest possible way. Which is the way nights go wrong.

A scout on the far left, easing back from the ridgeline, put his boot through the crust of an old test pit that four days of genius could not have found in the dark.

The sound it made was not loud.

It did not need to be loud.

The nearest picket stopped mid stride. Turned, unhurried. Looked up the black slope with the flat professional patience of a man whose whole trade is nights like this one.

Raised something to his mouth.

All across the great wound in the forest, one lamp at a time, with no shouting and no alarm and a discipline more frightening than any noise, the dig began to close like a fist.

---

"Back," Bandar breathed. "All of us. Now."

They fell back fast and low through the buttressed dark. The whole survey folding north by the drilled route.

They were quick. They were good.

It did not matter.

The forest had spent five days holding still and was not holding still for them. The night was full of moving iron sounds that came from more directions than the dig alone could account for.

Somewhere behind Aesir a man screamed, short, and was silent.

The retreat became the other thing.

---

What Aesir would remember afterward was not the fighting.

The fighting was brief and blind and ugly. Grey green shapes coming out of the dark in pairs, exactly as drilled, herding rather than killing.

He would remember instead the moment he understood the shape of it.

The pickets were not driving the survey away.

They were driving it somewhere.

---

The trees opened without warning onto a clearing he had not chosen.

The rest of them were being pressed into it around him. Naska with an arrow nocked and nothing worth loosing at. Askan bleeding above the eye and counting enemies with his lips moving. Bandar roaring the perimeter into a square.

A dozen soldiers of the survey formed up back to back in a green well of darkness with the iron sounds closing from every side at once.

And then, all together, stopping.

---

Silence.

The pumps, far off, beating.

And a fire sprang up.

Not near them. Across the clearing. A torch touching a prepared cairn of wood that had been waiting, Aesir understood with the coldest feeling of his life, since before the survey ever left the city.

The flames climbed and threw the clearing into moving amber.

Out of the dark at its edge, walking without hurry, without weapons, without the smallest doubt as to how the evening would proceed, came a man.

His clothes were very clean.

---

He crossed the firelight the way he'd once crossed a burning warehouse. Calm and connected and unafraid.

He stopped at a conversational distance from forty drawn blades as though they were furniture.

Looked, not at Bandar, not at the soldiers, but past all of them.

Directly at Aesir.

With the warm and terrible satisfaction of a man arriving at a page he marked long ago.

"There you are," he said. "I did tell you I would have time to prepare."

---

And at the treeline behind him, far outside the fire's reach, where no one but a boy whose eyes worked half a beat ahead of the world could have caught it, something else moved.

Not grey green. Not his.

Shapes at the forest's black edge. Patient and separate and uninvited. Standing just outside the reach of another man's fire the way creditors stand at the back of a room.

---

Two ledgers, Aesir thought, with the fire climbing and his own light rising unbidden under his skin like a tide answering a moon he had never chosen.

Two ledgers, opened on the same child.

Both of them had turned south.

And both of them were here.
