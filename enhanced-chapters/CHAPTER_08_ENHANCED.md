# CHAPTER EIGHT
## The Underside of the Sky

### PROLOGUE

Some years come down like a door kicked off its hinges. You know them by the splinters. Ever after you can name the very hour they began.

The year that took Aesir up out of his boyhood and set his feet at last toward the wall was not one of those.

It came the way rust comes to iron left out in the weather. Without a sound. Without a single morning that showed its hand.

Only at the end of it could a person stand back and see that the boy who walked in had been eaten quietly hollow. Something harder now stood in his place and wore his face like a borrowed coat it had slowly grown to fit.

---

It happened where the heaviest years tend to happen. Down in the dark and out of the light. Underneath a city that had spent a thousand winters talking itself into the belief that it was the last warm room in a house long since surrendered to the cold.

Utilta slept above it all. Sure of its walls the way a sleeper is sure of the floor beneath the bed. Never once thought to set a bare foot down and test it.

For the wall around the great sealed city was, if you looked at it straight and did not flinch, less a work of stone than a work of agreement.

A long unspoken promise passed down the generations from open palm to open palm, like a candle carried the length of a drafty hall.

Its whole flame was this: never to ask what lay past the final street. Never to go out to the edge and lean into the black to look.

---

Aesir had cupped that flame too, once. The way a small child holds it for a settled fact that the ceiling of the room he was born in is the underside of the sky.

He held it no longer.

One night in the deep of the under city a thing had woken in his own two hands. Cold and blue and far older than the city that would have burned him for the holding of it.

A wall, he had begun to understand, stays the edge of the world for exactly as long as every soul behind it agrees not to lay a palm to the cold stone and feel along the dark for the seam.

He had stopped agreeing.

What follows is the account of the season in which he learned what that refusal costs, paid out to him a coin at a time, with no change ever counted back into his hand.

It began, as these things so often do, with a knock at a door in the dark.

---

**✦✦✦**

For four days after the deep city, Aesir did not go out. Did not sleep past the thin grey minimum a body will take before it seizes sleep by force.

Could not look down at his own two hands without half expecting them to answer him.

The thing he had done beneath the Waterworks sat banked and low inside him the whole while. An ember buried under ash that throws no light of its own but warms the whole room around it.

He kept waiting for it to catch.

---

So he kept the hands busy.

Cleaned blades that didn't need cleaning. Ran the estate wall in the blue hour before the household stirred, lap after lap, as though a boy might outrun a thing by wearing out the legs that carried it.

None of it held.

What he fled lived inside the fleeing.

---

And on the fourth night he did the single thing he'd been avoiding above all the rest, harder than any wall and worse than any hunter:

He went and knocked at Aelayla's door and asked her, plainly, for the truth.

---

She did not look surprised.

That was the first blow. It landed before a word had passed between them.

She took in the boy standing in her doorway at that hour with that face, and something in her went quiet and sad and unsurprised. The way a woman looks at a letter she's been expecting for years and dreading the whole time.

"I wondered," she said, "which night it would be."

She stepped back to let him in.

"Not here. Come where the walls are thick."

---

In the small inner room, with the door shut and a single lamp burning, all the careful approaches he'd rehearsed out on the wall deserted him at once.

The way rehearsed words always desert you the moment the face you meant them for is actually in front of you.

What came out instead was the bare child's version. Which was the true one.

"It came out of me. In the deep city, under the Waterworks. There were men, and they had Naska, and something came out of my hands. Light. Cold, and blue, and I could not stop it. I did not know it was in me. It should not be possible."

His voice thinned to a wire.

"And you are not surprised."

---

For a long moment she did not answer.

She crossed to the old iron press. The one he'd never once seen her open. Rested a hand flat upon its lid. Left it closed.

When she spoke, her voice had the level evenness she kept for the hardest history.

"No. I am not surprised. I have known it was in you since you were small enough to carry in the one arm. You had a fever once, when you were still small enough to be frightened of the dark on your own account, while Mandu was away at the border."

She paused.

"Your whole body lit like a coal when the ash is blown off it. I sat with you until the dawn and banked it down with my hands and my breath the way you smother a fire you cannot let the neighbors see. In the morning you remembered a strange dream and nothing more."

She looked at him steadily.

"I have watched for it every day since. Every discipline I ever set you. Every game. Every lesson you believed was about patience, or breath, or stillness. Was the same single lesson wearing a different coat each time: how to keep the fire down."

She paused.

"And I told myself, year upon year, that if I only taught you well enough, the night I am standing in now would never arrive."

---

"What am I?"

And here she did the bravest thing he would ever watch her do, which was to tell him the exact shape of the edge of her own knowing.

"I do not know."

She held his eyes so he'd understand it was not a dodge.

"Hear me, because I will only be this naked with you the once. I am a keeper of my people's memory. My people's memory is long, and deep. It has no shelf anywhere in it for what runs in your blood."

She paused.

"I know what you are not. You are not an Eliant, though the world will name you one because it is the nearest small word the world happens to own. You are not a tale out of the temples, though something in those tales was afraid of something real."

She stepped closer.

"You are older than any answer I can give you. Stranger. I have spent the whole of your life standing between you and a world that murders on sight the thing it cannot name."

Her voice went quieter.

"And I am telling you tonight that I cannot go on standing there much longer. You have grown too bright to hide behind the body of one tired woman."

---

"Then what do I do?"

It came out of him small. In the voice of the child she had once sat up with, through a night's fever, until the dawn.

She crossed the room. Took his face in both her hands the way she'd once taken Raijeen's.

There was no softness left in her now. Only the iron that had been under the softness the whole time.

"You stop hiding," she said. "Not from them. From *it.*"

She paused.

"That was my mistake, and I made it out of love. Which is the one excuse that has never once worked in the whole long history of the world. I taught you to fear the fire and press it down. A thing you only ever press down is a thing you never come to know. A thing you never come to know will, on the worst night of your life, when you can least afford the lesson, rise up and own you whole."

Her thumbs pressed at the corners of his jaw.

"I have watched it happen to others. I will not watch it happen to you."

She paused.

"You are going to have to learn what you are, Aesir. Not to wield it. To *survive* it. Because the world has just been told that you burn. The world is already on the road. It will not sit and wait for you to be ready."

---

Something in Aesir that had been falling for four days struck a floor at last.

Found, to its own surprise, that a floor is a thing a person can stand on.

He was still afraid. Would go on being afraid, in a low and constant way, for years to come.

But underneath the fear a harder thing was setting. The way water sets to ice from the bottom of a still pond up.

If the world was coming for him, then he was finished sitting in the dark waiting to be found by it.

He would go out and find the thing that hunted him before it finished the finding of him.

He would learn what he was by walking, on his own two feet, straight into the faces of the people who already seemed to know.

---

**✦✦✦**

Naska had told him to stay away a few days. He lasted five, which was, for Aesir, a form of discipline.

When he came down to the Waterworks again he came differently.

Not creeping down to scratch at an itch he could not name, but walking down to begin a thing.

She read the change in him before he'd opened his mouth. The way she read everything. Quick as a hand reading a coin in the dark by its weight alone.

Her first words were not a greeting.

"You've got a look," she said. "I don't like the look."

"The ink comes from outside. So does the thing that hunts me. They are the same thread, Naska. I would stake my life on it. I am done sitting in the dark waiting for it to draw tight around my neck."

He said it plainly, without heat. Which was new in him. Frightened her a great deal more than heat would have.

"I am going to follow the Orma up its own supply until it walks me to whoever it is that opens that door."

---

She was quiet a moment.

Then she rose. Strung her bow in that one economical motion that always ended an argument before her mouth got round to confirming it.

"If you are going to do the single stupidest thing I have ever heard a person say out loud," she said, "then you are not doing it with only me at your back. I'm calling my brother."

---

Askan Sulph was Naska's twin the way a wall is the twin of a window. Cut from the one frame and set to opposite work.

Where she was all edges and quickness, he was breadth and stillness. A big unhurried young man who'd taught himself to cross a crowded room without grazing a soul and an argument without lifting his voice.

He carried a glaive taller than himself and used it, as Aesir would come to learn, the way a careful clerk works a ledger. With a patience that made violence look like plain arithmetic.

---

He came in through the low door of the dead pumphouse, where the great stilled machines loomed out of the black like the bones of animals no one alive remembered the shape of.

Considered Aesir for a long, level moment.

Then he said the first thing anyone had offered him in weeks that was neither a warning nor a plea.

"My sister says you glow."

A beat.

"She also says you're going to get us all killed. I came to learn which of the two she's the more afraid of."

---

"Both of you carry half Eliant blood from your mother's line," Aesir said, because he'd spent the long empty days doing the one thing he was truly good at, which was noticing.

"The bone. The way neither of you breaks when by rights you should. It is why you fight the way you fight. It does not make you like me. But it means you already know in the body what it is to carry a thing the city would burn you for, and to keep it quiet, and to be looked at sideways your whole life long for a strangeness you never once asked for."

---

Askan and Naska traded a glance. That private and wordless dispatch of twins that passes clean over the heads of everyone else in the room.

Something in the pumphouse settled and became a decision without anyone having to call for a vote.

"The Orma trade is eating the under city alive, a mouthful at a time," Askan said at last. Planted the butt of the glaive on the wet stone. "It has taken people we grew up beside. If your monster and that trade are the one monster, then to hunt the one is to hunt the other. I have wanted an honest reason to hunt the second of them for a long while now."

He looked to his sister, then back to Aesir.

The corner of his mouth shifted without ever fully committing to a smile.

"So. We follow the poison home. Try not to set fire to anything we still have a use for."

---

**✦✦✦**

The truth reached Mandu the way water reaches the lowest room of a flooding house. Which is to say all at once and from above.

Aelayla told him.

Aesir never did learn exactly what she said. Only that he was summoned to his father's study the following evening.

Braced himself, the whole walk down the corridor, for the full weight of a general's disappointment: the lecture, the confinement, the guard nailed to his window.

He got none of it.

---

Mandu stood at the window with his back to the door. The way he stood for all the hardest things.

Did not turn.

When he spoke his voice was very tired and very level.

"I spent the whole of your childhood building walls to keep the world off you," he said. "And you have spent this past year going over every one of them, to go and stand in the exact places I built the walls against."

A long silence sat between them.

"A wall a boy will not stay behind is not a wall. It is a lie a man tells himself so that he can sleep at night."

He turned then. Did not look angry. Looked like a soldier setting down a pack he's carried a full mile past the point of sense.

"So. No more walls. If you will not stop walking into the dark, then you will walk into it trained, and armed, and with men at your back who answer to me. You will bring me home the shape of what you find out there."

He paused.

"There is a thing eating my city from underneath. I would sooner learn its face from my own son than read it off a coroner's report."

---

And so the boy's secret, which had been a thing of locked windows and marks that came from outside, became instead a thing with a roof over it and a discipline beneath it.

Taught across that long season in two rooms that never once knew of one another.

One was a training hall full of daylight and cadets and the honest clatter of wood on wood.

The other was a cold cellar with a single lamp, where his mother taught him the one lesson that none of the cadets could ever be allowed to see.

---

**✦✦✦**

The cellar beneath the house had held wine, once. Now held the two of them and one lamp and a secret that could burn the family to the ground.

It had no window and one door.

Aelayla would not teach it anywhere else.

"What we do down here," she told him the first night, "the world has put people to death for. Keep that close on every night you resent me for the cold."

---

He'd expected drills. Expected her to teach the light the way Mandu taught the blade, by repetition until the body learns the thing below the level of thought.

He was wrong.

The wrongness of it was itself the first lesson.

"Call it," she said.

He set his jaw. Reached down inside himself for the place it had torn open under the Waterworks. Pulled, hard, the way he pulled at everything. With will and want and the flat certainty that had never once failed him in a fight.

Nothing came.

It was like reaching into a dark drawer for a thing you know past doubting that you left there. Closing your fingers on bare wood. Reaching again. Again.

He tried harder, until his hands shook and a vein stood proud at his temple.

Still there was nothing. Not the ghost of a flicker.

He turned to her furious and humiliated and found her watching him with something close to pity.

"You are trying to lift it," she said. "It is not a stone. Sit."

---

"It does not answer strength."

She said it once he'd folded down onto the cold floor, sullen as a struck dog.

"I have watched it in you your whole life. I will tell you the thing it took me years of watching to understand, and which you are going to hate. It answers truth. It rose in you under the Waterworks not because you were strong in that moment but because you were, for once in your clever life, too frightened to lie."

She paused.

"You loved that girl. You believed she was dying. The fear went straight past every one of the charming little walls you have built between yourself and yourself. The fire came up through the gap. That is the only door it has ever used. The truth is the only door it has."

---

"Then ask me something true," Aesir said, half a dare.

She took him at his word.

The questions she set him in that cold room over the next hour were worse than any beating Mandu had ever handed down.

She asked him what he was afraid of. He gave her the brave answer. The room stayed dark.

She asked again. He gave her the clever answer. The room stayed dark.

She only waited, in that particular silence that is a pressure all its own. The kind that leans on a person until something in him gives.

The third time he answered he was too tired to perform.

What came up out of him was small and true and terrible:

That he was afraid the thing in his hands was not a gift and not a curse but only, simply, what he was. All the way down to the floor of himself.

That the boy the family loved was a costume stitched over a furnace.

That one day the costume would burn away in front of all of them and they would finally see the thing that had been wearing it the whole time.

---

The lamp guttered.

Along both his forearms, faint as breath fogging a cold pane, the light came up blue.

It lasted the length of one held breath and went out.

Aesir sat shaking on the floor of the wine cellar. Emptied in a way that had nothing at all to do with muscle.

Understood at last the true shape of the thing he'd signed himself over to.

He'd believed he was learning a weapon.

He was learning a mirror.

---

Every step he took toward the mastery of the fire was going to be a step deeper into the one country he'd spent his whole clever life keeping clear of.

Which was the plain truth about himself.

There were rooms in that country he'd locked for good reasons. The light, he saw now, was going to want the key to every one of them.

"Again," Aelayla said quietly, from the dark beyond the reach of the lamp. "While it is still close to the surface. This is the only way I know to teach you to survive it. I am sorry that the way is a cruel one."

---

**✦✦✦**

By the turning of the year the thing had a rhythm to it.

Twice in a month, sometimes more, they went down into the under city and cut a piece out of the Orma trade: a cache, a courier, a cutting house where the raw ink was stepped on and weighed and bagged for the streets.

Mandu's small unit had teeth now. Half a dozen cadets who'd learned to keep their formations tight and their mouths shut.

At the head of it: three young people who had become, without any one of them quite marking the day it happened, a good deal more dangerous than the plain sum of them.

---

Askan planned the raids the way a patient man lays out a chessboard. Days ahead of the first move. Every exit marked. Every bad ending already grieved in advance and guarded against.

Naska was the thing that happened when the plan met the world. Quick and merciless and forever standing exactly where the trouble was going to be a half second before the trouble arrived to find her there.

Aesir was the part that could not be planned for at all. Not by them and not by the men they hit.

---

It was on a bad night in a flooded cutting house down near the eastern locks that the cadets first saw the whole of what that meant.

A man came at his back that he had no way in the world of seeing. A big man. With a length of iron pipe.

Swung it two handed at the base of Aesir's skull with everything he owned. A blow that would have folded a grown soldier and killed a boy outright.

Aesir was already moving before it landed. Not quite fast enough to be gone but fast enough that the pipe took him across the shoulder instead of the neck.

There was a sound like a hammer coming down flat on a green log.

The man's arms shivered back with the shock of the impact.

Aesir turned around and looked at him with an expression of mild irritation and put him on the floor with a single strike.

---

The pipe had raised a welt he'd feel for a week. It had not broken so much as a thread in him.

It should by every law of the body have shattered the shoulder of anything ever born of a woman.

Every cadet in that room had heard the sound it made.

One of them, a level headed young woman named Perrin who did not rattle easily, said into the sudden quiet:

"What in every god's name are you."

Not as an insult. Not really even as a question. Only as the involuntary noise a person makes when the world in front of them does a thing the world is not permitted to do.

---

Aesir did not answer her.

He'd learned there was no answer that didn't make the whole thing worse.

But he caught Naska's eye across the wet room. She did not look away.

Something passed between them that had been passing more and more often of late. A whole long conversation with not one word laid into it.

---

And always, out at the edges of it, the other thing.

It never came at them head on. Not once in that whole season. Its patience was the worst thing about it.

But the marks kept surfacing.

Orma that bore no maker's stamp any Utiltan foundry had ever pressed into it.

Supply that thinned wherever they struck it in one quarter and thickened in another the following week. The way water pressed flat in one place will always shoulder up in another. As though somewhere far above the whole flood a single unhurried hand were keeping the level.

---

And once, at a cache burning itself down near the western wall, Aesir looked up through the smoke and saw, far back among the watchers where the firelight did not reach, a man in clean clothes who did not run, and did not come closer, and was simply gone the next time the smoke drew breath and shifted.

Aesir told no one.

Told himself it had been a trick of the smoke.

He was becoming, he was uneasily aware, very good at telling himself things. Which was the exact habit the cold cellar kept trying, and failing, to break in him.

---

**✦✦✦**

There was a place Naska took him, once, that winter, that he never told the others of.

Because some things are not for the telling.

High up in the dead quarter of the old city, where the buildings the regime had forgotten leaned their foreheads together over streets no living foot still used, there stood a broken tower with its crown sheared clean off.

If you climbed it, and Naska could climb anything that would hold her weight, you came out over the rooftops of the sleeping city into open, freezing air. The roofs falling away beneath you in a black tide of slate and shadow.

There, in the one direction the streets never once let a person look, you could see it.

The wall.

And past the wall, faint and grey and impossibly far, a low dark line lay along the bottom of the sky like a smudge of charcoal a thumb had very nearly wiped away.

It was not city. Not stone. Not anything at all that Utilta owned a name for.

The forest. The outside. The far edge of the agreed upon world.

---

They sat on the cold ledge with their legs hung over the drop. Not touching.

In that deliberate and painful not touching of two people made keenly aware of the few inches of air between them.

"My mother used to say she could remember outside," Naska said, after a while. "From before the closing. She said the air moved differently out there. That there was a smell that came up after the rain that we don't have in here. Because in here the rain only ever falls on stone."

She was quiet a moment.

"I used to think she made it up to frighten us small. Now I think on it more than I would like to."

She glanced at him, sidelong. None of her usual edge in it. Only a girl born inside a wall looking at a boy who had once lit up from somewhere under his own skin like the first grey coming of a dawn.

"You're going to go out there. Aren't you. Whatever the plans say. Whatever your father sanctions. You decided it a long time back."

---

"Yes," he said.

Because with her he had lost, at some point that winter he could not have marked on a calendar, the ability to do the easy charming thing.

"I have to. It is the only place the answer is."

He looked at the far grey line. Felt the dread of it and the pull of it arrive together in the one breath. The way you feel the whole drop rush up through your stomach in the instant you lean out over a great height and look down.

"You don't have to come."

"Don't," she said. Sharp again for half a second. Then not sharp at all. "Don't insult the both of us."

---

She closed the few inches. Not dramatically. Only leaned until her shoulder came to rest against his.

They sat like that a long while in the cold with the forbidden world laid out grey and far in front of them.

Two people who fought their small wars in the dark and had somehow, in the thick of all of it, become the safest thing that either of them owned.

Neither of them said the larger thing.

They were young. There would be time, they both believed, in the way the young always believe that the supply of time is a well with no bottom to it.

There would not be.

But they did not know that yet. For the length of one hour, high up in the cold above the sleeping city, they were given the grace of not knowing it together.

It was, Aesir would think for the whole of his long and difficult life afterward, the last hour in which he was ever simply happy.

---

**✦✦✦**

The answer, when it finally surfaced, surfaced the way the worst answers always do.

Quietly. On an ordinary night. Out of a stack of papers drawn from the coat of a dead man.

---

It was a courier they had not meant to kill, in a cache house near the western wall.

Aesir knelt in the settling quiet after the fighting. Drew the folded packet from inside the man's coat.

Unfolded it.

Went still.

---

They were plans.

Routes, and dates, and a place.

And the place was not in the city at all.

It sat past it. Past the wall no living soul crossed. Past the ragged edge of the only world any of them had ever been permitted to know.

Deep in the southern forest that Utiltan children were taught of only as a thing their grandmothers' grandmothers had been right to fear.

---

Naska read it over his shoulder a long while. Said nothing.

She'd grown up, as they all had, inside a wall that was less a work of stone than a work of agreement. That shared and unspoken decision never to ask what lay on its far side.

"You understand what this is," she said at last, and her voice had gone strange and flat. "The thing you have been chasing. The thing that has been chasing you. It was never down here at all. It only ever passed through on its way to somewhere else."

She set the papers down as though they'd gone warm in her hand.

"It is outside. If we want it, we have to do the one thing this city was built, from its deep foundations to its high gates, to keep any soul from ever doing."

---

"We have to leave," Aesir said.

No one answered him.

Far above them, muffled by a mile of stone and by long centuries of agreement, the great sealed city slept on. Sure of its walls. Sure that its walls were the edge of the world.

For the first time in the whole of his life Aesir understood that a wall is only the edge of the world for exactly as long as everyone behind it agrees not to walk to the end and look.

He was done agreeing.

Had been done, he understood now, since the night his own two hands had caught fire in the dark.

---

The forest waited out past the wall. Ancient and feared and thick with the answer he had bled a whole season to reach.

And the answer, though he had no way yet of knowing it, had already turned, and risen, and begun its own slow and patient walk toward the forest.

To be there to meet him when he came.
