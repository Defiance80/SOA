# CHAPTER NINE
## A City of Masks

A city keeps its real face in its cheapest rooms.

Utilta wore a hundred masks in the daylight. Aesir had grown up believing every one of them.

The mask of merit that the Academy pinned on the children of the right houses.

The mask of mercy the empire fitted over the collar it locked at an Eliant's throat.

The mask of law, which was the finest of them all, because it was worn even by the men who sold the law by the ounce in back rooms Aesir now knew the way home to.

He was sixteen. Had spent the last year learning to read the faces under the faces.

The lesson had aged something in him that had nothing to do with height.

---

He had not meant to become an investigator of anything.

It had started as a hunger to see. And seeing, once you have properly done it, does not let you stop.

You pull one thread in the Ninth because it is bright. The thread runs to a name. The name runs to a warehouse. The warehouse to a shipment. The shipment to a number of children that you then cannot unknow.

By the turning of the year it was no longer wonder that took Aesir down the old stairs.

It was something with a harder spine. He did not yet have a clean word for it.

The word other people would eventually use was one he'd have been embarrassed to claim: conscience.

---

He had not gone down alone in months.

That was the change the year had made. Surprised him more than any of the rest.

Naska and Askan had found the thing he was doing, in the way the Sulphs found everything, which was by paying attention. Rather than talk him out of it they'd done the far more dangerous thing and joined him.

Three of them now. Threading the dark.

Raijeen did not go.

That was its own quiet fault line. It ran under everything. No one had stepped on it yet.

---

They had, between the three of them, assembled a map of the city no mapmaker in Utilta would have dared to draw.

At the top of it, where the honest maps put the palace, they had learned to put three names instead.

**Marik Draven,** who held the weapons and the muscle. Ran his corner of the underworld the way a landslide runs a hillside: without cleverness, without mercy, and with an absolute confidence in gravity.

**Selene Varros,** who held the narcotics and something worse than narcotics, which was people's wanting. Said to have never once raised her voice because she'd never once needed to.

**Kael Corvis,** who held the thing the other two only rented from him: knowing. Corvis knew. It was his whole trade. He knew which magistrate had a second family in the Fourth and which quartermaster watered the garrison's Orma and skimmed the difference. He sold the knowing back to the city a piece at a time, and grew, in the selling, quietly more powerful than either of the loud ones ever understood.

---

Three kings.

Aesir had been almost proud of the map. The way you are proud of a hard thing nearly finished.

Until the night a drunk in the Ninth had heard him say the three names and laughed until he had to sit down.

"Kings," the drunk had said, wiping his eyes. "That's good. That's a good one, clean boy. You go on and call them kings."

When Aesir had asked him what was funny, the man had stopped laughing all at once. The way frightened men do.

Looked over both his shoulders in a room where no one was listening.

Said a fourth name so quietly that Aesir felt it more than heard it.

Then he'd gotten up and left his drink and not come back.

Aesir had stood there with the name settling into him like cold into a bone.

**Veskar.**

---

Nobody had seen him.

That was the first thing. Should have been comforting. Was the opposite.

Draven you could find. He wanted finding, wanted the whole Ninth to know which doors were his.

Varros you could find if you had something she wanted. Which meant she found you.

Even Corvis, who lived in shadow as a matter of professional pride, could be located by a person willing to spend enough and lose enough.

But Veskar had no door. Veskar had no face.

Veskar was the reason the other three, who feared nothing on the surface of the world, went careful and quiet in exactly the same way at exactly the same moments. Like three big dogs that have caught, on the wind, the scent of the one animal that eats dogs.

You could triangulate him only by the shape of the silence around him.

And the silence, when Aesir had learned to read its edges, pointed in a direction that made no sense to a boy raised up top:

Not out toward the rich Quarters where power was supposed to live.

But down. Down, and east.

Toward the oldest stone. Past it. Toward a rumor of country that the up top histories did not admit was still inhabited.

## CHAPTER NINE · I
### The Names Above the Names

*Three kings the city could point to, and above them a silence with no face: a boy learning that the map he drew so proudly had a fourth corner, and it pointed down.*

---

Naska came to his room that evening, which she was not supposed to do, and did anyway.

Which was the whole of Naska in a sentence.

"You've gone quiet," she said, shutting the door with the flat of her hand. "You. Quiet. Do you know how wrong that reads on you? It's like watching a fire decide to be a lamp. Everyone in the house has noticed and no one will say it, so I'm saying it."

"I'm thinking."

"You're brooding. There's a difference and you've never once managed the first one."

But she came in. Sat on the edge of his desk. Put her boots among his papers, which she knew he hated. Did it slowly so he'd know she knew.

Then the teasing went out of her. The way it did when she decided a thing was real.

"Talk to me. It's the name, isn't it. The fourth one."

---

He looked at her.

Had the thought he kept having lately and kept not saying: that Naska at sixteen had become a problem his training had no drill for.

She'd come up lean and quick and certain. Her mother's green eyes in her father's hard face. Had a way of arriving in a room that made the room reorganize around her.

He knew the shape of the thing between them now. Even if neither of them had been reckless enough to name it.

Knew it stood in the room with them the way the fourth name stood in his map. Present. Unspoken. Bending the geometry of everything.

---

"Veskar's not a crime lord," he said instead, because it was safer.

"That's what I keep circling. Draven and Varros and Corvis, those are men who want the city. Veskar doesn't want the city. You can feel it in the way the money moves. He wants something the city is only in the way of."

He rubbed his eyes.

"Every thread I pull runs the wrong direction. Down. East. I keep waiting for it to turn toward the Quarters, toward some fat man in a good house. It never does. Just keeps going down, past where the maps stop."

"Toward Uwell," Naska said.

She said it lightly. Said it the way you say a word you've been told not to.

"That's what you're not saying. The old smugglers' word. Everything that doesn't make sense in this city, sooner or later somebody blames the underground east and the ghosts that are supposed to live in it."

"I'm not blaming ghosts."

"No. You're worse. You're starting to believe in them."

She held his eyes a moment. The tease came back up over the worry like water closing over a stone.

"Which is why you need people with you who believe in arrows. Askan's found the meeting. Corvis, in the flesh, and the other two. Tomorrow night, a dead warehouse on the east edge. If we're ever going to put a face to the silence, that's the room to do it in."

"And if it's a trap."

"Then it's a trap with the three loudest men in the underworld standing in it. We'll have learned something about who set it."

She stood. Gathered her boots off his papers. Paused at the door with her hand on it.

"Bring your brother's head and leave his heart at home, Aesir. You go in there wanting a fight, the way you always do, and one night the room's going to be glad to give you one."

"You sound like Raijeen."

"Raijeen's right about you. That's the most annoying thing about him."

And she was gone. The room felt smaller with her out of it.

Which he also did not say.

---

**✦✦✦**

He found Raijeen in the study, which was where he always found him now.

The finding always cost Aesir something small he never named.

His brother was thirteen and looked at the world like a man reading its mail.

The blue had come full into his hair, deep and strange. He sat cross legged in Aelayla's lamplight with a book open that Aesir could not have read a single line of.

This, more than the height difference reversing, more than the changing voice, was the thing that told Aesir they were growing into separate countries.

Aesir had gone out into the city to learn what was true.

Raijeen had gone inward, into the drowned history, to learn the same thing by the opposite road.

Somewhere in the going they'd each started to carry a knowledge the other couldn't hold.

---

"You're going tomorrow," Raijeen said, not looking up.

He didn't ask how he knew. Never did. One of the things about him that had stopped being uncanny and started being simply Raijeen.

"Askan found a meeting."

"And you didn't ask me."

Now he looked up. The old silver eyes had that quality they got, of holding still while everything moved behind them.

"You never ask me. You take Naska into the worst rooms in the world and you leave me here with the books. You tell yourself it's because I'm younger. It isn't. You know it isn't."

---

"It's because you're worth more than the rooms," Aesir said.

Meant it. Watched it land wrong.

"That's what people say about a thing they've decided to keep on a shelf."

Raijeen closed the book. Slowly. With the care he gave everything.

"I'm not fragile, Aesir. I'm the one who can tell you what these people actually are. You're out there learning who moves the Orma. I'm in here learning why a thing like Orma exists. Who first cut a sacred science into a street poison and taught a whole empire to hang the poor for touching what the rich sell."

He paused.

"You need both. You've always needed both. You just like your half better because your half lets you hit something."

---

It was so exactly true that Aesir had no answer to it.

The not having was its own kind of answer. Both of them heard it.

"When it's safe," Aesir said finally, which was the lie brothers tell, "I'll bring you in."

"It's never going to be safe."

Raijeen opened his book again. Which was how he said the conversation was over.

"That's the part you haven't learned yet. You think you're waiting for safe. You're just waiting for me to stop asking."

Then, quieter, to the page, so quietly that Aesir almost missed it, and would remember it later, after, when remembering it hurt:

"Go carefully. You're the only brother I've got. I've read enough history to know how the world treats the ones who go out alone into the dark to fix it."

---

## CHAPTER NINE · II
### Separate Countries

*Two brothers grown into different rooms: one who went out into the city to learn what was true, one who went inward into the drowned history to learn it the other way, and the quiet fault line between them.*

---

**✦✦✦**

The warehouse crouched on the eastern edge of the city where the pale stone gave out and the old dark stone began.

In the seam between the Utilta the empire admitted to and the Utilta it had built over and forgotten.

It had been something once. A granary or a customs house. Now it was nothing.

Which in this city meant it was perfect for the things nothing is good for.

---

They came in through a bad window Askan had found the night before.

Because Askan always found the window the night before.

That was Askan. He arrived early in his mind. Walked the room before he walked it. By the time his body caught up he'd already lived the next hour twice.

He went first. Broad and silent. A shape that had no business moving as quietly as it did.

Naska went second. Bow strung and low.

Aesir went last.

The smell of the place closed over the three of them like a held breath. Damp wood. Cold iron. Old rot.

---

Voices, from the deep of the building.

They found the crates. Put the crates between themselves and the sound. Eased up close.

Three of them stood in a cleared space under a single failing bulb.

Aesir knew two of the faces from a year of hunting them.

**Draven,** a wall of a man, arms crossed, contributing to the meeting mostly by being large near it.

**Varros,** still and lovely and cold, watching the others the way a cat watches a room it already owns.

And a third who could only be **Corvis,** because he was the one Aesir's eye kept sliding off of. The one built by long practice to be forgotten the instant he left your sight.

Speaking now in a voice pitched to travel exactly far enough and not one inch farther.

---

"The shipment moves on schedule," Corvis was saying. "The forest site is prepared. What Veskar wants out of that ground, Veskar will have. Our part is to see the road stays quiet while he takes it."

"And our part gets paid," Draven said, "or Veskar can dig his own dirt."

"You'll be paid."

Something moved across Varros's lovely face. There and gone.

Aesir, who had learned this year to read the small weather of people, understood with a jolt that it was fear.

And under the fear, worse: a kind of longing.

"We are always paid. That's what should frighten you, Marik, if you had the wit for it. Never once been cheated. Never once been crossed. Never once had a man of ours simply vanish in a way that couldn't be answered."

She paused.

"Do you know any other power in this city you could say that of? I don't. Men who pay perfectly and are never seen aren't being generous. They're keeping accounts we haven't been shown."

---

"Veskar hears you talk like that," Corvis said mildly, "he'll hear it wherever you say it. You know the stones he uses. You've held one."

Varros went quiet.

Her hand rose halfway to the collar of her gown as if to check for something that might be listening there.

Lowered again.

That small aborted gesture told Aesir more than the whole rest of the meeting: that even these three, at the top of the visible world, lived under an ear they could not find and could not close.

---

"We've seen enough," Naska breathed, her mouth an inch from Aesir's ear. "The forest site. That's the thread. We take it home and we pull it where there's light."

Askan's hand said the same thing in the dark. Two fingers, a flat cut: *out.*

They would have.

Were already easing back when the old floor, which had held for the three kings and their guards all evening, chose Aesir's weight and the wrong board and gave a single long groan that carried across the whole dead building like a word spoken in a church.

---

Everything stopped.

"Who's there."

Corvis did not shout it. That was the terrifying thing. He said it the way you'd note a change in the weather.

The guards were already moving before the sentence finished. Spreading out with the ugly competence of men who did this often.

"Go," Aesir said.

They went.

---

It came apart the way these things come apart.

Fast and stupid and loud. All the careful quiet spent in an instant and nothing left but bodies deciding in the dark.

They ran the crates.

A guard rose up in front of Askan and Askan simply removed him from the path without breaking stride. A short brutal economy of motion. No anger in it. The fighting of a man doing sums.

Naska put an arrow into a shoulder that was about to hold a blade. The blade rang on the stone unused.

They were three strides from the bad window and the cold clean night beyond it when the last of Draven's men closed the gap.

Four of them. Hard men with no theater in them.

There was no more running to do. Only the other thing.

Aesir met them.

---

## CHAPTER NINE · III
### The Board That Groaned

*A meeting glimpsed from the dark, a name that keeps its face, and one wrong board in an old floor: the clean plan spending itself all at once into the ugly arithmetic of a fight.*

---

He had told himself, all year, a quiet arrogant thing:

*I have never met the room I couldn't walk out of.*

Four hard men in a dark warehouse was, it turned out, still not that room.

Which was exactly the problem. Because it taught him nothing except the wrong lesson again.

---

He moved the way he always moved. A half beat ahead of the world. His body answering a question before his mind had finished asking it.

The first man swung and Aesir was already not there.

The second came in low and Aesir turned him with a short sword and put an elbow through his guard and felt the man fold.

It was fast. Too fast. The way it was always too fast.

The eerie effortless speed that the Academy masters had never been able to account for and that Mandu watched with a father's pride and a soldier's private dread.

Danger slowed around Aesir. It always had. He'd stopped questioning it.

He had, God help him, started to enjoy it.

---

Which was why the third man's knife found him.

It was a small mistake. The kind arrogance makes. A half beat spent grinning that should have been spent moving.

The blade went in along his ribs. Not deep. A hot line. The first real wound the underworld had ever given him.

---

And here is the thing that mattered. The thing Naska would carry out of that warehouse and turn over in the dark for a long time after:

It should have been worse.

A knife to the ribs of a sixteen year old boy is a bad night at best and a funeral at worst.

Aesir took it, hissed, and kept fighting.

The wound closing itself around the shock of it as though his body had simply declined the appointment.

He put the third man down. Turned to the fourth.

And that was when it happened.

---

Naska saw it from six feet away, mid draw.

For the rest of her life she would not be able to say it cleanly to anyone. Because there were no clean words for it. Because saying it would have made him a thing the world hunts.

The lines came up under his skin.

Not blood. Not sweat.

*Light.*

Thin veins of it. Gold going to white. Surfacing along his forearms and up the side of his throat. Tracking some hidden geometry no wound and no lamp could explain.

Brightening in the exact instant the pain and the fury and the animal nearness of death all crested in him at once.

It lasted a breath. Maybe two.

It threw a faint warmth she felt on her own face across the six feet of dark between them.

---

The last henchman saw it too. Up close.

His hard competent face came apart into pure superstitious terror.

He did the one useful thing he did all night, which was drop his blade and run.

Screaming a word she'd heard the frightened use her whole life. A word that had chained her people and burned their cities and was, she understood with a lurch, now being screamed at the boy she'd been carefully not naming her feelings for.

*Eliant. Strange Eliant. Run.*

---

Then Aesir breathed out. Ragged. Doubling over the wound at his ribs.

The light went back down under him like a tide pulled home.

He was only a bleeding sixteen year old boy again in a dark warehouse.

The fight was over. The quiet came down hard.

---

Nobody moved for a moment.

Askan stood with his glaive still up. Staring. A strategist handed a variable that broke the equation.

Naska lowered her bow slowly.

Aesir straightened, one hand pressed to his ribs, and looked at the two of them looking at him.

Something in his face that Naska had never once seen there in a year of the worst rooms in the world came up and showed itself before he could stop it.

Not the grin. Not the heat.

Fear.

Fear of the exact kind she had, and had learned young to hide, and recognized instantly because it was her own:

The fear of the extraordinary person in a world that has only two uses for them.

---

"Say it," he said.

His voice wasn't steady.

"Whatever you're going to say. Say it now, so I know."

---

And Naska, who had a hundred sharp things ready at all times, who had never in her life been caught without something to throw, looked at the boy standing there braced for her to become the world.

Put every one of the sharp things down.

"I knew you were fast," she said. "Known you were wrong fast since the Academy yard. But that—"

She stopped. Started again. Her voice did a thing it almost never did, which was shake.

"What in every drowned god's name was that, Aesir? You lit up. From the inside. You took a knife in the ribs and shrugged it off like a raindrop and then you lit up like a lamp with a person inside it."

She paused.

"I want to be angry that you never told me. Except I can see your face. Your face is telling me you didn't know either."

---

"I didn't."

It came out of him cracked.

"It's happened before. Small. At the edges. I always told myself it was nothing. The heat, the light. I told myself—"

He stopped.

"Naska. I don't know what I am."

---

It was the most naked thing he'd ever said to anyone.

He'd said it to her. Both of them felt the room change around the saying of it.

---

She crossed the six feet. Took his bloody hand off his ribs. Looked at the wound, which had no business having stopped bleeding as much as it had.

Looked back up at his face.

Made the choice that would tie her to him past all sense and past the tragedy still years down the road and waiting:

She chose not to be afraid of him out loud.

"Then we'll not know together," she said. "But you'll bind that rib before you drip a trail of yourself across half the east side. Because whatever you are, you still bleed. There are men in this city who'd pay Corvis a fortune to know exactly how much."

She paused.

"On your feet, strange thing. We're going home."

---

## CHAPTER NINE · IV
### The Light Under the Skin

*The thing the world is trained to fear, surfacing for the first time where a friend can see it: gold going to white under the skin, a body reaching past its owner, and the one who chose not to be afraid of it out loud.*

---

**✦✦✦**

They got out clean. Or clean enough.

Askan led them home the long crooked way, doubling their trail through the Ninth. Did not once ask Aesir the question that was sitting behind his strategist's eyes.

Aesir understood the not asking was Askan's version of loyalty. Was grateful for it in a way he couldn't say.

---

On a fallen henchman, in the running, Naska had lifted a folded sheaf of papers.

Because Naska lifted things in the running. It was a reflex her mother had drilled into her before her mother died.

Back in the warmth of the estate, with the door shut and a lamp lit and Aesir's rib bound in strips of a shirt he'd have to explain to no one because no one up top asked the right questions, they spread the papers out and read them.

The reading changed the shape of the war.

---

A site. Deep in the southern forest. Past the last of Utilta's admitted roads. In the green dark where the city's law had never once bothered to reach.

A distribution center, the papers called it. In the bloodless language men use for the things they don't want to picture.

This was where the Orma was finished.

This was where the thing Veskar wanted out of the ground was being pulled up and packed and sent across Quandrum.

This was the road he'd paid three kings to keep quiet.

---

"The forest," Askan said.

There was something in how he said it. An old unease. The forest's long bad reputation stirring under the practical words.

"Everyone's got a story about that forest and none of them end well. Now we know why somebody's been so glad to let the stories stand."

"We have to go," Aesir said.

"We have to *think,*" Naska corrected, but she was already looking at the map, already gone into it.

Aesir saw that she would go. That they would all go. That the thread ran into the trees now and there was no version of the three of them that didn't follow it.

---

He did not sleep.

The rib throbbed. Under the throb sat the memory of the light, his light, coming up out of him where a friend could see it. The word the henchman had screamed running its cold finger down his spine.

*Strange Eliant.*

He'd been called it before, as an insult, a boy's slur in an Academy yard.

He'd never before had a reason to wonder if it was true.

Worse: if true was even the right word for whatever he actually was.

Aelayla had raised Raijeen on the truth of the Eliants. Nothing in it had ever mentioned a boy who lit up from the inside and healed like the wound was an insult he could refuse.

---

He went to the study before dawn. Didn't know why, except that the study was where the true things in this house were kept.

Where Raijeen would be. Because Raijeen was always where the true things were.

His brother was awake, of course.

Looked at Aesir standing in the doorway with his shirt stiff at the ribs and his face gone strange.

Raijeen did not ask what happened. Did not say *I told you the rooms would give you a fight one night,* though both of those were sitting right there.

He simply closed his book. Moved over on the floor to make a space in the lamplight the way he had when they were small and one of them had a nightmare.

Said, "Sit down and tell me. All of it. The part you're afraid to say first."

---

And Aesir, who'd gone out into the whole dark city to learn what was true and had come home carrying a true thing he couldn't hold alone, sat down in the gold light beside the brother he'd been keeping on a shelf.

Told him.

Somewhere in the telling, the fault line that had been running silently under everything did not close, exactly, but it stopped widening.

Because the one thing the two of them had never once failed to do, in all their growing apart, was carry each other's worst thing when it came.

---

Outside, the city put its daylight masks back on.

Draven counted his coin and Varros checked her collar for a stone she'd never find and Corvis forgot himself, professionally, into another ordinary morning.

Far below all of them, in the old east dark, a silence with no face kept its own accounts.

One of the accounts, though the boy did not know it yet and would not learn it for years, had just had a new line entered in it, in a careful hand:

*A strange light, in an east edge warehouse, in a boy who bled gold.*

Veskar heard everything.

That was the whole of what Veskar was.

And the stones that swallow sound had been in the room.

---
