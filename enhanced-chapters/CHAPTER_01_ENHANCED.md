## CHAPTER ONE
### Sleeping Gods

**What follows is a piece of history the modern world only half-remembers. Keep it in mind — almost no one alive in this story knows it.**

---

Five hundred years of quiet. Not the quiet of peace—the quiet that follows a wound.

There was a time, long enough ago to have hardened from history into legend, when a single people held the center of the world. The Eliants: born with light in their eyes and power in their minds, able to move what they did not touch and know what had not yet happened. From Profront, their civilization rose so high that nations across Eloquence measured themselves against it. For golden centuries, the Eliants were the mark.

Admiration sours. Envy follows. Fear comes last.

The fear became a war—a hundred years of it, fought not over land but over a question: does such power have any right to exist?

It ended the way these things do. Exhaustion. Ruin. Profront left poisoned. The Eliants shattered, scattered, their gifts shackled by law, their place reduced from marvel to menace. Out of that ending came the present world: empires risen on Eliant defeat, borders drawn in blood, and a peace that was less agreement than held breath.

And behind even the Eliants lies a fainter shape. The Indextos—beings of older power still, said to have touched creation itself before vanishing. But no living soul has seen one. What survives is rumor, myth, the kind of story told to children and believed by no one.

This is the world as it stood: ancient powers spent, a great people brought low, secrets buried.

This story begins late in that long forgetting—on a night with no name, when something out of the nearly lost ancient world fell from the sky and landed in the desert of a country called Quandrum.

It begins, as such things often do, with a man who thought he was only chasing a question.

**✦✦✦**

His name was Mandu Dakshad, and the first sign the world was changing came as ash.

## CHAPTER ONE · I
### The Ride Toward the Mountain

*Mandu's column races north across broken country toward an ancient ruin—and the Beastmen who reached it first.*

---

He noticed it the way he noticed everything that didn't belong—a wrongness at the edge of sense, caught and filed for later. Pale flakes drifting down through amber dawn, settling on armored shoulders, on cracked earth, on the upturned faces of soldiers too young to distrust weather.

It looked like snow.

The southern desert had never seen snow. And snow didn't smell like this—sulfur, sharp enough to taste, the reek of something buried deep that had been cracked open to air.

Mandu kept the thought to himself. A commander who spoke his unease only spread it.

He watched the horizon instead. Haze thickening into shapes. Shapes sharpening into the outlines of men who'd stopped being men.

They came over the ridge in a flood.

Twisted. Goat-horned. The northern tribes called them blessed. Everyone south of the sea called them the Beastmen of Faint. Common knowledge said Valkhorra took its own people and remade them with alchemy—stronger, crueler, past what nature allowed.

Mandu had read the field reports. Trimmed the worst details down to rumor.

Now, watching them pour toward his line, he understood he'd trimmed away the truth.

Horns spiraled like disease made solid. Fur matted dark with something that wasn't sweat. Eyes lit from within—not fever, something else. The eyes of men emptied out and refilled with another thing's hunger.

"Form up!" Bandar's voice cracked down the line. The general was a mountain of a man with a way of giving orders that sounded like heavy doors slamming shut. "No quarter. They've got none in them to give."

The line locked. Behind them, pacers—hovering war-vehicles that had carried the company across the desert all night—powered down with a dying whine, settled into dust.

The gap closed.

The morning broke apart into violence.

---

The first creature came at Mandu in a dead sprint, crude axe arcing to open him shoulder to hip.

He didn't block. Blocking was death—the thing carried two men's weight behind that swing.

He stepped into it. Close enough to gag on rot breath. The axe hissed past his ear instead of through his chest.

Same motion, blade up. Found the soft gap beneath the arm where no armor covered. Point through lung. Wrench free. Turn before the shriek finished leaving its throat.

"The joints!" Not full voice—full voice was for men frightened of their own silence. "They're stronger. Not faster. Take them at the joints and they fall like anything else."

Should've been a clean fight. These were southern soldiers, hardened against raiders every season, men who fought with the grim patience of people defending home.

But the Beastmen wouldn't die the way dying men die.

One took Mandu's sword clean through the belly and kept coming. Walked up the blade toward him, jaw working, utterly indifferent to its own ruin. He had to plant a boot against its hip and shove it off the steel like a farmer freeing a stuck spade. Black fluid sheeted his arm to the elbow.

Second one howled over the first's body. He dropped under its swing—wind tore the sweat off his face—rose inside its reach, opened its belly with a single upward cut. It folded around its own spilling weight.

---

To his left, a scream cut through the din. Not the battle scream of a man still fighting. The other kind.

Lorian—the young scout, barely twenty—was down. Three Beastmen had him. Were dragging him backward across the cold desert sand even as they hacked at him.

"Lorian!" Mandu was already moving. Bandar a half step behind.

But the gap was wrong. Too much ground. Too many bodies between.

They ran. Leapt over corpses. Ducked under a wild swing. The sound Lorian was making got worse. Then stopped being a scream at all. Just a wet, breathless choking.

By the time they closed the distance, it was done.

The boy's lower half was gone. Simply gone. Severed clean at the waist, the two pieces of him lying in the sand like a broken doll someone had forgotten to put away.

Mandu drove his blade through the nearest Beastman's throat. Bandar took the other two in a single sweeping arc that painted the ground black.

But Lorian was already past helping.

The rot was spreading.

Mandu had seen it before, in reports. Never this close. Never this fast.

From the ragged edges of the wound, black veins were creeping upward into what was left of the boy's torso. Not blood. Something else. The alchemical poison the Beastmen carried in their claws and blades, designed to spread, to corrupt, to turn living flesh into the same twisted ruin they'd made of themselves.

Lorian's eyes found Mandu's. Still aware. Still in there.

"Sir—"

"I know." Mandu knelt. Put a hand on the boy's shoulder. "You held the line. You did everything right."

The black veins reached Lorian's chest. His breath went shallow. Rattling.

"Antidote's in the med packs," one of the nearby soldiers said, voice high and thin. "If we—"

"He doesn't have hours." Mandu didn't look up. Kept his eyes on Lorian's. "This one's too far gone."

The boy nodded. Just once. Understanding.

Mandu drew his knife.

"You won't feel it," he lied.

Made it quick.

---

When he stood, the black rot had already begun to fade from Lorian's body. The poison needed living flesh. Without it, the alchemy simply... stopped. Left behind only a boy in pieces, looking smaller in death than he ever had in life.

Mandu wiped his blade on his cloak. Turned to the rest of the platoon, who'd gone quiet in a circle around them.

"The rot spreads from their weapons. From their claws. You've got hours at most before it takes you past saving. Anyone gets touched, you call it. Loud. We've got antidote, but only if you tell us in time."

He paused.

"Lorian didn't call it because he was trying to hold. Don't be Lorian. Call it. That's an order."

The men nodded. Some of them looked at the body. Most looked away.

"Wrap him," Mandu said. "We'll carry him home."

Then the world closed in.

Three of them. Wheeling together. Surrounding him the way a fist closes around a moth.

High blow toward his skull. Low sweep at his knees.

No version of the next half-second where he survived both.

He threw his blade up—force rang down his arm into his back teeth—twisted away from the low strike. Not fast enough.

Horn raked hot across his thigh.

For one strange, slow, almost peaceful instant, Mandu Dakshad looked at his own death arranged around him and thought, with the flat calm thirty years of war had hardened into him: *So this is it. Killed by things too far gone to know they've won.*

Then Bandar hit them like weather changing.

## CHAPTER ONE · II
### Mandu and Bandar

*Two veterans fighting as one machine—Mandu low and precise, Bandar high and devastating—amid the swarming Beastmen of Faint.*

---

The general's glaive came in level with a throat. Didn't stop at the first throat it found.

Opened one neck, carried through, buried itself in the second's shoulder. Flung both off Mandu's flank in a tangle of black spray and shattered horn.

Bandar planted, spun the weapon, drove the butt-spike through the third one's eye with a grunt that was almost bored.

The closing fist came apart.

Mandu found footing. The two old soldiers fell into rhythm—Mandu low and precise, Bandar high and devastating, moving as a single machine that had been running, with only occasional repair, for most of both their lives.

The Beastmen had raw strength.

Mandu and Bandar had each other. Decades of shared violence. That was the whole difference between living and dead.

---

Last creature fell. The field went quiet the way battlefields do when screaming stops and wind remembers it has other places to be.

Mandu pressed two fingers to the gash on his thigh. Shallow. Would scar. Would ache. Would heal. He'd taken worse for less.

Around him, men stood bloodied but upright—most of them upright—among corpses steaming faintly in cold dawn air, the alchemy in their flesh still working even after death, refusing to let them be simply, cleanly dead.

Bandar nudged one body with his boot. Beneath the horns, horribly, it still wore most of a human face.

"Valkhorra's handiwork," he said. "That alchemy. Twist a man until a blade can't find anything left inside him that still wants to live."

He turned north, the direction the horde had come from.

"But here's what troubles me. They were retreating, Mandu. Already pulling back—north, deeper into Quandrum, not away from it." He paused. "Man doesn't cross the northern sea and march a thousand miles inland just to die. Not for nothing."

"No," Mandu agreed. "He doesn't."

He tilted his head back. Let the foul ash settle on his face. Turned the puzzle over the way Aelayla turned over difficult verse—patient, certain the meaning was there and only the language was missing.

The Beastmen hadn't been fleeing. They'd been racing *toward* something. His company had just had the bad luck to plant itself in the way.

He raised his voice. "Mount up. Whatever's making this ash lies north. We follow it to the source."

---

**✦✦✦**

The land changed as they rode—the way a face changes when it stops trying to look pleasant.

Cracked flats gave way to broken hill-country. Slopes rising sharp. Unwelcoming. The ash grew thicker until it lay over everything like a burial shroud pulled over a body.

The pacers cut dark trails through grey. The grey closed behind them.

Lorian spotted it first—youngest scout, still had the eyes of a man who hadn't seen enough of the world to stop truly looking.

"Sir. Ridge ahead. Something built into the hillside."

---

It rose half buried, as though the earth had tried to swallow it whole and given up before the work was done.

The stone was ancient.

Mandu used that word deliberately. Not the weathered stone of some abandoned fort he could name. This was older than Utilta's white towers. Older than the empire those towers belonged to. Older, he suspected, than the people who told stories about how old things were allowed to be.

From cracks in its surface, pale sulfur rose slow into air—as though the structure were a sleeping thing, and this was the rhythm of its breathing.

Bandar dismounted. Went completely still.

From a man like Bandar, that was the equivalent of a shout.

He laid one armored hand flat against the carved face. Worn into stone: a procession of robed figures, features rubbed nearly smooth by uncountable years, faces lifted upward—some in worship, some in unmistakable terror.

And beneath them, carved smaller, driven along like herded cattle: beasts. Horned. Twisted.

The exact shape of the corpses cooling on the field behind them.

---

## CHAPTER ONE · III
### The Sleeping Gods

*Robed figures lifted in worship and terror, horned beasts herded beneath—a carving older than the empire, older than the name Valkhorra.*

---

"The Sleeping Gods," Bandar said quietly.

He wasn't a religious man. Spoke the name the way you speak words from a childhood song, before the meaning was understood.

"Old northern tribes worshipped them. Long before Valkhorra was even a name." He traced one weathered stone face with a fingertip. "My grandmother had a rhyme. *The gods did not die, child—they only lay down to rest. And the world was built quietly ever after, so as not to wake them.*"

Mandu kept his next thought behind his teeth. Not the kind of thing a commander said in front of frightened men.

*Then the Beastmen weren't here to raid. They were here to wake something.*

*And whichever way you measure it, they arrived at the wrong hour—either far too late, or far too soon.*

"Spread out," he said instead. "Search it. I want to know what they came all this way for—before it finds someone who knows how to use it."

---

*The reader should understand something Mandu cannot:*

*He has already made the decision that will reshape his entire life.*

*He simply hasn't walked far enough into the ruin to find it waiting.*

*And when he does, he will call the feeling duty—because duty is a weight he was built to carry—and spend the rest of his years half-believing that was all it ever was.*

---

**✦✦✦**

Deep inside the structure, they came to a chamber that was cold, silent, and lit by something that wasn't fire.

They entered the way soldiers enter a place they already suspect is a tomb. Slow. Torches raised. Shoulders drawn against darkness that felt textured, like it had grain.

The carvings were denser here. Crowded. Frantic. Covering every surface—as though whoever cut them had been running out of stone or time and decided, at the end, to use up all the stone.

And at the center, surrounded by machinery that had no business still existing this deep—seized gears, hanging chains, rods of clouded crystal glowing with sourceless light—stood two pods.

Each pulsed with soft inner light.

Each held a child.

## CHAPTER ONE · IV
### The Pods

*At the chamber's heart, ringed by dead machinery and ancient chains, two pods pulse with soft inner light—and each holds a child.*

---

Mandu crossed toward them before he'd consciously decided to move.

Two boys.

The elder, maybe nine or ten. The younger, six or seven at most.

They lay in the complete, unguarded trust of children who hadn't yet been taught the world has sharp edges.

Even unconscious, the older boy wore fierceness—a set to his jaw that promised an argument with whoever woke him.

The younger lay neat and composed, hands folded, as though he'd carefully put himself away and fully intended to be found exactly as he'd left himself.

And both of them were glowing.

Faintly. From beneath the skin.

Not the sickly blue green of Orma—Mandu had seen that drug's glow often enough to carry private hatred for it.

Not Eltech, the biotechnology that let ordinary Utiltans perform extraordinary things.

Not any light his long, hard, well-traveled life had ever given him a name for.

"By the gods," Bandar breathed beside him. "What *are* they?"

"I don't know."

Mandu's voice came out hushed. Almost reverent. He disliked himself a little for it.

"But they aren't ours. Look—" He gestured at the writing on the casings. "Not Uwellian. Not Utiltan. Not any script I've seen in a lifetime of seeing them."

He laid his bare palm against the cold crystal over the older boy's chest.

For one impossible heartbeat, he'd have sworn the light leaned toward his hand—tilting the way a sleeping dog turns toward warmth.

"Someone placed them here. Hid them on purpose. Meant for them to be found—but not by us. Not now. Not anywhere near now."

The earth answered before he'd finished speaking.

---

Low shudder rolled up through the floor.

All at once, dead machinery woke.

Gears tearing rust loose with a screech. Chains snapping taut like gallows ropes. Light around the boys flaring so sudden, so brilliant, Mandu threw an arm across his eyes.

Dust sifted from a ceiling lost somewhere in dark above.

"Get them out—*now!*" He was already wrenching at the crystal seam. "Whatever this thing was built to do, it's doing it. I won't have them inside when it finishes."

Other hands joined his in blinding light.

Sparks bled off seized metal.

Crystal casings cracked. Split. Breathed out trapped cold air—so cold it burned the back of Mandu's throat.

The older boy's eyes opened.

---

They found Mandu's at once.

Grey. Threaded with veins of gold.

*Awake.*

Not the blind, underwater confusion of a child surfacing from sleep.

A deep, steady, knowing regard—as though something seated far back behind the boy's face had been waiting a very long time, and had just now opened its eyes onto the first thing in a new world, and was quietly taking that thing's measure.

The boy's lips parted.

No word came.

Only a thin, ruined gasp—the sound of a throat that hadn't made sound in far longer than a throat is built to survive, and hadn't expected to make one ever again.

Mandu had killed men that morning and felt less than he felt now, under the weight of that gaze.

"Easy." His voice came out gentler than he'd allowed it to be in years. Gentler than a general had any right to sound. "You're safe now. I have you."

---

Beside him, Bandar gathered the younger boy against his breastplate—enormous, brutal man cradling the small limp body with tenderness he'd deny, within the hour, to any soldier foolish enough to mention it.

The boy's legs wouldn't hold. He simply gripped two fistfuls of Bandar's collar. Didn't cry.

Somehow harder to watch than crying would've been.

From the passage behind: a roar. Heavy thunder of approaching feet.

"More of them." Bandar's tenderness folded back into command between one breath and the next. "Can't hold this room. We get them out now, or we die in here keeping them company."

"Then we get them out."

Mandu lifted the older boy into his arms.

The child weighed almost nothing.

The child weighed everything in the world.

"Fall back. Move!"

---

**✦✦✦**

They fought their way back up through dark.

The narrow passage that had felt like a trap coming in became mercy going out—Beastmen could only come one or two at a time, and few things in the world did Mandu and Bandar do better than face horrors one or two at a time.

In the corridor throat, Mandu set the older boy down behind him. Put his own body square between child and dark. Went to work.

No elegance left. Only brutal arithmetic—buying seconds with his own arms and other men's lives.

Thrust to an eye. Boot to a knee, bending it wrong. Backhand cut across exposed throat.

Step back. Again. Again.

Passage so tight the dead had nowhere to fall but onto the feet of whatever came next. Press of their own bodies slowed the living horrors behind.

Bandar held the younger boy with one arm, held the line with the other. Long glaive shortened to a butcher's tool in cramped stone.

Between the two of them, they turned the narrow passage into a mouth that swallowed everything fed into it and gave nothing back.

---

They burst out into grey dawn at last, horde still howling behind.

Bandar passed the younger boy to Lorian's waiting arms. Knelt. Set explosive charges along the entrance with quick, ugly competence—a man who'd sealed tombs before and never once enjoyed the task.

Fuse caught. Hissed.

They ran.

Behind them, ancient stone came crashing down in a roar of dust and a long, low groan—a sound every man who heard it felt was unpleasantly close to a sleeping thing being forced back down into sleep.

---

**✦✦✦**

The pacers carried them south, then west.

Toward home. Toward Utilta. Toward the whole library of questions Mandu didn't yet know how to ask.

The older boy clung to the vehicle's edge, stared out at falling ash with eyes far too aged for the small face that held them.

Seeing, perhaps, a world he couldn't remember leaving.

Or a world he couldn't remember being promised to.

## CHAPTER ONE · V
### Into the Rising Dark

*South and west, toward home and Utilta—Mandu carries away a mystery he does not understand, and a vow he did not mean to make.*

---

Mandu watched him.

Felt that thing settle over him that he'd spend the rest of his life calling duty—because duty was a word whose weight he knew exactly how to carry, and the truer word for it was heavier than anything he'd carried off that battlefield.

"Hold tight."

He'd meant it as an order.

It came out, to his own quiet surprise, as a vow.

"Together, we'll find out who you are—and why the sky saw fit to drop you onto this cursed soil."

The boy didn't understand the words.

But he turned his head. Looked at Mandu. And something in that aged grey patience seemed almost to recognize him—as though, out of all the wreckage of whatever he'd survived, this one weathered face was the very thing he'd been sent across the dark to find.

---

The pacers ran on into rising dark.

Behind them, the Sleeping Gods settled back toward rest.

The ash kept falling—soft, grey, without end—as though the world itself had begun, very quietly, and for reasons not one living soul yet understood, to grieve.

---

Far to the south, where the storm had carried the ash, another figure knelt in another ruin.

He wore a cloak so deep-black it seemed to drink torchlight rather than answer it. His hood was low. The lower face lost beneath it.

He stood over an empty pod—its twin shattered, its keeping already carried off.

He turned something small and red in one gloved hand, watching it pulse in the dim.

Not the common Orma that hollowed men into wraiths.

The rarer thing. The thing the oldest texts named in the same breath as relics of the vanished ones.

He slid the crystal into the pouch at his belt and walked out into the rain, into the dark, into the long first hour of a hunger the whole world would one day learn the name of.
