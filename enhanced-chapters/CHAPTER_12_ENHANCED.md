# CHAPTER TWELVE
## The Children Who Shouldn't Be

The world came back in fragments.

Cold metal against his wrists. The taste of blood in his mouth. The smell of something acrid and industrial.

And pain.

Aesir's eyes opened to bars. Iron. Close. A cage mounted on something that hummed beneath him. Not the steady vibration of wheels on stone. Something else. A low electromagnetic pulse that made his teeth ache.

He was moving.

---

The cage swayed gently. Hovering. But he could hear the rhythm of hooves ahead. The creak of leather harness. Something pulling the hover platform through uneven terrain.

A cargo pacer. Damascus military design. He'd seen them in books. Hover tech married to draft animals to conserve fuel.

Efficient. Pragmatic.

Prison variant.

His hands were bound behind him. Thick rope, professionally tied. His legs chained to a bolt in the cage floor. The gag was gone. They'd removed it.

They wanted to hear him.

---

"Naska!"

His voice cracked. Raw from screaming he didn't remember.

"Let me out! NASKA!"

One of the guards turned. Damascus uniform. Iron grey with crimson trim. The man's face was scarred. Bored.

"Shut up, boy."

Aesir lunged against the chains. They held.

"Where is she? What did you—"

The guard slammed a baton against the bars. The sound rang through Aesir's skull.

"I said shut up."

---

Aesir tried to reach for it. The power. The cold blue fire that lived beneath his skin.

Nothing.

He focused. Reached deeper. Felt for the hum of electromagnetic current. The potential energy in the metal around him. The kinetic force in the moving pacer.

Nothing.

Just emptiness. A void where his power should be.

Panic clawed up his throat.

"What did you give me!?"

The guards laughed.

"Suppression tonic," the scarred one said. "Damascus specialty. Keeps troublemakers quiet." He spat over the side of the pacer. "You'll get used to the numbness. Or you won't. Doesn't matter."

---

Aesir's breath came fast. Shallow.

The emptiness felt wrong. Like a limb he couldn't feel. A part of himself severed and distant.

He tried again. Focused on the chains. Tried to heat the metal. Tried to feel the frequency of the iron.

Nothing.

"Naska..."

His voice broke.

The last thing he remembered. Her smile. Her lips against his. The warmth of her hand in his.

Then the arrow.

The blood.

Her gasping his name.

---

"Is she alive?"

The guards didn't answer.

"Please. Just tell me if she's—"

The baton slammed the bars again.

"One more word and I'll break your jaw."

Aesir closed his eyes. Forced himself to breathe.

She had to be alive. She had to be.

If she wasn't—

He couldn't finish the thought.

---

Hours passed.

Or maybe minutes. Time felt liquid. Unstable.

The pacer had descended long ago. The air was cool. Damp. The sound of hooves echoed through stone tunnels. Underground.

They were already deep in Uwell.

He'd heard of it. The buried kingdom. Six cities carved into the bones of the earth. A place that survived the War of the Black Queen by hiding beneath the poison.

He'd never imagined he'd see it.

Not like this.

---

The guards relaxed after a while. Aesir had gone silent. Slumped against the bars like he'd given up.

He listened.

There were four of them. Two Damascus soldiers up front, controlling the draft beast and navigating. Two Uwell guards in the back, watching the cargo.

Aesir's cage was bolted close to the front. Close enough to hear.

The Damascus soldiers spoke quietly. Thought he was unconscious.

---

"Two more hours to the main city," one said. Scarred face. Grey uniform with crimson trim. Older. "Straight to Nidus's compound. Then we're done with this."

"Good," the other replied. Younger. Less scarred. "Three days underground. I want to see the sky again."

The scarred one snorted. "At least we get to leave. The Uwell guards back there?" He jerked his thumb toward the rear of the pacer. "Smell like no one washes their ass down here."

The younger soldier chuckled.

"Well," he said. "The women at least are attractive."

The scarred soldier grunted. "I had one. She removed the bottom half of her clothing and I swear she was growing plant life."

The younger one laughed. Hard. Tried to stifle it.

"These are the perks I'm looking for," the scarred soldier said. "Take me back to Karkadann."

They both laughed. Low. Crude. Tired.

Silence for a moment. Just the rhythm of hooves. The hum of the pacer.

Then the younger one spoke. Voice lower. More serious.

"Speaking of Karkadann." He shifted. "You really overheard Veskar? Before they deployed us to City 4?"

The scarred soldier's humor faded. He nodded.

"Yeah," the scarred soldier said quietly. "I was outside the war room. Day before we got our orders for City 4." He glanced back at the cage. Aesir didn't move. "Heard Veskar talking to Ramstock. About this prisoner."

"What'd they say?"

The scarred soldier's voice dropped. "Said the boy's Indexto stock."

Silence.

Then the younger soldier laughed. Nervous. Disbelieving.

"Indexto? Come on. Those are stories. Myths."

The scarred soldier's voice went cold. Sharp.

"You think Lord Veskar is lying?"

The younger soldier's laugh died in his throat. Like someone had strangled it.

He swallowed. Hard. Audible.

"No. No, not at all. I just— I didn't mean— My grandmother would beat me for even thinking—" He stumbled over the words. "I mean, I wasn't questioning Lord Veskar. I was just— This is insane information. I don't know what to think. I don't really know enough about the subject matter. I was a kid when I heard those stories. Just a dumb kid. My grandmother told me— I mean, she wasn't lying either. She believed it. I believe it. I believe Lord Veskar. I'm not—"

"Relax," the scarred soldier said. Almost amused now. "I won't report you for being an idiot."

The younger soldier exhaled. Shakily. "Thank you."

"None of us do," the scarred one said. "But Veskar's one of Ramstock's five generals. You think they'd go through all this trouble for nothing? For a story?"

The younger soldier was quiet for a moment.

"I used to hear stories," he said finally. "As a kid. About the Indextos. They were like... gods. Or demons. Depending on who was telling it. My grandmother said they had wild beasts that would devour you. Creatures that obeyed their will. Powers that could rip the sky open."

"Sounds like a bedtime story to keep children in line."

"That's what I thought. But I was a kid." The younger soldier's voice was uncertain now. "You're saying it's real? That this boy—"

"I'm saying Lord Veskar believes it's real. And Ramstock wants him studied. Wants to know what he is. What he can do." The scarred soldier's voice dropped to a whisper. "They said something about genetic markers. Bloodline. Things they thought went extinct two hundred years ago."

Aesir's eyes opened. Just a sliver. He didn't move.

"Extinct like the Indextos?"

"Exactly like the Indextos."

Silence.

"What else did Veskar say?"

The scarred soldier hesitated. Then: "That Nidus thinks she's running this operation. That we're letting her think it. Once we're positioned in Utilta, Uwell becomes... expendable."

"Utilta's sealed. Hard target."

"Already working it. Veskar said they're getting people inside. Slowly. Carefully. Once the sealed city falls, the whole Quandrum continent opens to us."

The younger soldier said nothing. Just stared ahead at the dark tunnel.

---

Aesir's mind raced.

*Indexto.*

He'd never heard the word before. Didn't know what it meant.

But they were talking about him. About his blood. His lineage.

*Like gods. Or demons. Powers that could rip the sky open.*

What was he?

*Genetic markers. Extinct two hundred years ago.*

His parents had never told him. Never said anything about where they came from. Who they were.

*Utilta. My home. They're getting people inside.*

He committed it to memory. Every word. Every fragment.

Even the fear in their voices.

If soldiers who served under one of Ramstock's five generals were afraid of what he was—

What did that make him?

---

Behind the pacer, the two Uwell guards talked quietly.

"Longest transport I've done," one said. "Three days straight."

"Madam Nidus must want this one badly. High-value prisoner."

"Wonder what he did."

"Does it matter? We deliver him. She handles the rest."

They didn't know. Didn't hear the Damascus conversation up front.

Just following orders.

Delivering a package they didn't understand.

---

Aesir's hands curled into fists behind his back.

They'd planned this. All of it.

The abduction wasn't opportunistic. It was surgical.

And Nidus—whoever she was—thought she was making deals with equals.

She had no idea what was coming.

---

The numbness in his chest shifted.

Not fading. Just... changing. A dull throb where the emptiness had been. His thoughts felt sluggish. Heavy. Like wading through mud.

Whatever they'd given him wasn't just suppression. It was something else. Something that made the world tilt and blur at the edges.

His power flickered. Distant. Weak.

Maybe thirty percent. Maybe less.

Aesir kept his breathing steady. His expression empty.

Waited.

---

The pacer slowed. The tunnel widened. He could hear voices ahead. A checkpoint. Uwell guards confirming transit.

The Damascus soldiers called out codes. Papers exchanged.

The draft beast ahead snorted. Tired.

Aesir felt it now. The barest tingle beneath his skin. The electromagnetic current trying to wake through the haze.

Not much. Barely functional.

But he had to try.

He waited until the pacer started moving again. Until the checkpoint was behind them and the tunnel narrowed.

Then he closed his eyes. Focused through the fog.

Felt for the metal of his chains.

---

The iron heated.

Slowly. Barely. Enough to weaken one rivet.

He pulled. Muscles straining.

The chain gave. Not a clean break. A desperate tear.

---

The guards didn't notice.

Still talking. Voices low. The scarred one explaining something about Utilta's defenses.

Aesir waited.

Felt for the lock mechanism. Hands still bound behind him. Wrists raw from the rope.

He focused through the fog. The drug still dulling everything.

Electromagnetic current. Weak. Stuttering. But there.

The lock heated. Barely. Just enough.

He pulled his shoulder against the cage door.

It gave.

Silent.

---

The Damascus soldiers kept talking.

Aesir moved.

Shoulder-first into the scarred soldier's back.

The man pitched forward. Hit the pacer's edge. Tried to turn.

Aesir drove his knee into the man's ribs. Felt something give.

The younger soldier shouted. Reached for his blade.

Aesir headbutted him.

The soldier's nose shattered. Blood everywhere. He staggered back.

His foot caught the cage door.

Fell hard.

---

The scarred soldier recovered. Drew his blade.

Aesir ducked. Used the cage bars for leverage. Kicked out with both feet.

Caught the guard in the chest.

The man flew off the pacer. Hit the tunnel floor.

Didn't get up.

---

The younger soldier was on the ground. Bleeding. Dazed.

Aesir grabbed the cage bars. Pulled himself to the front. Hands still tied.

He focused. Shoved a pulse of energy into the pacer's hover mechanism.

It sputtered. Overloaded.

The draft beast panicked. Felt the energy surge. Bolted.

The pacer lurched.

The younger soldier scrambled for purchase. Grabbed the edge.

Too late.

The pacer tilted. Scraped the tunnel wall. Sparks flying.

Aesir jumped.

Hit the ground rolling. Hands tied behind him. Couldn't break the fall properly.

Pain shot through his shoulder. His ribs. Everywhere.

Behind him, the pacer crashed. Metal screaming against stone.

The Uwell guards in back shouted. Scrambling.

Aesir forced himself up.

Ran.

Hands still bound. Vision swimming. Blood running down his face.

But free.

For now.

---

The tunnels twisted.

Aesir ran. Or tried to. His legs felt wrong. Heavy. The world tilted with every step.

He'd never been to Uwell. Didn't know the layout. The roads. The districts.

Stone corridors branched in three directions. Then four. Then more.

A labyrinth.

He chose left. Then right. Then left again.

Lost.

His shoulder throbbed. His ribs screamed. Blood ran down his temple from where he'd hit the ground.

The drug in his system made everything blur. Sounds echoed strangely. Shadows moved when they shouldn't.

He stumbled into a wider road. Underground. Carved from living rock. Glowstone lanterns casting everything in dim orange light.

Empty.

No. Not empty.

Footsteps.

---

Aesir turned.

Four men. Uwell uniforms. City guard.

He tried to raise his hands. Tried to summon power.

Nothing came.

The tall one in front smiled.

"Well. What do we have here?"

Four men. Uwell uniforms. City guard. Patrol.

The one in front was tall. Broad. His face was angular. Sharp. Eyes cold and amused.

"Well," the man said. Voice smooth. Almost friendly. "What do we have here?"

---

Aesir tried to straighten. Raised his hands.

No energy came. Just the faint ghost of what should be there.

"Stay back," he said. Voice slurred.

The tall man—Borum—smiled. "Oh, I don't think so."

He gestured to two of his men. "Take him."

---

The guards moved.

Aesir met the first one.

Tried to block. Too slow. The punch caught his ribs.

He gasped. Swung back. Missed.

The second guard came in low. Swept his legs.

Aesir hit the ground. Hard.

Everything spun.

He tried to push up. A boot caught him in the side. He rolled.

The guards were on him. Professional. Controlled.

He wasn't a threat. Just a half-conscious fugitive.

---

Borum watched. Chuckling.

His men worked efficiently. One held Aesir's arms. The other landed measured strikes.

Aesir tried to fight back. Managed one weak hit. It barely connected.

The guard didn't even flinch.

Aesir's vision darkened at the edges. The drug pulling him under. His body failing.

He couldn't focus. Couldn't think.

Just pain and fog.

---

Borum's smile faded. Boredom settling in.

This wasn't even interesting.

"Enough."

He stepped forward. Casual. Unhurried.

Aesir saw him coming through the haze. Tried to move.

Couldn't.

Borum's fist connected with Aesir's jaw.

Everything went white.

Then black.

---

Aesir hit the ground. Didn't move.

Borum looked down at him. Shook his head.

"That's the infamous Indexto boy? The threat Damascus was so concerned about?"

He nudged Aesir's ribs with his boot. No response.

"Drugged, disoriented, half-dead already." Borum snorted. "Easier than the drunks we pull from the third district taverns."

One of his men laughed.

Borum gestured dismissively. "Bind him. Take him to the main city. Cell block three. Madam Nidus will want to see what all the fuss was about."

The guards moved. Tied Aesir's hands. His feet. Lifted him like cargo.

Borum watched for another moment. Then turned.

"I have more serious matters to attend to."

He walked away.

Left Aesir to the darkness.

To the cell waiting in the buried heart of Uwell.

Where nightmares would find him.

---
