# CHAPTER ELEVEN
## Voices in the Dark

The clearing held forty drawn blades and two kinds of waiting.

One was the waiting of soldiers. The survey, pressed into a square, watching the grey green shapes at the treeline with the flat readiness of men who'd been herded here and knew it.

The other was worse.

It was the waiting of a thing that had set a table and now sat back to watch what came to eat at it.

The man in clean clothes stood at the far edge of the firelight. Did not move. Did not speak again. Simply watched Aesir with the warm and terrible patience of someone who'd bought time in bulk and intended to spend it slowly.

At the treeline behind him, the other shapes held their own silence. Not his soldiers. Something else. Patient and separate and utterly still.

Two ledgers, Aesir thought again. Both opened on the same page.

---

Bandar's voice came low and level from the center of the square.

"Formation holds. Nobody moves until I say the word. We are not fighting our way out of this one. We are *thinking* our way out."

The general's eyes never left the man in clean clothes. Calculating. Reading the angles the way he'd read a thousand ambushes before this one.

But this was not a thousand ambushes. This was something new.

And Aesir could see, in the set of Bandar's shoulders, that the old soldier knew it.

---

The man in clean clothes tilted his head slightly. As though listening to something no one else could hear.

"A dilemma," he said at last, his voice carrying easily across the clearing. Not loud. Didn't need to be. "Forty good men. Four clever children. One very patient fire. And no good roads out of the room."

He paused.

"I could take you all, of course. But that would be wasteful. Inefficient. And I have never been a wasteful man."

His eyes found Aesir's again. Held.

"So. A proposal. One of you walks over here, of his own will, and sits down beside this fire. The rest walk home. Your emperor gets his survey. Your families get their sons. And I get what I came for."

The silence that followed was the kind that bends things.

"No trade," Bandar said flatly. "We leave together or we don't leave."

The man smiled. It didn't touch his eyes.

"General Bandar Sulph. I was told you'd say that. Admirable. Doomed, but admirable." He glanced past Bandar, to where Naska stood with an arrow half drawn. "Your daughter has her mother's eyes. Pity she'll likely have her mother's end if you make this difficult."

Bandar went very still.

The kind of still that comes right before violence.

---

And that was when Aesir understood the shape of it.

The man didn't want a fight. Wanted a *choice.* Wanted Aesir to step forward. To make it clean. To make it his.

Because a boy taken by force is a crime. A boy who walks into the dark of his own will is a mystery. And mysteries, Aesir was beginning to learn, were the kind of story that ate cities from the inside.

He took a breath. Felt Naska's eyes on him without looking. Felt Askan's hand tighten on the glaive.

Felt the weight of forty lives standing between him and the thing that had been hunting him since the warehouse. Since before the warehouse. Since the moment the first mark appeared on his skin in a city that should have had no door out.

"Aesir," Naska breathed. "Don't."

But he was already moving.

Not toward the fire. The other way.

---

"Scatter formation," he said, loud enough for the square to hear. "On my mark. North by the drill. Bandar leads. I'll draw them off."

"*No,*" Bandar snapped. "That is not the play. We hold and we—"

The rest of the sentence died as the first shapes moved at the treeline.

Not the grey green soldiers. The others. The ones that had been standing outside the light.

They came forward slowly. Into the firelight.

And every man in the clearing saw, in the same cold instant, that the thing they'd been trying to map for five days was not one enemy.

It was two.

---

The grey green soldiers were still human. You could see it in the way they held their weapons. In the rhythm of their breath.

These were not.

They moved wrong. Bent wrong. Their limbs too long, their joints hinged in places joints did not hinge. Skin grey and slick, stretched tight over frames that had been something else once and were now only themselves.

Beastmen.

Not the degraded raiders from the northern wastes. These were older. Purer. The kind the stories said died out in the last war.

The stories, Aesir understood with a sick lurch, had been wrong about a great many things.

---

The man in clean clothes sighed. As though disappointed.

"I had hoped we'd do this the civilized way," he said. Raised one gloved hand.

The Beastmen surged forward.

"RUN!" Bandar roared, and the square broke.

It broke the way drilled men break under pressure—not into chaos, but into vectors. North by the trees, two files peeling off left and right, covering fire as they moved, the whole formation compressing and flowing like water through a crack.

Aesir ran with them. Naska at his shoulder. Askan a pace behind. The forest swallowed them in three strides and the firelight fell away and there was only the green dark and the sound of things crashing through it behind them.

---

They ran for what felt like hours and was probably minutes.

The trees bent distance. Made it a liar. Every time Aesir thought they'd put ground between themselves and the clearing, he'd hear the wet snarl of a Beastman far too close, and the running would start again.

Bandar kept them together as long as he could. Calling the turns. Reading the slopes. But the forest had other ideas, and men at a dead run in the dark do not stay together no matter how good their drill.

The files split. Split again.

By the time Aesir registered that Askan's footfalls had faded somewhere to the left and Bandar's voice was gone entirely, it was only him and Naska, their breath ragged, their boots skidding on wet needles, the night closing like a fist.

---

She caught his arm. Pulled him sideways into a hollow beneath a fallen tree.

They dropped, pressed flat, chests heaving.

The sounds of pursuit passed them. Kept going. Faded.

Silence came back, tenanted and wrong.

They lay there in the dark, shoulder to shoulder, waiting for their hearts to slow.

Aesir could feel the warmth of her against his side. Could hear the small catch in her breath that meant she was frightened and would die before saying so.

"We're separated," she whispered after a long moment.

"I know."

"Bandar will rally. Regroup north of the dig site. Standard dispersal protocol."

"I know."

She was quiet a beat.

"So why aren't we moving?"

---

He didn't answer right away.

Because the truth was complicated and simple at once.

They were alone. For the first time in three years of threading the dark together, nobody was watching. No drill. No protocol. No Askan reading the room with his strategist's eyes or Bandar counting the intervals.

Just the two of them and the thing they'd been circling since the tower.

"We should move," he said.

"We should," she agreed.

Neither of them moved.

---

The forest breathed around them. Slow. Patient. Utterly indifferent to the forty small lives running through it.

"Aesir."

"Yeah."

"If we don't make it out of this—"

"Don't."

"—I need you to know—"

"*Naska.*"

She turned her head. In the dark he could barely see her face. Just the shape of it. The faint gleam of her eyes.

"I'm not saying it because I think we're dying," she said quietly. "I'm saying it because I've been carrying it for two years and I'm tired."

His heart did something complicated against his ribs.

"Don't," he said again, but the word had no strength in it.

"I love you," she said. Simple. Flat. The way she said everything that mattered. "Have since the warehouse. Maybe before. I know it's the worst possible time. I know we're in the middle of a forest with things hunting us. I know you're about to tell me all the reasons it's a bad idea."

She paused.

"But I needed you to hear it once. While I could still say it."

---

The silence that followed was the kind that remakes the world.

Every clever deflection he'd ever built went to ash in his mouth.

Every reason it was impossible—and there were a hundred of them—rang hollow against the plain truth she'd just handed him.

He'd known. Of course he'd known. You don't spend three years fighting beside someone, learning the shape of their silences, without knowing.

But knowing and *hearing* were two different countries.

And hearing it, in the dark, with death a few trees over, broke something in him that had been holding for too long.

---

"I know," he said.

She went still.

"You *know?*"

"I've known since the tower. Maybe before." He turned toward her, close enough now that he could feel her breath. "I didn't say anything because I thought if I ignored it long enough it would stop being true."

"Did it work?"

"No."

"Good."

She closed the last few inches.

---

The kiss was not the first kiss of the stories. Not soft. Not tentative.

It was three years of circling each other, of not saying, of standing at the fire's edge and never stepping in, all spending itself at once.

Her hand found the back of his neck. His found her waist. The hollow beneath the fallen tree became the only warm place in a cold forest, and for the length of a held breath the thing hunting them did not matter, the survey did not matter, the whole weight of what waited back in the sealed city did not matter.

There was only this.

Only her.

---

And then the night changed.

It changed the way nights change when you've been watched for longer than you knew.

A sound. Small. Behind them.

The whisper of a bowstring settling.

Aesir's body knew before his mind did. The way it always knew.

He twisted, pulling Naska down, his mouth opening to shout a warning—

The arrow took her in the side of the neck.

---

She made a sound. Small. Surprised.

Her hand came up to the shaft. Touched it. As though she couldn't quite believe it was there.

Aesir caught her as she fell.

"No no no no—"

Blood. Too much blood. Hot against his hands. The arrow had gone clean through. Punched out the other side.

She looked up at him, her eyes very wide, her lips forming a word she didn't have the air for.

"Stay with me," he said, and his voice cracked. "Naska, stay with me, I can—"

But he didn't know what he could do. Didn't know anything except that the warmth was leaving her and he couldn't stop it and the forest around them was full of moving shapes now, closing in, and there was nowhere left to run.

---

He looked up.

The grey green soldiers stepped into the clearing. A dozen of them. Weapons trained. Professional. Patient.

And behind them, walking with the same unhurried certainty he'd had beside the fire, the man in clean clothes.

He looked down at Naska. At Aesir kneeling beside her with her blood on his hands.

Smiled slightly.

"There we are," he said. "I was beginning to think you'd make me work for it."

---

Aesir's vision tunneled. The light was rising under his skin without his permission. Gold going to white. His whole body answering a rage he had no name for.

The man saw it. Read it. Did not look afraid.

"That's the thing I've been waiting to see," he said quietly. "Go ahead. Show me what you are."

Aesir pulled.

Pulled the way he'd pulled in the cellar under Aelayla's lamp. Reached down into the furnace he'd been taught to bank and tore every door off it at once.

The light came up like a dawn.

---

It rose through his arms, his chest, his throat. Gold turning white turning something past white. The air around him shimmered. Leaves curled and blackened.

The grey green soldiers stepped back. One of them made a small frightened sound.

The man in clean clothes did not step back.

"Yes," he breathed. "There you are."

Aesir let it go.

The wave that tore out of him was not fire. Not light. Something else. Something the air itself wasn't prepared for.

It caught three of the soldiers and threw them backward into the trees. Caught the others and put them on the ground. Stripped bark from trunks and sent needles scattering in a hot wind that tasted like ozone and burning copper.

The clearing went white. Then dark. Then white again.

When the light finally died, Aesir was on his hands and knees, gasping, emptied, with nothing left.

---

The man in clean clothes stood exactly where he'd been.

A faint shimmer hung in the air around him. A barrier of some kind. Old Eltech. The kind that predated the empire.

He looked unharmed. Unsurprised.

"Better than I'd hoped," he said quietly.

He gestured. The soldiers who could still move closed in.

Aesir tried to rise. Couldn't. His body was done. The furnace was ash.

Naska lay beside him, so still, her blood pooling dark on the needles.

He reached for her hand. Found it. Held it.

"I'm sorry," he whispered.

She didn't answer.

---

The man in clean clothes knelt. Not close. Careful. As though Aesir were a fire that might catch again.

"You'll come with me now," he said. Not a question. "There are people who need to see what you are. Who have been waiting a very long time for something like you to surface."

He paused.

"The girl will be found. Probably. Your people are competent. But you won't be here to see it."

Aesir tried to speak. His mouth wouldn't work.

The last thing he saw before the blow came down was Naska's face, pale in the dark, her eyes half open, her chest rising once, shallow, and then not rising, and then the world folding into black.

---

**✦✦✦**

When the survey found her three hours later, she was cold.

Bandar dropped beside her. Put his ear to her mouth. Felt for a pulse at her throat with shaking hands.

Found one. Faint. Failing.

"MEDIC!" he roared, and his voice broke on the word.

They carried her north through the forest at a dead run, her father's cloak wrapped tight to slow the bleeding, four men taking turns because she could not be set down, because every second was one she did not have to spare.

By the time they reached the wagons her lips were blue.

By the time they reached Utilta's walls her pulse was a thread.

She lived.

Barely.

Woke six days later in a white room with no memory of how she'd gotten there and a bandage at her throat and her father weeping beside the bed.

"Where's Aesir?" were the first words out of her mouth.

Bandar didn't answer.

Couldn't.

The silence told her everything.

---

**✦✦✦**

Mandu received the news in his study.

A rider. Palace seal. Three lines.

*Survey ambushed. Seventeen casualties. Your son missing, presumed taken. Bandar's daughter critical. Column returning.*

The paper slipped from his hands.

Aelayla found him an hour later, still standing at the window, looking south, his face very old.

"They have him," was all he said.

She did not ask who *they* were. Did not need to.

She crossed the room. Took his hand. Held it the way she'd held it thirty years ago when they'd buried his brother.

Outside, the city went on sleeping. Certain of its walls.

Unaware that a boy who could burn had just been taken beyond them.

And that the thing that had taken him was about to learn what a mistake that was.

---

**END OF CHAPTER ELEVEN**
