# CHAPTER SEVEN
## The Sealed Door

Once, the green door in the Ninth had opened before Aesir reached it.

Lysse heard everything. Read his step in the alley the way a blind woman reads a room she knows, by the small shifts in how air moves around a body it recognizes. So that by the time his knuckles rose to knock, the latch had already lifted and the door swung back on lamplight and pipe smoke and the low broken music of people with nowhere better in all the world to be.

That was once.

For three weeks now the door had stayed shut. A flat green refusal set in a wall that wept rust.

Aesir had finally stopped walking down that alley to watch it fail to open, because there's a point past which returning to a closed door ceases to be hope and becomes instead a kind of begging.

And there was too much of his father in Aesir to beg a door.

---

The fire had done precisely what Lysse warned it would do.

Soldiers came first. They always come first and always come loudest. Swept the Ninth for show. Splintered a few doors. Rearranged a few faces. Marched home able to report the matter well in hand.

Then, once the noise had guttered out, came the second sweep. The quiet one. The men Aesir had actually cost money moving through the district with no appetite for show at all. Only for accounts.

And by the time that second tide had drawn back out, half the doorways that once tipped their chins to him had taught themselves a simpler sum:

That the safest thing to do with a boy who calls the light down into the dark is to have never learned his name.

---

It was not cruelty.

Aesir had wanted it to be cruelty, because cruelty can at least be hated cleanly.

It was only arithmetic.

He had opened two dozen cages and made two thousand lives more dangerous to live beside. The Ninth had done its arithmetic and closed its ledger against him.

There was no villain anywhere in it.

Which was the worst of the whole thing.

---

He should've stopped going down there at all.

Knew it the way you know a wound you cannot stop pressing.

Most nights he heard the argument against it the whole way down through the terraces, spoken in Aelayla's voice, patient and tired.

Most nights he went regardless.

Because the thing Naska had breathed to him in that stairwell had lodged somewhere beneath reason and begun, slowly, to work its way toward bone.

*That ink does not exist in this city. It came from outside.*

Somewhere inside a city sworn shut past all living memory, a door that could not open was opening. Things were passing through it.

The not knowing of it had set a small relentless itch in a part of Aesir he could not have pointed to. A part that had itched, he was beginning to suspect, for as long as he had drawn breath, without his ever once daring to scratch it.

---

And there was the other thing. The thing he had confessed to no one, because to say it aloud would be to make it real.

He was being followed.

Not the ordinary appraisal of the low city, where every eye weighs every other out of long habit and means nothing by it.

This was cleaner than that. Newer.

A face at the rim of a market three days running. Well fed and unremarkable. Always, in the half instant it took him to turn his head, already studying something else.

He told himself the fire had left him flinching at shadows.

He was fifteen, and gifted past accounting, and had spent his short life as the most dangerous thing in nearly every room he entered.

And that is its own slow poison, that kind of certainty. It persuades a boy that danger will always come at him from the front, wearing a face, granting him the time to set his feet.

---

At home, the great house had begun to move carefully around him. The way a household moves around a sickroom no one will name.

Raijeen had taken to being awake whenever Aesir came in over the wall. He'd always half done that, keeping his brother's hours out of some private arithmetic of love.

But there was a new weight in it now. A watchfulness turned strangely inward, as though Raijeen were reading a line of script off Aesir's skin that Aesir himself could not find in any glass.

And Aelayla had stopped telling him not to go.

That frightened him more than the shut doors and the following faces together, because a person stops issuing warnings for only one of two reasons: because they have given you up for lost, or because the thing they spent years warning you against has already come quietly through the door and is standing, even now, in the room.

---

So in the end there was a single door left in the whole low city that might still open to him.

And it belonged to the one person down there furious with him for the right reasons.

Aesir went looking for Naska.

---

**✦✦✦**

Finding Naska was never a thing you accomplished. It was a thing she permitted, and only once she'd judged the permitting to cost her less than the refusing.

Aesir left word at the two drops she still trusted him with.

On the second night a boy he'd never seen pressed a folded scrap into his palm without breaking stride. Was three shapes deep into the crowd before Aesir had it open.

An address in the Waterworks. A time.

And beneath them, in a slanting hand he'd have known anywhere: *come alone, idiot.*

---

The Waterworks were their own drowned country. A sunken warren where old cisterns sweated through masonry the way a cold glass sweats in a warm hand. The air lay on the tongue like rust and cold stone.

He found her in the gutted shell of a pumphouse that had moved no water in a lifetime. Perched on a fallen roof beam with her bow unstrung across her knees.

She didn't lift her head when he came through the broken arch.

Which was exactly how he knew she'd marked his approach a hundred paces back and simply declined to grant it the courtesy of surprise.

"You look terrible," she said.

"Slept badly."

"Good." She still hadn't looked up. "I've slept worse. Would sting to think I was the only one."

---

He lowered himself onto the lip of a dry channel across from her.

For a while the two of them only held the silence. Turning it slowly over. Neither reaching for the thing that mattered.

That circling had been a craft they built together in the weeks before the fire. The not saying. The patient orbit around the truth.

And, God help him, he had missed it.

He was half angry at how much he'd missed it. This one person in all the sunless city who met him blow for blow and never once looked at him the way the others had lately begun to look, as though he were a story that had not yet made up its mind how it would end.

---

"You'll want to know why I called you down here," she said at last.

When she raised her eyes to him they held none of the anger he'd braced against.

What they held was worse.

They were careful.

"It isn't to shout at you. I did that already, at a wall, and the wall took it well. It's to tell you one thing, once, plainly. Then to tell you to leave the city. Then to watch you ignore me. But at least it will have been said."

"Naska."

"There is a word out."

She rode straight over him. Flat and quick. The way a person says a thing they've resolved to say before their nerve can talk them down.

"In the deep places. The ones that sit beneath the ones you think you know. Someone up in the clean light is asking after a boy. Young. Fights like something he has no business being. Red in his hair."

She let the shape of it settle into the cold between them.

"And they are not asking the way soldiers ask, loud and stupid and hoping. They are asking the way men ask who already own the answer and are only pricing it."

---

Something in Aesir, the cold clean thing that always moved a half step ahead of his fear, went utterly still.

He had felt those eyes. Three days running he'd told himself they were nothing.

And here was Naska, who had never once in her life mistaken a something for a nothing, laying it before him like a body on a table.

The floor of the night seemed to tilt a degree beneath his boots.

"The man from the warehouse," he said quietly. "The one in the good clothes."

"I don't know his name. I know he is high. Higher than anything I've ever brushed against and lived to describe. The word reached me down through four sets of hands. Every set who passed it along was more afraid than the last."

She leaned toward him. The old beam groaned.

"Aesir. Hear what I'm telling you. You didn't merely irritate some fight ring boss with more coin than sense. You put your face in front of something that lives up in the clean air. It turned and looked back at you. Now it's reaching down into the dark to close its hand."

She paused.

"People do not come back up from being reached for by a thing like that. They simply stop being anywhere at all."

---

"Then I have to know what it is before its hand closes."

He heard exactly how the words sounded. Reckless and young. Found he could not make himself care.

"Naska, that ink came from outside. A city sworn shut past all memory, and something strolls in and out through its wall as though the stone were a hanging curtain. Whoever is asking after me is the same whoever that opens that door. If I run now, I run blind, and I run forever, from a thing I never once saw the face of."

He paused.

"I can't live that way. I would rather it took me with my eyes open, looking straight at it."

---

She held his gaze long.

He watched a dozen answers rise behind her eyes and be set, one by one, back down.

What she chose in the end came out low.

"You know the difference between brave and stupid? Only one thing. The outcome. That's the whole of the difference. Nobody is permitted to know which one you were until it's already over."

She rose. Strung the bow in a single unhurried motion that laid the argument to rest before her mouth confirmed it.

"The outside goods move down through the old city. Under the Waterworks. Under all of it. There are doors down there that were sealed shut before anyone up top had so much as a name for the place. Some of them are standing open tonight."

She paused.

"I found the way in a week ago. Haven't once gone down alone. Because I am reckless, not suicidal."

A breath. Something beneath the hardness gave a little.

"And I'm not about to let you go down alone either. Not because you couldn't manage it. Because if that thing reaches for you in the dark, I mean to be standing near enough to see its face when it does."

---

**✦✦✦**

They went down through a broken sluice gate Naska had found weeks before, into a stairwell that had plainly never been cut with human comfort in mind.

With every turning of the steps the city grew older around them.

Aesir had believed he understood the word *old.*

He'd been raised among old things, in a lord's house where the furniture had outlived dynasties. Had spent a year in the low city persuading himself its filth was ancient.

This was something else entirely.

---

The stone down here had not been laid so much as grown. Poured into being without a single seam.

Across its faces ran carvings his eye kept sliding off of. Not for any faintness in the lines but because they answered to some logic his mind owned no shelf to hold.

To rest his gaze on them too long was to feel the small sick lurch of a foot come down on a stair built to the wrong height. The body pitching after a ground that was not where it should have been.

*It was the very way, Aelayla said, that the surface world owned no shelf to hold him.*

---

Doorways opened at heights and widths that answered to nothing he could name.

Long corridors ran on toward purposes their makers had clearly thought too self evident to label, the way a man hangs no sign upon his own front step.

A people had lived here once. Wholly certain of themselves and their place beneath the sky.

Then the city above had been raised upon their sleeping faces. Had forgotten them so thoroughly, so completely, that it had begun at last to call the forgetting by the name of history.

---

And then there were the doors.

These were the thing Naska had descended to show him and had found no words to carry back up into the light.

Here and there, set deep in the grown stone, stood a door of altogether different making. Its metal gone the green black that only vast age lays upon metal. Sealed by mechanisms that wore no keyholes because no key had ever been meant to trouble them.

Most remained shut. Looked to have stood shut since before the first war any living tongue could name.

But a few of them stood open.

Not broken. Not forced. *Opened.* Their impossible locks simply undone, as though whoever had passed through carried in his mouth the very word the makers left sleeping in the works.

From the black beyond each open door came a slow exhalation of cold moving air. Air with somewhere it needed to be. Brushing past them with the flat indifference of a draught slipping under a door in a house you'd believed was empty.

---

Aesir stopped before one of them. Could not make his feet go on.

Because the old itch beneath his life had rolled over at last and fixed its whole attention upon him.

Though he owned no memory he was permitted to reach, some wordless animal stratum of him recognized the shape of that doorway. Knew it the way the body knows a face from before it could speak.

Because he had come up once, out of a door exactly like this one, into a life no one would ever fully explain to him.

---

They had gone perhaps a hundred paces beyond the open door when Naska's hand closed hard around his wrist and stopped him where he stood.

A half beat behind her sharper ears he heard it too:

Bootfalls. More than two pairs. Ahead of them and behind them at once.

Unhurried.

"Down here," Naska breathed, and there was no fear left in the words at all, only a flat and dreadful readiness, "no one walks unhurried unless he already knows exactly where you are."

---

They stepped out of the dark on both sides at once.

They were not fight ring muscle. Aesir saw that in the first instant.

They were built as the two who had cornered Naska in the ring were built. Eltech ridged in pale seams beneath the skin. But where those had been crude, these were refined. Quiet. Exact.

The whole difference between a butcher's cleaver and a surgeon's blade.

They came on with the untroubled economy of water finding the one crack in a wall. Men who had done this a hundred times. Been paid handsomely for every one. Expected no difficulty in the hundred and first.

The one at their head let his gaze come to rest on Aesir with a small flicker of something almost like recognition.

Spoke, not to them but past them, to his own.

"That's him. Take the boy whole. The girl is nothing."

---

What came next happened in the older language Aesir's body spoke without ever asking his leave.

He had the first man off his feet before the order was fully out of the leader's mouth. Moving the way he alone moved. Quicker than his lean frame had any right to promise. His own strength and nothing bought or bolted on.

The sheer wrongness of that speed bought them a breath of stunned hesitation.

But there were five. Every one of them engineered for precisely this. The corridor was tight as a throat.

In the crush of it, one of them got a hand around Naska's arm.

---

That was the thing that undid him.

Afterward, whenever he tried to lay the night down in its proper order, that was the one seam he could never quite step across:

The sight of that manufactured hand closing on Naska's arm. The sound she tore out of the dark, more fury than fear. The sudden sickening drop of the whole floor of himself into a room he had never before known was down there.

---

The warmth came up his arms the way a spark walks a fuse.

Unhurried. Certain. Utterly beyond calling back.

He did not understand it. There was no room in the moment to understand anything.

He felt it begin somewhere in the marrow and pour outward. A heat that was neither effort nor terror though it came riding in on both.

All at once the cramped black corridor turned, impossibly, blue.

---

The light was coming out of him.

In the half second he could spare to look down he saw his own forearms alight from within. Cold fire running the lines beneath the skin. Tracing patterns he had never once seen and yet knew the way your hand knows the dark shape of a room you were a child in.

Each of them pulsing slow and bright with the hammer of his heart.

Out of his throat came a sound he had not chosen to make.

---

The men felt it before their minds could catch up to it.

The one who had hold of Naska flung her arm away as though the flesh beneath his fingers had turned to open flame. Staggered back.

Every scrap of that untroubled economy drained out of his face and left behind it the oldest expression a face can wear:

The animal horror of a thing that should not, by any law the world had ever taught him, be.

"What is that," another of them said, and his voice broke clean in half on the word. "What in every cold hell is that."

---

And Naska, loosed, staring at Aesir across the blue drenched dark with her bow half raised and wholly forgotten in her hands, said nothing whatsoever.

Because there was nothing in any language she owned to say.

Because she was watching the laws of the world come apart for the second time in her life, and this time no amount of willing it would let her shrug it away.

---

Aesir had no idea what he was doing.

That was the true horror of it. Deeper than the men. Deeper than the hunt. Deeper than anything that wore a face.

His own body had unsealed a door he'd never known was set into him. Light was flooding up out of it. He could no sooner have willed it shut than willed his eyes shut against a scream already leaving his mouth.

But the men could not see that he was helpless.

To them he was simply a boy who had, between one breath and the next, ceased to be quarry and become an omen.

And an omen met in the deep dark, under orders to be taken whole, with no man among them any longer certain what *whole* would even mean, is not a thing that anyone reaches for.

---

"We were not paid for this," the leader said.

It left him almost as a prayer. He was already giving ground.

They went.

It was not quite a rout. They were professionals to the marrow even in their fear. But they went, folding back into the dark that had birthed them.

The last of them turned once at the corridor's mouth to look back at the burning boy.

Aesir would carry that look a long while.

Because it was not the look a man gives a target.

It was the look a man gives a thing he knows he will have to describe to someone above him, and be named a liar for, and describe again.

---

And then Naska was before him. Close. Her hands lifted but not landing. The way you come at a fire, or at a horse gone wild in the dark.

She was saying his name. Over and over. Low and level.

"Aesir. Aesir. Breathe. You have to breathe. Whatever this is, it is yours. It came up out of you. That means it answers to you. So breathe, and call it back down. Aesir."

---

He never learned how she came to know that.

It would be a long time before he understood that she had not known it at all. That she had reached blind into the dark of the moment for the truest thing she could find.

And that the thing had simply happened to be true.

He breathed. It cost him.

By slow degrees, the way the flush drains from a face, the light sank back down into whatever deep place it lived.

The ordinary dark closed over them again.

He was only a boy once more. Shaking. Deep in a dead city. With the girl he had never once let himself admit he loved staring at him as though she had never in her life laid eyes on him before.

Which, in the way that counted most, she had not.

---

**✦✦✦**

For a long while neither of them spoke.

The dead city did not mind the silence. It had kept far longer ones. Had learned across its centuries the vast, unhurried patience of stone.

"So," Naska said at last.

Her voice was not steady. She was laboring to make it steady. The labor showed in every word.

He loved her helplessly for the labor.

"That's new."

A laugh broke out of him. Cracked and terrible and entirely real.

"Is it. I hadn't noticed."

"Don't."

But there was no edge left in it.

She sat down close beside him. Her shoulder against his. The two of them facing together the dark they would soon have to walk back through.

For a time she only breathed, deliberately, matching her breath to his until both of them had come down out of the worst of it.

Then, quietly, her eyes on the black ahead and not on him:

"I knew you weren't ordinary. I'd made my peace with strong. Strong, I could carry. But this."

The sentence failed her. She began it again.

"What are you, Aesir?"

---

"I don't know."

Saying it aloud, there in the dark, to her, he understood it for the truest and most frightening sentence he had ever let leave his mouth.

Because for once it was not a dodge or a deflection. He genuinely, wholly did not know.

His whole life he had been the thing in the room that other things were right to fear.

Tonight, at last, he had met the thing inside himself that he could neither understand nor command.

And it had been lying there within him the entire time. Sleeping behind a sealed door of his own. Waiting only upon a word he had spoken tonight without ever knowing it was his to speak.

---

And Aelayla's voice rose in him then, out of a hundred lamplit evenings he had let wash half heard around his ears.

*Strength no one can account for makes people curious. Be careful it stays a thing you would be willing to explain to me, while it is still small enough to be explained.*

He had always assumed she meant the fighting.

He understood now, with a slow and sickening clarity, that she had never once meant the fighting.

She had known. Whatever the full shape of the thing she carried, she had known there was more waiting beneath the surface of him.

For years she had been trying, gently, patiently, by candlelight, to ready him for a night precisely like this one.

And he had grinned his practiced grin and named her a worrier and slipped out through the window into the dark.

The shame of that settled over him colder and heavier than any fear the night had yet handed him.

---

They climbed back up out of the buried city as the sky above the vents was beginning, grudgingly, to consider grey.

They parted at the broken mouth of the Waterworks with almost nothing said between them, because everything that had ever needed saying had already been said in a dead stone hall by the light of a fire that came out of his own arms.

Any words spoken up here in the ordinary dark could only shrink it.

---

"Go home," she told him. "Sleep, if you can find it. Don't come looking for me for a few days. I need to think. You need badly to not be seen. For now those two things live easier apart."

But she caught the edge of his sleeve as he turned to go. Held it a moment. Kept her eyes fixed on the middle distance rather than on his face.

"And Aesir. Wherever those men run to tonight. By this time tomorrow, that man in his good clean clothes is going to know that the boy he has been asking after does not merely fight strange."

She let the sleeve go.

"You lit up a room tonight, in front of exactly the wrong eyes. That is not the kind of thing a person is ever allowed to take back."

---

He came back over the garden wall as the grey came up, the way he had come over it a hundred times. Without a sound and without any apparent effort.

But tonight his hands were not entirely steady on the old stone. The drop that had never in his life cost him a single thought seemed to fall away much farther beneath him than it ever had.

When he reached the window at the end of the east hall it stood already open, the way it always stood open.

Because there was always someone who left it open for him.

---

Raijeen was standing beside it.

He was not pretending to have risen for a cup of water. Was not pretending anything at all.

He simply stood there in the blue hush of the hour in his nightclothes, as awake as he ever was, and looked at his brother.

Aesir set himself for the questions. The *where have you been.* The *what happened to you.* The *you look as though you have been walking with the dead.*

None of them came.

Raijeen only looked at him. Steadily. The way a man looks at a page he has already read to its end.

---

And Aesir understood, with a lurch that went straight through the floor of him, that his brother knew.

Not the particulars. But the shape of it.

Raijeen had been watching him for weeks with that strange inward weight, reading something written just under his skin.

Tonight, at last, whatever it was had been given a name.

The two of them stood there in the dark, in the very same breath, each too afraid to be the one who spoke it.

---

"You're up," Aesir said.

It came out of him wrong. Thin and unsteady.

"I'm always up," Raijeen said.

Then, very softly, the single thing either of them would allow himself. The sentence with all the rest of it folded up small and hidden inside:

"You should sleep. You look like you're carrying something."

A beat. Then, quieter still:

"You don't have to carry it alone. You know that. Whatever it is."

---

And Aesir, who had learned only hours before that he had been carrying it alone his entire life, that it ran in the very blood of him, that a door had swung open inside him tonight which he had no idea in all the world how to close again, gave the one answer he was able to give to the one person who had never once, in all their strange shared life, let him fall.

"I know," he said.

They both understood it for a lie. They both let the lie stand between them untouched.

Because there are nights when the kindest thing two brothers can do for one another is to agree, without ever saying so, to be afraid apart until the morning comes.

---

**✦✦✦**

Below the sleeping house, the city stirred toward morning. Sure of itself. Sure of its walls. Sure past all questioning that nothing came into it from the outside.

Out in that certain city, in a high room where the lamps burned clean and the air had never once carried smoke, a frightened man was climbing a stair he'd have given a great deal not to climb.

To lay before a calm one a thing he'd have given a great deal more not to say:

That the boy in the deep city had not fought like a boy at all.

That he had burned. Cold, and blue, and lit from somewhere far inside himself.

Like nothing any of them had ever been handed a word to hold.

---

The calm man would not be frightened.

That, in the end, was the whole of the danger. The one true difference between the two of them.

He had marked Aesir's face across a burning warehouse and gone quietly home to wait. Patient as a sealed door in the dark.

He had not spent those weeks waiting to hear that the boy was strong.

Strength was cheap. The deep city bred strong men the way damp breeds mould, in every unwatched corner, given nothing but time and dark.

He had been waiting, all along, to be told that the boy carried a light inside him.

---

Now he had been told.

And so the hunt, which for three long weeks had been only a question drifting down through frightened hands, became, in the space of one man's unwilling confession, something else entirely.

It became a certainty.

And certainties, unlike questions, have names.
