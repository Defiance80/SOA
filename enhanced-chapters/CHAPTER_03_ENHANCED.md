# CHAPTER THREE
## Rivals and Revelations

Three years and a season had passed since two children were carried out of screaming dark.

Time had done its quiet work on all of them.

It's a strange thing, to raise a mystery.

The Dakshad household had set out to guard two secrets. Found, somewhere in the doing, they'd begun to raise two boys instead.

For a secret doesn't ask to be tucked in at night. Doesn't ask to be taught its letters. Doesn't ask to be held when it wakes from a dream it can't name and won't remember by morning.

The children did.

And in the daily smallness of that—meals, lessons, skinned knees, the thousand unremarkable mercies that turn a house into a home—the guarding had become something they hadn't planned for and couldn't now undo.

It had become love.

Most inconvenient possible outcome. Only one that had ever been likely.

---

They'd come up like seedlings forced through stone.

The elder, Aesir, had been a fierce and watchful child. Was becoming a fiercer, less watchful youth. Quick to laugh. Quicker to burn. His red hair deepening year on year into the color of banked coal—as though his body were slowly making visible something that had always been true of him.

Thirteen now. All restless edges. Forever a half step ahead of his own good sense.

The younger, Raijeen, was ten. Had grown into a stillness that unsettled adults who expected children to be loud.

Where Aesir announced himself, Raijeen *arrived.*

Quietly. Completely. Having already thought three moves past wherever the conversation was.

His eyes had been the first sign he wasn't what he appeared. Silver, flecked with violet. Too old for any child's face.

Lately a second sign had begun.

His black hair, at temples and through the crown, had started to darken not toward grey but toward *blue.* Deep, impossible, oceanic blue. Coming in strand by strand like a tide that had decided, very slowly, to come in.

No physician Mandu trusted could say why.

Aelayla, who'd stopped expecting the boys to obey biology's rules, only watched it happen. Kept her counsel. Thought private thoughts she shared with no one.

---

The world outside the estate's ancient walls had gone on as worlds do.

Valkhorra hadn't crossed the northern sea again—not in force—though reports from the coast never quite stopped.

Council schemed. Markets churned. White spires of Solspire caught sun each morning and threw it back, indifferent and certain.

The secret had held.

Two dozen soldiers kept their oath. Household kept its silence.

And the two most consequential children on Eloquence had been permitted, by the grace of obscurity, to do the one thing the world would never have allowed had it known what they were:

They'd been permitted to simply grow up.

---

And on this particular morning, three years and a season since the dark, that growing-up sounded like wood cracking against wood in the courtyard of the Dakshad estate.

Where two boys who weren't brothers fought like the only kind of brothers worth having:

The kind who'd die for each other and would rather lose a hand than admit it.

---

**✦✦✦**

Aesir came in high. All fire and forward weight. Red locs swinging. Grin that said the fight was already won and he was only being polite about finishing it.

Raijeen gave ground the way water gives ground—going nowhere, in no hurry, letting his brother spend himself.

Where Aesir was a thrown torch, Raijeen was the bank of the river. Blue-darkening hair falling across a still face. Silver eyes tracking every motion with the patience of someone reading a book he'd read before and intended to enjoy again anyway.

"You lean," Raijeen observed, sidestepping a strike that would've folded a lesser boy. "You've always leaned. One day someone's going to simply step aside and let your own enthusiasm put you on the ground. I'd like very much to be there when they do."

"You've been there for every defeat I've ever suffered." Aesir pressed. Wooden blades ringing. "All two of them. I keep you around for the witnessing."

"Two." Raijeen turned the next blow aside with economy that was almost insulting. "I admire a man who can count so low and sound so proud of the number."

Aesir laughed.

The laugh cost him a half-beat.

Raijeen took it. Slipped inside his guard. Laid the flat of his practice sword against his brother's ribs with the soft tap of a man closing a door he owns.

"There," said Raijeen. "Defeat the third. I'll begin a list."

"That list's going to be real short," Aesir said, rubbing his ribs. "Like your odds next round."

"My odds are excellent. Unlike your memory."

## CHAPTER THREE · I
### The Brothers at Practice

*Fire against water: the elder all forward weight and red fire, the younger calm and economical, his hair shading toward blue. Brotherhood worn as a fight.*

---

From the colonnade's shade, two figures watched the boys reset and circle again.

Neither was smiling the easy smile the morning seemed to ask for.

"They get faster," Lady Aelayla said. "Every month. Not stronger—they're children, strength will come. *Faster.* As though the rest of us are wading and they've found the dry path across."

Mandu didn't answer at once. Had the look of a man choosing which of several heavy things to set down first.

"I had the physicians run their blood again. My own men. Quietly."

He paused.

"Aelayla. I need to tell you what's in it. I need you to hear it as my wife and not as a scholar. Because the scholar in you is going to want to publish, and we can never, ever let her."

That earned him a glance: dry, amused, entirely aware of itself.

"You've been married to me fourteen years and you still think you can manage which woman answers you. Tell me. I'll decide afterward which of us heard it."

---

"Phosphorus." He kept his voice low, though the boys were well out of range. "Levels in their blood that should have them in a sickbed. Or a grave. Instead it seems to *feed* them."

He stopped. Hunting the word.

"And their neuromelanin. The dark matter in the deep brain. Theirs is laced with copper. Not a trace. A *thread* of it, woven all through, in a measure no Utiltan carries. No Eliant either. I checked yours against theirs. It isn't close."

Aelayla had gone very still.

The stillness was the scholar he'd warned her about, listening hard behind the wife's face.

"Copper changes how a current moves," she said slowly. "Through a wire. Through a nerve, I'd imagine, if a body were strange enough to thread itself with it. It would make them—"

"Fast." Mandu watched Raijeen read a feint before Aesir had committed to it. Watched the boy's blade already drifting to the answer of a question not yet asked. "It would make them fast in a way that looks, from the outside, like a man seeing one breath into tomorrow."

He paused.

"I've stood in front of soldiers thirty years. I know when a fighter is reacting. Those boys aren't reacting, Aelayla. Half the time they're *arriving.*"

---

For a moment, the two of them simply watched their not-sons live seconds ahead of the morning.

Aelayla felt something cold settle in her chest.

Not fear. Not quite.

*Recognition.*

She'd spent years studying the old texts. The fragments that survived the Eliant Wars. References to the Indextos—beings who'd touched creation itself before vanishing.

References to what they could *do.*

Move through time like water. See probability laid out like a map. Thread themselves with elements that shouldn't survive in living flesh.

*Copper. Phosphorus. Blue hair. Silver-violet eyes.*

She'd told herself those were myths. Exaggerations. The victors rewriting history to justify what they'd done.

Now, watching two children who shouldn't exist play at being ordinary, she understood.

The myths had undersold it.

"You're frightened," she said. Not an accusation. A reading.

"I'm a soldier. I'm frightened of what I can't name. In my experience, the thing you can't name is the thing that kills your men."

He let out a breath.

"And I'm something else, too. Have less practice with it. I look at them and I don't think *asset.* Don't think *weapon*—though the Lord Mandu of twenty years ago would've thought of nothing else."

He stopped. The word was an embarrassment to a man like him.

Said it anyway.

"I think *mine.* And a thing you think is yours, you stop seeing clearly. So I need you to keep seeing clearly for both of us. Will you do that."

She looked at him long.

Something in her expression gentled and sharpened at once.

"I've been seeing clearly since before you knew there was anything to see," she said. "It's the only way my kind is permitted to walk through your world, Mandu. With both eyes open and our hand on the door. You'll find I'm well practiced."

## CHAPTER THREE · II
### The Watchers in the Colonnade

*A soldier's worry and a scholar's knowing: two people in shade, carrying a secret they've come to love more than is wise.*

---

The boys finished. Flushed and grinning. Shoving each other toward the wash-house.

Courtyard emptied of their noise.

"They go to the Academy in the morning," Aelayla said, to the empty stones where they'd fought.

"They do. With Bandar's two. Askan will keep the order. Naska will break it. Between the pair of them, the boys will be looked after." Mandu almost smiled. "Good house to be folded into."

"It's a *Utiltan* house to be folded into."

She let that sit a moment. Not unkindly.

"You taught them the sword, Mandu. I will teach them the rest. The part you were spared and never had to learn. How to be extraordinary in a world that's only ever found two uses for the extraordinary: to worship it, or to break it."

She paused.

"And it tires of worship so quickly."

"Then teach them," he said. "I'll sit at the back of the room. I've lately discovered I'm a poorer student than I believed. And a better one than I was."

"That," she said, corner of her mouth moving, "is progress so slow I can almost see it."

---

**✦✦✦**

The Utiltan Academy of Ancient Strategy didn't teach children.

It forged them.

And like all forges, it ran hot. What it couldn't shape, it was content to burn.

It rose at the city's eastern edge. All grey stone and disciplined lines. Older than the banners that hung on it. Through its gates each morning poured the sons and daughters of every house that mattered, to be made into the next generation of the empire's blades and minds.

The Academy called this *opportunity.*

What it meant, beneath banners and drilling and high talk of merit, was simpler. Older.

This was the room where empire taught its children, very early, exactly who would be permitted to hold power and who would spend their lives serving it.

The lessons in the yard were only the lessons you could see.

## CHAPTER THREE · III
### The Academy

*The room where empire teaches its children who will hold power and who will serve it: two small figures crossing a yard that quietly parts around them.*

---

Aesir and Raijeen crossed the great courtyard through a current of students that thinned, subtly, around them.

The particular space a crowd leaves around the unexplained.

"They're staring again," Aesir said, not lowering his voice nearly enough.

"You have hair the color of a warning flag. I have eyes that catch light like a coin at the bottom of a well." Raijeen kept his own voice even. "We could wear sacks over our heads and they'd invent a reason. Let them stare. Staring is free and changes nothing."

"It changes *my* mood."

"Your mood was always going to find something. I'm simply grateful, today, that it's them and not me."

They'd nearly cleared the yard when the way closed in front of them.

Five older boys. Unhurried. Arranging themselves with the lazy confidence of people who'd never once been made to answer for anything.

Their leader was tall, handsome in the soft way of a boy who'd been told so by people who wanted things from his father. He wore a smirk that looked permanent. Like a scar from a wound he'd given himself.

"The flame-head," he said, "and his little well-eyed shadow." His friends produced the laughter boys like him keep on retainer. "Tell me something. Does your Eliant mother teach you both to skulk, or does it come natural to your sort?"

Raijeen felt his brother change beside him the way you feel a coal catch.

He set two fingers against Aesir's forearm.

*Not yet. Not here. Not for him.*

Said, with a calm that cost him something: "We've no quarrel with you. We'd like to pass."

"He'd like to *pass.*"

The boy stepped in. Shoved Raijeen in the chest.

Hard. Two-handed. The shove of someone who'd learned the world rearranged itself around his hands and never bruised them.

Raijeen went back a step. Caught himself. Didn't fall.

And Aesir stopped being warm.

---

"Put your hands on him again," Aesir said.

The thing that made the older boys' smiles flicker was that he didn't say it loudly. Or hotly.

He said it the way Mandu said things. The way a man states a fact about the near future.

"I'll be honest with you, since we're being honest about our sorts. I've wanted, all morning, for something to happen. You've got a generous spirit, walking up to volunteer."

The spear came off the rack behind the leader. Into his hands.

The yard's mood tipped from cruelty into something with edges.

---

What followed was fast and ugly and graceless, the way real fighting is.

Five against two. Fists and wooden blades and a stolen spear. No rhythm to it. Only the scramble of bodies deciding in the moment whether they were the kind that broke or the kind that held.

A hand closed on Aesir from behind. He drove an elbow back into something soft. Felt it give.

Raijeen took a blow across the shoulder. Rolled with it. Came up under a boy's reach and put him down with a strike to the knee that folded the leg wrong.

They fought back to back without deciding to. The way they'd done everything since they could walk.

Each one's blind side covered by the other's eyes.

---

For one half-instant, in the worst of it, a boy at the edge of the scrum thought he saw light move under the flame-head's skin.

Thin and gold. Like a lamp carried past a shuttered window.

He blinked. It was gone.

He'd mention it later, to a friend. "Thought I saw something weird." The friend would laugh. "Dust in your eyes, idiot." He'd nod, uncertain, and never bring it up again.

It was probably nothing.

Then the leader had the spear leveled.

Point coming for Aesir's chest with a boy's panic behind it—which is more dangerous than a man's malice.

There was a half-breath in which the morning could've ended very badly.

It didn't.

## CHAPTER THREE · IV
### Back to Back

*Five against two, and a leveled spear: the brothers fight as they've done everything since they could walk, each one's blind side covered by the other's eyes.*

---

The spear stopped.

Caught, a hand's width from Aesir's tunic, knocked aside by a practice blade that arrived from nowhere with a crack like a snapped beam.

The boy who'd swung it was broad through the shoulders. Planted like a fortress. His face arranged into the calm of someone who'd decided the matter and was now only seeing it through.

"Five on two." Askan Sulph didn't raise his voice either. Seemed to run in good Utiltan houses. "And one of you reaching for a spear. I've watched cleaner work from men I'd have flogged for cowardice."

He set himself between the brothers and the bullies. Unhurried. Immovable.

"Let's see how the arithmetic feels with the numbers fixed."

---

The arithmetic changed because a second blade had joined it.

And the one who held it moved nothing like her brother.

Where Askan was a wall, she was a wind through a gap in it. Quick. Economical. Already past you by the time you'd squared to meet her.

She put herself at Raijeen's flank. Flicked the closest boy's wrist with the tip of her sword—precise, stinging little correction, the kind a teacher gives a slow student.

"You'll want to drop that," Naska Sulph advised the boy, pleasantly, "before I decide your wrist looks better at an angle."

---

*Naska Sulph had learned, very early, that the world gave you two choices:*

*Be small and safe, or be sharp and seen.*

*She'd chosen sharp.*

*Not because she was fearless. Because she'd watched her mother once—just once—make herself smaller to pass through a Utiltan parlor without disturbing the air, and the sight had made Naska's stomach turn.*

*Her father, Bandar, had taught her the blade. Her mother had taught her the sharper lesson: that some people are born having to prove they deserve the same air, and you could spend your whole life proving it or you could make them prove* you *didn't.*

*Askan had chosen the first path. Quiet competence. Earned respect.*

*Naska had chosen the second. Be loud. Be undeniable. Make them look away first.*

*She told herself it was strength.*

*Some nights, alone, she wondered if it was just another kind of fear.*

---

It was over quickly after that.

Bullies are only ever brave at the odds they choose. Presented with worse ones, the leader discovered an urgent appointment elsewhere. His retainers remembered they were really very busy.

The five of them withdrew with what dignity they could carry.

Which wasn't much. Bled none of it on the way out.

---

Aesir stood in the sudden quiet. Chest heaving. Heat still coming off him in waves with nowhere left to go.

Askan offered a hand.

Aesir looked at it a beat. Pride and gratitude wrestling, the way they do in proud people.

Then took it. Let himself be hauled up.

"Askan Sulph." A nod. No warmth wasted, none withheld. "And the loud one is my twin sister, before she tells you otherwise and claims she's the elder. We were born the same hour. She's never let me forget she arrived first."

"By a quarter hour," Naska said, sheathing her blade, "which in this family is the difference between the one who leads and the one who broods about it. I'm the competent one. He got the shoulders. I got everything else."

Her green eyes went to Aesir. Stayed a moment past where they needed to.

Taking the measure of him. The red of his hair. The way the anger hadn't quite finished leaving him and he wasn't bothering to hide it.

Something flickered there. Filed away. Quick and private. Behind a grin.

"You fight angry. That's going to get you killed, eventually." A beat. "It was also the best thing I've watched all week, so I'm of two minds about correcting it."

---

*She didn't say:*

*I've never seen anyone that certain of themselves.*

*I've never seen anyone that unbothered by what the world thinks.*

*I've spent my whole life being sharp because I was afraid to be soft, and you're standing there like softness never occurred to you as a choice.*

*I want to know how you do that.*

---

"Aesir." He found he was grinning back before he'd decided to. "This is Raijeen. He's the one who'll lecture you about not fighting angry, so you two should get along."

"At last," said Raijeen, rubbing his bruised shoulder, "an ally with sense." He looked at Naska. "Please tell me you also believe leaning into a strike is a character flaw and not a fighting style."

"It's a eulogy waiting to be read," Naska said immediately.

Raijeen made a sound that in a more dignified person would've been a laugh.

---

Askan watched the four of them fall into it. The easy, instant grammar of people who would, it was already clear, become something to each other.

Allowed himself the smallest nod of a boy who'd just decided where his loyalty was going to go and saw no reason to take it back later.

"Stick close to us," he said. "The Academy's a longer war than that lot. You'll want people at your back who know the difference between an order and an excuse to be cruel."

He glanced at the gate the bullies had fled through.

"There's a great deal of the second kind here, dressed up as the first."

---

## CHAPTER THREE · V
### The Four

*The small, fierce company assembled: Askan the wall, Naska the wind, and two brothers who are about to find that the walk is easier with four than it was with two.*

---

They walked in together. The four of them. Into grey stone and drilling and the long shaping morning.

None of them could've told you, then, that they'd just assembled the small, fierce company the coming years would test to its breaking.

And past it.

They only knew the walk was easier with four than it had been with two. That laughter came quicker. That somewhere in the clash and the rescue and the trading of barbs, a door the world keeps locked between strangers had, for the four of them, quietly come open.

---

The Academy bells rang the hour.

The empire called its children to their lessons.

And four of them went in already learning the one the empire hadn't meant to teach:

That the people who stand at your back by choice are worth more than all the power the grey stone was built to hand out.

And far harder to take away.

---

Far above the courtyard, in the high tower where the Masters kept watch, one figure didn't turn away when the bells rang.

Master Eryndor. Robes the color of ash. Eyes that had spent forty years reading histories most burned after the war.

He'd seen the fight. Seen the way the red-haired boy moved—not faster than the others, but with a certainty that looked like he knew what was coming.

And he'd seen the moment the boy's anger flared.

A pulse of light beneath the skin. Faint. Unmistakable.

Raw psionic manifestation. Not channeled through Eltech.

Eryndor looked down at the courtyard. At the boy's wrist, where civilian-grade Eltech glinted in the sun. The kind that limits output. Keeps young Eliants from burning themselves out before they learn control.

That boy had just bypassed it entirely.

Eryndor had only seen that described in pre war texts. Before Eliants needed tech to sustain their gifts. When their reserves were deep enough to manifest raw.

That stamina didn't exist anymore. Centuries of regulated dependency had bred it out.

*Unless it hadn't.*

He turned from the window. Pulled parchment.

Wrote three words:

*Mandu. We need to talk.*

Then burned it. Too risky to send.

He'd visit tonight. Quietly.

Some conversations couldn't be written down.
