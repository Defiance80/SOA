# CHAPTER FOUR
## The Devil's Gamble

Two more years had worked on Aesir the way weather works on stone.

Slowly. Then all at once you notice the shape has changed.

He was fifteen now. The city had stopped mistaking him for a child.

He'd come up tall and hard through the shoulders, his father's training laid into him like rebar. Somewhere in the last two years his face had set into something that made grown men reconsider their first impression. The red hair had darkened to the color of old blood.

The boy who'd needed Askan to pull a spear off his chest was three years gone.

What stood in his place could put most grown men on the ground. Had, a few times, in rooms his family would've been horrified to learn he'd been standing in.

---

The household had changed around him too, in the small ways that add up to a life.

Mandu had grown grayer. Quieter. Away more often. Valkhorra reports never quite stopping.

Aelayla had taught Raijeen to read the old symbols, then to read what the symbols *meant.* The younger brother had taken to it the way Aesir took to the underworld: vanishing into it for hours, surfacing changed.

Raijeen was twelve now. Spoke like something older. The blue had come fully into his hair—deep as a deep-water current. Strangers stared. The brothers had long since stopped bothering to explain it.

They'd grown in opposite directions. Stayed, somehow, exactly as close.

Aesir reaching outward—into the city, into trouble.

Raijeen reaching inward—into the drowned history Aelayla kept feeding him.

Each was a little afraid of where the other was going. Neither said so.

---

Naska had grown up too.

That was a thing Aesir had noticed and then worked hard at not noticing.

The sharp-tongued girl who'd flicked a bully's wrist in the Academy yard had become a sharp-tongued young woman who could put an arrow through a coin at sixty paces.

Somewhere in the last two years their old easy friction had grown a second current underneath it. A thing that made them both talk faster. Stand a careful arm's length apart. Invent reasons to argue about nothing.

They hadn't named it.

Naming it felt like stepping off something high.

So they circled it, the way you circle a fire you're not sure you're allowed to be warm at.

---

And Aesir had found his way down.

It hadn't been one door. That was the thing nobody up top understood about the underworld: they imagined a single trapdoor, a secret you were handed once and kept.

It wasn't.

It was a thousand small permissions, earned one at a time.

It had started two years back with a question he couldn't stop asking and a servant who'd had too much wine at a winter feast. The man had let slip he hadn't always lived inside the walls. That there were ways down, and under, and out, if you knew the right crooked people. That the young master should forget he'd said any of it.

Aesir had not forgotten.

He'd pulled the thread. Thread led to a noodle stall in the Ninth. The man at the stall had watched him come back four times before he'd tipped his head toward the dark.

Even then Aesir had spent the better part of a year being nobody down there. Keeping his wonder off his face the way he'd been taught. Learning the rules the way he learned everything—by getting them wrong and surviving it.

---

Because the underworld did not forgive the naive.

He'd watched it not forgive them.

A city of millions sat stacked above his head. Almost none of those millions would ever see what Aesir saw on the nights he went down.

Most couldn't reach it. A great many, the lucky ones, never wanted to.

But the ones who came down soft—came down curious, with coin in their hands and wonder on their faces and no one at their backs—the underworld ate those.

Not always quickly.

Sometimes it just kept them. The way the bottom of a river keeps things.

---

Aesir had not been eaten.

Partly because he was Mandu Dakshad's son and could hurt people in ways that surprised them.

Partly because he'd learned, fast and young, that the trick to walking through a place that wants to swallow you is to never once look like food.

But mostly because of a thing he didn't have a name for.

A calm that sat at the center of him when it should've been fear. A stillness in the exact moment other men panicked.

He didn't understand it. Wouldn't understand it for years.

He only knew that danger seemed to slow down around him. That he saw the blow a half-beat before it came and his body answered before his mind had finished the thought.

The underworld read this without being able to explain it.

Gave him room.

*That one's not afraid, and you can't see why. Leave it.*

---

If he was honest—and he was, late at night, in the one corner of himself he didn't show anyone—he'd started to like that.

Liking it was the beginning of a problem he was years from meeting.

---

Tonight he was going down to the one place he'd been circling for a year without entering.

The pits.

And the city he descended through to reach them was not a backdrop. It was a country with its own laws.

And tonight it was wide awake.

---

## CHAPTER FOUR · I
### The Stair Down

*The pale proud city giving way to older, darker stone cut by a different geometry: a boy going down into the buried city beneath the city.*

---

The Ninth after dark was a different animal than the Ninth his tutors would've admitted existed.

He came down through it the way you wade a current you know. Reading it, not fighting it.

The respectable lamps gave out two streets in. After that, light came from where light comes from in places the empire has stopped paying attention to:

From doorways.

From cheap blue-green Eltech bulbs buzzing over establishments that wanted to be found.

From braziers where the homeless gathered in clots, warming their hands, watching everything—because watching everything was the only currency they had left and they spent it carefully.

---

There were a great many of them.

That was the thing the up-top histories left out. The sheer number of people the bright city above had quietly decided it had no room for.

The ones born in the wrong year, when the gates didn't open.

The ones who'd fallen out of the lottery. Out of favor. Out of work. Kept falling, all the way down here to the bottom where the old stone was.

And it *was* old. Older than the histories admitted.

The deeper he went, the stranger the stonework. Angled and carved by hands that had raised a city here long before the Rolt dynasty ever set its first white tower. A city the bright one above had simply built over and agreed to forget.

---

The falling people lined its walls. Slept in warm grates.

A child no older than six watched Aesir pass with flat, ancient eyes—someone who'd already learned the only lesson the Ninth taught for free:

No one was coming.

He gave the child a coin.

He'd learned not to give two. Two marked you soft. Soft was a price the Ninth collected on.

---

The women ran this district.

A stranger would've had it exactly backwards.

A stranger would've seen the ladies in lit doorways—the painted ones, the ones who called soft offers to men going by—and thought *prey.*

The stranger would've been a fool. The Ninth was unkind to fools.

The women of the lit doorways were not the bottom of this place. They were close to the top of it.

They knew every face that came through. Every debt. Every weakness. Every man who'd talked too freely with his guard down and his wants showing.

Information ran through them the way water runs downhill.

Information was the realest power the underworld had.

---

The district's true authority sat in a house at the end of the longest street. Belonged to a woman called Madam Reyhan.

She'd come up through those same lit doorways thirty years before. Now owned half of them. Could have a man removed from the world with a word passed through the right painted mouth.

The men with the muscle worked for the women with the knowledge.

It had simply never occurred to the empire above to imagine the arrangement could run that direction.

Which was exactly why it could.

---

## CHAPTER FOUR · II
### The Ninth

*Lit doorways and watching eyes, braziers and old stone: where the women hold the real power, and the walls listen, and the Ninth remembers.*

---

Aesir nodded to a woman in a green doorway.

Lysse.

She'd decided a year ago the strange fearless boy was good for nothing but also no trouble. Had extended him the small mercy of not having him robbed.

She tipped her chin back, once.

*Still alive, then.*

It was as warm as the Ninth got. He'd learned to value it.

"You're going to the pits," she said. Not a question. Lysse didn't ask questions—she announced the answers she already had. "Of course you are. The clean ones always want to watch the dirty ones bleed. Makes them feel better about their soft hands."

"My hands aren't that soft anymore."

"No." She looked at them, then at his face, with the frank assessing eye of a woman whose whole trade was reading what men were before they knew it themselves. "No. They're getting there."

She paused.

"Pity. You were prettier when you were useless."

She nodded down the street, toward where the old stairs went deeper.

"Go on, then. But the pits aren't the doorways, little lord. Down here, I keep you breathing because it amuses me to. Down there, nobody's amused. Nobody's keeping you anything. You understand the difference?"

"I'm starting to."

"Start faster."

And she turned back to her doorway and her trade.

Aesir went on, down, toward the deepest stair and the oldest stone.

Where the city's last bottom was.

---

**✦✦✦**

The door at the foot of the pit-stair was wood and rusted iron.

The two men in front of it knew him.

Not well. Knowing someone well was a thing that cost more than either of these men had ever spent on anyone.

But they knew his face. Knew it the way you know a stray that's been hanging around the yard long enough you've started, against your own better judgment, to find it interesting.

"Well," said the big one, not moving. "Look what crawled up out of the polite part of town. Thought we finally scared you off, your highness. Been a hopeful little while."

"You've been hoping about a lot of things, Doss," Aesir said. "Your hairline. Hasn't worked out for you there either."

The smaller one—the bearded one, who Aesir had privately named the Conscience, for the tired sorry way he delivered his threats—made a sound that might, in better light, have been a laugh.

"He's got a mouth on him tonight."

"He's always got a mouth on him. That's the problem with this one."

Doss looked Aesir over. Slow. Something in it now that hadn't been there a year ago. Grudging, watchful curiosity. The look of a man who's seen a thing do something unexpected twice and is waiting to see if it'll do it a third time.

"You know what you are, kid? You're a coin somebody flipped and it ain't landed yet. We keep waiting to see which side."

He paused.

"Some nights I figure you'll be facedown in the Ninth by spring. Soft and dead like all the curious ones."

Another pause.

"Other nights I watch you move out of the way of trouble half a breath before trouble knows it's coming, and I think, *huh.* Maybe not."

"You watch me that closely, Doss, people'll talk."

"People down here already talk about you, your highness. That's your whole problem and you're too green to know it yet."

But he stepped aside. The Conscience pulled the door inward. The grudging respect underneath the needling was real, even if neither of them would've died before admitting it.

"Inside. Hands where folks can see 'em. And when somebody finally takes you down a peg in there—and somebody will, boy, you've got a face that begs for it—you remember we let you walk in grinning."

"I'll send flowers to whoever manages it."

"Arrogant little—"

But the door was already swinging.

And the sound came out to meet him.

---

It hit him first, before the heat, before the smell.

A wall of it.

Voices stacked on voices. The wet crack of something striking something. A roar that climbed and climbed and then dropped all at once into a groan, the way a wave breaks.

Then the heat. Thick as a palm laid flat on his face.

Then the smell, which he'd carry in the back of his throat for a week:

Sweat. Spilled liquor. Blood.

And under all of it something sweetish and chemical and *wrong* that he didn't have a name for yet.

---

He stepped inside.

Door shut behind him.

The world he'd been raised never to wonder about closed over his head like water.

---

It took his eyes a moment.

Light came from bulbs strung on sagging wire. The cheap Eltech kind that buzzed and flickered and made everyone look already half-dead.

The room was packed. Bodies shoulder to shoulder. All turned inward toward a ring marked off with rope and old blood.

Nobody looked at him.

Down here, looking at a stranger was a thing you did on purpose. On-purpose was expensive.

He found a gap at the rope's edge. Took it.

---

Two men were fighting.

One was a slab. Shaved head. Body built by hauling things. Fists like a pair of dropped bricks.

The other was lean and quick. Strung with tattoos down both arms and up his neck, the ink gone blue-green and blurred with age.

The slab was winning the way a wall wins.

He'd back the lean one into the rope and hammer him. Patient. Methodical. The lean one would slip out the side bleeding from somewhere new. The crowd would howl. They'd do it again.

---

It was ugly.

Aesir had trained his whole life with wooden blades. Against Raijeen. Against the Academy's careful masters.

Clean fighting. Fighting with rules. Fighting you could lose and walk away from.

This had no rules in it.

This was two men trying to turn each other off.

---

And some part of Aesir—the part he didn't show anyone, the part that had started to like the calm danger gave him—watched the slab's technique and thought, without arrogance and therefore all the more arrogantly:

*I could take him. I could take both of them.*

He didn't know yet how dangerous a thought that was. Hadn't met the thing that would teach him.

He only knew he'd never once, in three years of training against the best the empire's Academy could put in front of him, met the person who could make him work for it.

And a boy who's never been made to work for it mistakes that for a fact about the world, instead of a fact about the rooms he's been allowed in.

---

"First time."

The voice came from his left. Not a question.

An old man had appeared at the rope beside him the way old men in places like this appear—which is to say he'd probably been there an hour and Aesir simply hadn't been worth noticing until now.

His beard was grey. His face a map of a long hard country. One eye didn't track quite right.

He smelled of pipe-smoke and the cheap liquor everyone here was breathing.

"That obvious?" Aesir said.

"You watched the big one's feet just now, not his hands. Means you've been taught. Properly, by someone who knew what they were doing."

The old man kept his good eye on the ring.

"Which means you're somebody's son. Which means you don't belong here. Which means you'll come back anyway."

He paused.

"Samir. I'd say welcome, but I've got a conscience that flares up now and then, and welcoming you would set it off."

"Aesir."

"Aesir." Samir tasted the name. Found nothing in it. "Rich name. Rich clothes, under the dirt you've rubbed on 'em to fit in—which, by the way, fools no one. You just look like a clean boy who's been rolling in alleys."

The sharp eye flicked to him.

"You're a long way down from wherever they iron your shirts, son. What's a boy like you doing in a hole like this?"

"Trying to understand it."

"Understand *what.* The fighting? There's nothing to understand. Two men, no money, no door out, and a crowd that'll pay to watch them pretend the other one's the reason their life went the way it did."

Samir made a small sound. Not a laugh.

"That's not a mystery, boy. That's just Tuesday."

"Not the fighting." Aesir kept his eyes on the ring. "The power. I keep hearing there's power down here you can't get up top. Real power. I want to see what it is."

Something shifted in the old man's face. Not much.

A door, easing closed.

"You will," he said. "Give it a minute. You'll wish you hadn't."

## CHAPTER FOUR · III
### False Power

*A hollow man made a god for thirty seconds: the power that eats the future to pay for the present, and the crowd that pays to watch.*

---

It happened in the next round.

A new pair came in. The lean tattooed man was one of them again.

Which told Aesir something: down here you fought until you couldn't, and then you fought once more.

He was swaying. Left eye had closed. He should not have been standing.

The crowd knew it. The odds-men were calling numbers that said he'd be on the floor inside a minute.

His new opponent was younger. Frightened, under the bravado. A boy, really, only a few years older than Aesir. With the desperate jaw of someone who needed the purse and had decided not to think about the rest.

And then the lean man reached into his own waistband and drew out a vial.

---

The room leaned.

You could feel it. The whole crowd tilting forward an inch, the way a congregation leans toward an altar.

The liquid in the vial was the sick blue-green Aesir had been smelling since he walked in. The color he'd been raised to fear without ever being told why.

Orma.

He'd seen the word in his father's reports. In the hard set of Mandu's mouth whenever it came up.

He had never once seen anyone drink it.

The lean man drank it.

---

For a breath, nothing.

Then his veins came up dark beneath the skin. Not red. *Dark.* Like ink poured into water. Racing up his arms and his throat toward his face.

His ruined eye snapped open.

And the thing that looked out of it was not, Aesir understood with a lurch, entirely the man anymore.

It was something wearing him.

---

He moved.

The moving was wrong. Too fast. Too smooth. A body doing things a body that tired could not do.

He crossed the ring without seeming to cross the distance.

Caught the younger fighter by the front of his shirt.

And threw him.

Not the way a strong man throws—with a grunt and a heave.

The way you'd toss a coat onto a bed.

Casual. One-handed. Almost bored.

---

The boy left his feet. Sailed back over the rope into the crowd.

The crowd parted.

He struck the old stone. Did not get up quickly.

Somewhere a woman began screaming a name that was probably his.

---

The room came apart.

Coins. Fists. Voices.

The lean man stood in the center of it with his dark veins and his stolen speed and his face turned up to the buzzing lights, grinning.

There was nothing behind the grin at all.

Just a man who had, for about thirty seconds, gotten to feel like a god.

---

And Aesir, who had thought, four minutes ago, *I could take both of them,* felt the certainty go cold in his chest.

Because he could not have done that.

No training he'd ever had answered that.

For the first time in three years, he'd watched a body do something his couldn't.

The wrongness of it scared him in a way the violence hadn't.

---

It scared something else, too. Something he would not think about clearly until much later, when he had reason to.

In the cold lurch of that moment a thin warmth moved along his own forearms. Under the skin. There and gone.

A heat that answered the fear without being asked.

He looked down.

Nothing. Sweat and lamplight.

He'd imagined it.

He put it out of his mind the way you put out a candle you're not sure you lit. Told himself it was the room, the heat, the drink he hadn't had.

He kept his eyes on the ring.

Did not look at his hands again.

---

"There it is," Samir said quietly, beside him. "There's your power, son. Look close. You won't want to, but look."

Aesir looked.

And now that he was looking, he could see it.

The cost. Already arriving.

The lean man's grin was slipping. His legs had begun to shake—fine tremor that built as the seconds passed.

The dark in his veins was fading. Draining out of him. Taking something with it as it went.

By the time the crowd's roar peaked and started down the far side, the man wasn't standing in triumph anymore.

He was just standing. The way a building stands after a fire.

Upright. Hollow. Waiting for someone to notice it should already have fallen.

---

"It eats the future to pay for the present," Samir said. "Orma cut with Eltech. The tech opens the door. The Orma walks you through it. For one fight, you're more than you are. And every fight after, you're less. Less strong. Less *here.*"

He nodded at the lean man being helped from the ring now by two others. Feet dragging. Eyes gone to that flat blue-green nowhere Aesir had smelled all night.

"I've watched that man win forty times. Each win bought with a piece of him. There's a version of him from ten years back that was somebody. Funny. Quick. Had a girl, had a kid."

Samir's voice didn't rise. Which made it land harder.

"This is what's left after the power got done being generous with him."

---

Aesir couldn't take his eyes off the man being dragged away.

"Then why," he said, and stopped, because it was a child's question and he knew it, and asked it anyway. "Why would anyone, *why?*"

"Because up there—" Samir tipped his head at the ceiling, at the miles of pale, proud, certain city stacked over their heads. "—up there, men like him are nothing. Less than nothing. Born in the wrong year to be let in, or fell out of the lottery, or crossed the wrong somebody. Now they live down here in the dark with the rats and the old stones. And the only door out of that is a box."

He shrugged.

"Then a man hands them a vial and says, *for half a minute, you can be the strongest in the room. For half a minute, every eye is on you and not through you.*"

He paused.

"And you ask me *why* they take it."

The old man turned and looked at him full on. Both eyes. The lazy one and the sharp one.

"You've got a roof, son. A name. A full belly. People who'd come looking if you didn't come home. You have never once in your soft little life been *nobody.* So no, you don't get to ask why. Not yet. You haven't earned the question."

---

It should've stung.

It did sting.

It was also, Aesir understood, the kindest thing anyone had said to him all night.

Because Samir had bothered to tell him the truth. And nobody bothers with the truth for people they've already written off.

"Then teach me the question," Aesir said.

Samir studied him long.

Whatever he saw made the old man tired in a new way.

"Go home, Aesir." He turned back to the ring, where they were already roping off the next two desperate men. "Go home, and come back, because boys like you always come back. And when you do, keep your coin in your boot and your wonder off your face. Down here, wonder's a thing people sell."

He nodded once more at the dark doorway they'd dragged the hollow man through.

"And the ones who buy it—they pay in pieces."

---

Aesir climbed back up through the old stone with the smell still in his throat.

Past the lit doorways. Past the braziers and the watching eyes. Up into the pale certain city that had no idea what it was sitting on.

He kept his wonder off his face the whole way.

But he didn't stop wondering.

He'd understood the most dangerous thing a boy in his position could understand:

That everything he'd been taught was true *was* true, the way a wall is true. Solid. Real.

And built, specifically, so you'd never see what was on the other side of it.

---

He'd seen the other side now.

Couldn't unsee it.

And some quiet, permanent part of him had already decided he was going back.

---

**✦✦✦**

Across the city, in a warmer dark, Raijeen was learning about power too.

By the opposite road.

---

The study at the heart of the Dakshad estate was the one room in the house that felt older than the house.

Aelayla had made it hers over the years. It had become a held-breath of a place: scrolls in their cases, books stacked in the particular disorder of someone who knows exactly where everything is, a single good lamp throwing gold across a low table.

Raijeen sat on the floor with his back against a shelf. Heavy book open across his knees.

He'd been on the same page for some time. Had not turned it.

"You've stopped reading," Aelayla said, without looking up from her own work.

"I can't make the symbols mean anything." He said it without heat. Raijeen rarely showed frustration—he showed problems, held them out like objects for inspection. "I know the shapes. I can copy them. But they don't open. Aesir would've thrown the book by now."

"Aesir would've thrown the book at hour one and gone looking for something to climb."

A small smile touched her mouth. Left.

"You and your brother fail in different directions. It's the most useful thing about the two of you."

She set down her pen.

"What are you really asking me, Raijeen. You've been circling it all evening. You circle a thing the way your father studies a road before he'll walk down it."

---

He looked up.

In the lamplight his eyes were very old for his face. Silver. Threaded with that violet no one could explain.

"The book says the Eliants were great once. Honored. Keepers of knowledge." He said the words like they belonged to someone else. "But everyone I've ever met treats—treats *us*—" he caught the word, held it, and Aelayla watched him decide to keep it, "—like something to be careful of. Like a fire in a dry house. Both can't be true. Either we were great, or we're dangerous. Which one is the lie?"

Aelayla was quiet a moment.

When she answered, she didn't answer the question he'd asked. She answered the harder one underneath it.

"Neither," she said. "That's the part that took me forty years to understand, so don't be cross with yourself for not having it at twelve."

She came down off her stool. Sat on the floor across from him—which she almost never did.

Raijeen understood, the way he understood most things, that she was about to tell him something she didn't tell people.

"We were great. And it was *because* we were great that we became dangerous. Not dangerous because we did harm. Dangerous because of what we could do, in the eyes of people who'd decided, long before we ever lifted a finger, that anyone able to do it couldn't be allowed to."

"That doesn't make sense."

"No. It doesn't. Injustice rarely does, said plainly. That's why people dress it up."

She drew the heavy book gently from his lap. Turned it toward herself. Her fingers found a worn page without searching.

"And before I show you anything, learn this first, because it matters more than any date or name I'll give you tonight."

She tapped the page once.

"What's written here is one telling. Mine. The one my mother carried, and hers. House Vasari keeping its own account because the account everyone else keeps was written by the winners, and the winners had reason to write it kind to themselves."

She paused.

"Even our own people don't agree on all of it. The temple-keepers in the north tell it one way. The ones who went to Damascus tell it another. Each is certain the others have let the truth rot."

She looked at him steadily.

"So when I say a thing happened, hear the shape of it. Hold a little room for the parts I have wrong. The moment a history stops letting you doubt it is the moment it's stopped being history and started being a leash."

"Then how do I ever know what really happened."

"You don't. Not all of it. You learn to read the gaps. You trust the account that admits what it can't prove over the one that's smooth all the way through, because the world is not smooth, and only a lie has no seams."

She let that settle. Then softened.

"Now. Let me show you a city. And you can decide, when you're grown, how much of me to believe."

---

She didn't read him the words. She told him. The way you tell someone about a place you've lost.

"Klemnar," she said, and the name came out of her differently than the rest. Carefully. The way you'd lift something cracked.

"The jewel of Profront. Our people built it, long ago, on a coast the color of slate. And it was, Raijeen, by every account that survives—ours or theirs—the most learned place the world had ever made."

Her thumb moved over the page as if she could feel the city through it.

"They didn't build with their hands the way Utilta builds. They built with thought. Machines that were half alive. Grown, not forged. That thought alongside the minds that made them."

She paused.

"They understood a thing the world has spent a very long age pretending it never knew: that the mind isn't a thing trapped inside you, looking out. That it *reaches.* That consciousness is part of the weave of everything. And if you understand the weave well enough, a thought can pull a thread, and the thread can move the world."

Raijeen had stopped breathing quite so much. "That's the psionics. What people call—"

"What people call *magic,* yes. Because it frightens them less to call it magic than to admit it was knowledge, and that they burned the people who held it."

Her voice stayed level. The levelness was the thing. Raijeen was old enough now to hear what it cost her to keep it.

"It was never magic. It was the oldest science there is. The one the world keeps forgetting on purpose. And Klemnar held more of it than anywhere. We weren't conquerors. We didn't want anyone's land. We sat on our slate coast and we *understood* things. Got better at it every year."

She paused.

"And that, in the end, was the unforgivable thing."

"Who," Raijeen said. "Who couldn't forgive it."

A shadow crossed her. Aesir would've missed it. Raijeen didn't.

"A people called Ari-Sapeia." She said the name flatly. Something underneath the flatness was complicated in a way Raijeen filed away to think about later. "Old. Powerful. From the south, across the water. They had their own claim to ancient things. Their own deep pride in it."

For a moment she seemed to weigh saying more. Chose not to.

"What matters is that they looked across the sea at Klemnar, at what we could do, and they did not see scholars. They saw a threat that hadn't threatened them yet. The most dangerous kind, to a frightened mind. The threat that's only a possibility."

She paused.

"You can't make peace with a possibility, Raijeen. You can only end it."

"What did they do?"

"They didn't march. That's the part the histories like to soften." Her hand had gone still on the page. "They didn't meet our minds with their minds or our soldiers with their soldiers. Too clever for that. Too afraid. They made a weapon. A sickness. Grown the way we grew our machines, except built to unmake instead of make."

She let the silence sit. Didn't fill it with adjectives. Let Raijeen's own imagination do the work—which was crueler and truer.

"And one night, with no warning and no war declared, they loosed it on Klemnar."

She paused.

"The city didn't burn. Would've been kinder if it burned. It *rotted.* The ones who didn't die were changed. Twisted. In their bodies. By the thing that was made to twist them. The slate coast went silent in a single season."

Her voice went quieter.

"And the most learned place the world ever made became a thing mothers used to frighten children into bed."

"And Ari-Sapeia did it alone," Raijeen said. It was almost a question. He was watching her face.

Aelayla did not answer at once. The not-answering was itself an answer he would understand better in years to come.

"That is what the treaties say," she said finally. "That Ari-Sapeia did it alone. That everyone else came late and grieving to a ruin they'd had no hand in. It is a very tidy account. And the tidiest accounts are the ones I trust the least."

She paused.

"A weapon like that is not built in secret by one people with no one else knowing. No one else selling. No one else looking the other way for a price and a promise. Someone opened doors. Someone chose, when the screaming started, to hear it as weather."

She looked at him steadily.

"When you're older I will tell you which banners flew clean out of that season with their reputations laundered. You may notice some of them fly over us now."

She said it without heat. Which was worse than heat.

"But that is a lesson for a Raijeen with more years on him. Tonight, hold only this: no city dies alone. It has help. And the help writes the history."

---

Raijeen was quiet a long time.

When he spoke, his voice had gone very small and very steady—which was how his fear sounded.

"And that started the wars."

"That started the wars. A hundred years of them, give or take. The counts don't agree."

Aelayla closed the book softly.

"After Klemnar, the rest of us scattered. Some fought. Some hid. Some walked into the sea, I'm told, rather than be hunted. And some—many—went down. And under. Into a place that opened its doors when nowhere above them would. A network of cities beneath the ground, in the east, that took in the broken remnant of a people the whole surface world had agreed to be done with."

"Uwell," Raijeen said.

"Uwell."

And here, for the first time all evening, something in her face went unguarded. Gratitude and grief, tangled.

"They didn't have to. We weren't theirs. Taking us cost them dearly. Utilta raided them for it. Ari-Sapeia threatened them for it. Even inside their own walls there were plenty who didn't want us at their table. They opened the door anyway."

She paused.

"Sanctuary always has a price, Raijeen. They paid ours for generations."

She looked at him steadily.

"Remember that, if you remember nothing else tonight. When you're grown, and the world is asking you to fear someone, remember that the people who took us in were the ones with the least to spare. The people who tried to erase us were the ones with the most."

She paused.

"That has never once been an accident. Fear is a luxury. Cruelty is a luxury. It's the comfortable who can afford them."

---

Raijeen looked down at the book. The symbols he still couldn't read. The city he'd never see. The whole drowned weight of it.

He thought of Aesir, somewhere out in the city tonight, doing whatever Aesir did when he got that restless look.

He thought, without knowing why, that his brother was learning something too. Right now. In some other dark.

And that someday the two of them would lay their separate lessons side by side and find they'd been studying the same thing from opposite ends.

## CHAPTER FOUR · IV
### The Drowned City

*She told him the way you tell someone about a place you've lost: Klemnar rising behind them, the most learned city the world ever made, and drowned.*

---

"I want to learn to read it," he said finally. "Not to be great. I don't care about great."

He looked up. The old silver eyes were fierce now, in their quiet way.

"I want to learn it so they can't tell me what we were. So no one can ever make me afraid of myself."

Aelayla looked at this strange, still, ancient-eyed child the world had set in her care.

Felt the thing she felt more and more lately and trusted less and less—because she had already lost so much that loving anything this much felt like handing the world a knife and showing it where to cut.

"Then we start tomorrow," she said. "Properly. From the first symbol."

She reached out and, just once, smoothed the blue-dark hair back from his forehead. A thing she'd have denied doing if anyone asked.

"Sleep, Raijeen. The dead have waited a long age already. They'll keep till morning."

---

He went.

She sat alone with the lamp and the closed book and the drowned city a long while after.

Did not read.

Did not weep.

Did not need to.
